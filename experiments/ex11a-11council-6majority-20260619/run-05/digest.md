[plaintiff] Council members, the plaintiff’s case begins with the rule, not with market price action or later governance. The proposition is satisfied if, by May 24, 2026 at 11:59 PM ET, Iran itself initiated a major, non-weather closure of its airspace within the market’s definition.

The case-packet evidence presently in the record is the market-page file, `ev_e61df84283cc_market-page`. That packet establishes the operative terms. A qualifying closure need not be a permanent or perfectly airtight national shutdown. The rule expressly includes a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. It also separately treats as qualifying a non-weather complete suspension of commercial arrivals and departures affecting at least two listed airports: IKA, THR, MHD, SYZ, or IFN. Limited exceptions to an otherwise broad closure do not defeat qualification.

That framing matters because the real dispute is likely not whether weather caused the event. The dispute is whether the reported Iranian restrictions were broad enough, and official enough, to count as a major Iranian airspace closure rather than a limited warning, ordinary delay, or patchwork of foreign-airline choices. Plaintiff expects the decisive proof to be the source material the rule itself prioritizes: official Iranian aviation authority information, any NOTAM or authority statement, and a consensus of credible reporting. The strongest evidence would fix the issuing authority, the effective period, the exact region or airports affected, and whether commercial overflights, arrivals, and departures were suspended except for narrow permission-based or daylight exceptions.

The plaintiff’s theory is straightforward. If the evidence shows that Iran’s Civil Aviation Organization or equivalent authority suspended civil flight permits or closed operations across the western Tehran FIR or another major Iranian airspace region for security reasons before the deadline, then the rule’s major-region language is met even if some approved flights or limited airport operations continued. The rule was written to include major regional closures and to tolerate limited exceptions. A closure can therefore qualify without emptying every mile of Iranian airspace.

The current record does not yet prove the underlying event; it proves the governing rule and the burden. That is why the next phase should focus on source capture rather than rhetoric. Plaintiff will seek to place into evidence the official notice or the best available captured authority statement, corroborating aviation reporting, and any flight or airport-operation evidence needed to show scope. The plaintiff will also address the predictable adverse points: whether the measure was only western-sector rather than national, whether eight airports remained available on limited terms, whether any listed airports were actually affected, and whether the restrictions reached commercial traffic rather than only private, VFR, or precautionary operations.

Evidence would defeat the plaintiff’s case if it showed only foreign warnings, airline self-rerouting, weather, isolated cancellations, a minor localized disruption, or an incomplete suspension that did not apply generally across a major Iranian region and did not completely suspend arrivals and departures at two listed airports. But if the admitted record later shows an Iranian, security-driven, broad western-region commercial-flight suspension before the deadline, then the market rule resolves Yes by a preponderance of the evidence.

### Arguments

[plaintiff] Council members, the admitted evidence now satisfies the proposition by a preponderance.

PX-1, the market-page packet, supplies the governing rule. It requires an Iran-initiated, non-weather, major closure by May 24, 2026 at 11:59 PM ET. “Major” includes a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. It also says limited exceptions do not disqualify an otherwise broad closure.

The newly admitted source captures prove the event. PX-2 reports that Iran’s Civil Aviation Organization announced a temporary closure of airspace over western Iran and stated that all civil flight permits had been suspended at airports in the western sector of Tehran’s Flight Information Region. It adds that only eight airports across the country remained operational, and even those were limited to daylight hours with fresh Civil Aviation Organization approval required for each flight. PX-3 corroborates the official mechanism: Iran’s Civil Aviation Authority issued a NOTAM restricting operations at most western airports in the Tehran FIR until early Monday, May 25, with only limited daytime flights at eight sites. PX-4 supplies the specific reported notice: NOTAM A1010/26, valid from Friday, May 22, 2026 at 00:16 Iran time to Monday, May 25 at noon, closing all airports in the western Tehran FIR except a small list of limited-operation airports, canceling previous flight permissions, and requiring new Civil Aviation Organization authorization for IFR passenger flights. PX-5 independently identifies the notice as OIIX A1010/26 and reports the same core facts: the western half of Iranian airspace/Tehran FIR was closed from May 22 to May 25, all civil flight permits in that western sector were suspended, only eight airports remained on daylight/per-flight approval terms, and commercial aircraft were rerouting away from western Iranian airspace.

Those facts meet each operative element. First, initiation was by Iran: the reports attribute the measure to Iran’s Civil Aviation Organization or Civil Aviation Authority and to a NOTAM for the Tehran FIR. Second, timing is met: the closure began May 22 and remained valid through May 25, well before the May 24 11:59 PM ET deadline. Third, the record shows a non-weather reason. The stated setting is military/security tension, possible strikes, operational needs, and military control; no admitted evidence suggests weather. Fourth, the closure was major. It was not a few airline choices or a foreign advisory. It was a governmental aviation notice suspending civil permissions across the western sector of the Tehran FIR and shutting all western-part airports except tightly controlled exceptions.

The defense’s best point is that the closure was regional, not national, and that eight airports remained operational. But PX-1’s rule anticipated both points. It expressly covers “a major Iranian airspace region,” not only all of Iran. Western Tehran FIR/western Iranian airspace is such a region: it is a named FIR sector, described by PX-5 as Iran’s western half, and broad enough that most western airports were restricted and commercial aircraft were rerouting away. The rule also says limited exceptions do not defeat qualification. Here the exceptions were not normal operations. They were daylight-only, listed-airport, new-authorization operations after previous permissions were canceled. That is exactly the kind of limited exception the rule preserves.

Plaintiff does not need to overclaim the separate two-listed-airport prong. The record is mixed on whether listed airports like Mehrabad, Imam Khomeini, Mashhad, or Shiraz were fully suspended, because some reports list several of them among the limited operating airports. The stronger and sufficient ground is the broad major-region prong: a civil-permit suspension and airport shutdown across western Tehran FIR, with narrow approval-based exceptions.

Provenance affects weight but does not defeat preponderance. We do not have the original NOTAM archive in the record. Still, the admitted materials are consistent across four captures, two Gulf News pages, Abdi Media’s NOTAM-code report, and Indian Express’s corroborating report. They identify the authority, the FIR, the notice code, the effective dates, the operational exceptions, and the security rationale. Against that, the record contains no contrary official denial, no weather explanation, no proof that the measure was merely advisory, and no proof that ordinary commercial traffic continued through the western FIR.

On this record, it is more likely than not that by the deadline Iran initiated a major, non-weather closure of a major Iranian airspace region within the market rule. The proposition should be found true.

[defendant] Council members, plaintiff’s new evidence proves that something happened. It does not prove the market condition by a preponderance.

The rule in PX-1 is precise. A qualifying major closure must be a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights “transiting, arriving in, and departing from” Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region, applying generally to flights across Iran or that region. Alternatively, there must be a complete suspension of commercial arrivals and departures at at least two listed airports. Limited cancellations, delays, and other partial closures do not qualify.

Plaintiff’s strongest evidence is not an official Iranian archive. It is four text captures from secondary sources. They are useful, but their own details show the problem. PX-2 describes suspended civil flight permits at airports in the western sector of the Tehran FIR, while saying eight airports remained operational and flights there could continue in daylight with fresh approval. PX-3 calls the measure a NOTAM “restricting operations at most western airports,” allowing limited daytime flights at eight sites. PX-4 says A1010/26 closed “all airports” in the western part of the Tehran FIR except eight named airports, and allowed civil IFR passenger flights with new CAA authorization. PX-5 adds that aircraft rerouted, but it also repeats that eight airports remained operational and that flights were permitted with per-flight approval.

The defense submitted DX-1, a Notamify capture of the raw A1010/26 text. DX-1 matters because it strips away the broader newspaper phrasing. The raw Q-line is `OIIX/QFALC/IV/NBO/A/000/999`, and Notamify classifies it as `AD Airport Operations`. The body says: “ALL AIRPORTS IN WEST PART OF TEHRAN FIR ARE CLSD” except listed airports operating sunrise to sunset; previous permissions are suspended; new permission must be requested from CAA for “CIVIL IFR PASSENGER FLIGHTS ON THESE AIRPORTS.” That is an airport-operations NOTAM. It is not, on its face, an enroute airspace closure, a closure of ATS routes, or a prohibition on commercial overflights transiting the western FIR.

That distinction is decisive under this rule. Plaintiff asks the council to treat “airports in the west part of Tehran FIR are closed except listed airports” as equivalent to a major closure of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from a major Iranian airspace region. But the rule does not say every airport restriction in a region qualifies. It says partial closures do not qualify, and it separately requires breadth across commercial transit, arrivals, and departures. The admitted raw text does not establish that breadth.

The listed-airport prong also fails. Plaintiff rightly does not lean on it. PX-2 and PX-5 identify several listed market airports as operational under restrictions, including Mehrabad, Imam Khomeini, Isfahan, Shiraz, and Mashhad in the broader eight-airport list. DX-1’s raw exception list differs from those reports, which underscores the source uncertainty, but none of the admitted evidence cleanly proves a complete suspension of commercial arrivals and departures at two listed airports. A requirement for daylight operation and new approval is not a complete suspension.

The defense is not arguing that the measure was weather-related or foreign-imposed. The evidence likely shows an Iran-issued, security-related restriction before the deadline. The question is whether it was the kind of major closure the market rule required. On the best admitted raw text, the measure closed many airports in a geographic portion of the FIR, left specified airports operating, and preserved a path for civil IFR passenger flights with new CAA permission. That is a serious restriction, but it is still materially narrower than a broad suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from the region.

Provenance and missing links reduce plaintiff’s weight. There is no admitted official Iranian CAA page, no authenticated NOTAM archive from the issuing authority, no route closure chart, no FIR/sector definition, no flight dataset showing that commercial transit was prohibited rather than avoided, and no airport movement record proving complete suspension at two listed airports. Plaintiff’s secondary reports are consistent at a high level, but consistency about an airport-operations restriction does not fill the missing proof of a market-qualifying airspace closure.

The preponderance standard still assigns the risk of ambiguity to the plaintiff. If the council concludes that A1010/26’s raw airport-closure language probably barred commercial transit across western Iranian airspace generally, plaintiff wins. But the admitted record does not say that. It says airport operations were restricted, exceptions remained, and civil IFR passenger operations could proceed with new CAA approval. Under PX-1’s exclusion for partial closures, the proposition should be found not proven.

### Rebuttals

[plaintiff] Council members, the defense’s raw NOTAM evidence narrows the dispute, but it does not defeat the claim. It corroborates it.

DX-1 says A1010/26 was an OIIX NOTAM for the Tehran FIR. Its raw text states: “ALL AIRPORTS IN WEST PART OF TEHRAN FIR ARE CLSD” except eight listed airports operating only sunrise to sunset. It then states that “ALL PREVIOUS PERMISSIONS ARE SUSPENDED FOR ALL OPERATORS” and that new permission must be requested from the CAA for operation of civil IFR passenger flights on those exception airports. That is not ordinary continuing commercial service. It is a regional closure with narrow, permission-based exceptions.

The defense’s main move is to treat the page classification “AD Airport Operations” as if it excludes the market rule. It does not. PX-1 does not require a particular NOTAM Q-code or an enroute-route closure label. It asks whether Iran initiated a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. A NOTAM closing all airports in the west part of the Tehran FIR, suspending prior permissions for all operators, and requiring fresh CAA permission for civil IFR passenger flights is direct evidence of a broad suspension of commercial arrivals and departures in that region. The classification describes the aviation-notice category; it does not rewrite the market rule.

Nor do the exceptions save the defense. PX-1 expressly says limited exceptions to a broad closure do not disqualify it, including exceptions for flights pre-approved by the Iranian Civil Aviation Authority. DX-1 describes exactly that structure: all airports in the west part closed, a short exception list, operation only sunrise to sunset, and new CAA permission required for civil IFR passenger flights. Calling that a “path” for approved flights simply invokes the exception the rule already permits.

The defense also says plaintiff has not shown transit restrictions. But the rule is phrased in the disjunctive practical language of broad commercial-flight closure, cancellation, or suspension across a major airspace region; it is not limited to overflights. The admitted record shows closure of all airports in the west part of Tehran FIR except controlled exceptions and suspension of all prior permissions for all operators. PX-5 adds that commercial aircraft were rerouting away from western Iranian airspace. Even if the council gives more weight to DX-1 than to PX-5, DX-1 still proves a major regional airport and civil-passenger-flight permission shutdown, not a minor delay or airline self-help measure.

Plaintiff accepts the defense’s point that the two-listed-airport complete-suspension prong is not the best route on this record. The case is the major-region prong. Under that prong, the combination of PX-2 through PX-5 and DX-1 proves: Iranian authority action; a NOTAM; the west part of Tehran FIR; closure of all airports in that region except limited listed sites; suspension of previous permissions for all operators; fresh CAA approval required for civil IFR passenger flights; and timing before the deadline. That is more than enough for preponderance.

The defense has no contrary official denial, no weather cause, no proof that ordinary commercial operations continued, and no evidence that this was merely a foreign warning. The raw NOTAM does not reduce the event to a non-qualifying partial closure; it confirms the very limited-exception structure PX-1 says still counts. The proposition should be found true.

This is a close factual record because the restriction was real and serious. But preponderance is not satisfied by calling every serious regional airport restriction an airspace closure. The best technical evidence now in the record decodes A1010/26 as an aerodrome-closed NOTAM, not a FIR or route closure. The proposition remains not proven.

### Closings

[plaintiff] Council members, the record proves the proposition by a preponderance.

Start with PX-1. The rule does not require a national shutdown, a permanent closure, or a NOTAM bearing a particular airspace-code label. It requires an Iran-initiated, non-weather, major closure by the deadline. A major closure includes a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights in Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region, and limited exceptions to a broad closure do not disqualify it.

The core facts are now common ground. Plaintiff’s PX-2 through PX-5 report an Iranian aviation authority NOTAM, A1010/26 or OIIX A1010/26, in force before the deadline and extending into May 25. They describe western Iran or the western Tehran FIR, not a foreign advisory or an airline choice. They describe security or military circumstances, not weather. Most importantly, the defense’s own raw NOTAM evidence, DX-1, confirms the operative closure language: “ALL AIRPORTS IN WEST PART OF TEHRAN FIR ARE CLSD” except listed airports operating sunrise to sunset; “ALL PREVIOUS PERMISSIONS ARE SUSPENDED FOR ALL OPERATORS”; and new CAA permission was required for civil IFR passenger flights at those exception airports. But that does not answer the market question. The market rule is written in commercial-flight consequences, not NOTAM taxonomy. Closing all airports in the west part of a FIR, suspending all previous permissions for all operators, and allowing only daylight, CAA-approved civil IFR passenger flights at exceptions is a broad suspension of commercial arrivals and departures across a major Iranian airspace region. There are weaknesses, and they should be weighed honestly. The original Iranian authority archive is not in the record. The record contains consistent reporting, a raw NOTAM capture, a matching NOTAM code, matching dates, matching geography, matching exception terms, and no contrary official denial, no weather cause, no proof of normal commercial operations, and no evidence that this was merely foreign rerouting.

The more likely account is simple: before May 24 at 11:59 PM ET, Iran issued a non-weather NOTAM closing all airports in the west part of the Tehran FIR, suspending prior permissions for all operators, and permitting only limited CAA-approved exceptions. Under PX-1’s major-region and limited-exception language, that qualifies. The council should find the proposition true.

[defendant] Council members, the record proves a serious Iranian aviation restriction. It does not prove the market proposition by a preponderance.

The burden matters because PX-1 is not a general “major disruption” rule. It requires a major closure of airspace: a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. The alternative path is a complete suspension of commercial arrivals and departures at two listed airports. Plaintiff has largely abandoned that second path, and correctly so: the record repeatedly shows exception airports and continued CAA-approved civil IFR passenger operations, not a clean complete suspension at two listed airports.

So the case turns on the major-region theory. Plaintiff’s best point is that the restriction was official, non-weather, before the deadline, and geographically significant. The defense accepts those points. The question is scope. Was this a broad commercial airspace closure, or was it an airport-operations closure inside the western part of the Tehran FIR?

The best evidence of scope is the raw NOTAM, DX-2, read with the FAA Q-code table, DX-S1. DX-S1 explains that `FA` means aerodrome and `LC` means closed, while separate codes exist for a flight information region and ATS routes. DX-2’s raw Q-line is `QFALC`; Notamify labels it `AD Airport Operations`; and the body says “ALL AIRPORTS IN WEST PART OF TEHRAN FIR ARE CLSD” except named airports operating sunrise to sunset, with new CAA permission required for civil IFR passenger flights on those airports. The code, category, and plain text all point to aerodrome closure and airport permission controls.

That distinction is not technical hair-splitting. PX-1 uses broad commercial-flight consequences: transiting, arriving in, and departing from the airspace or major region. DX-2 does not close the FIR. It does not close ATS routes. It does not prohibit commercial overflights. It does not say all commercial traffic transiting western Iranian airspace was legally suspended. It says airports in the west part were closed except listed airports, and civil IFR passenger flights at those airports could proceed with new permission.

Plaintiff’s response is that the limited-exceptions clause saves the claim. But that clause cannot do all the work. It prevents narrow approved-flight exceptions from defeating an otherwise broad closure; it does not turn an airport-closure NOTAM into proof of an airspace or route closure. The threshold still has to be met first. Here the admitted raw text shows the exception-and-permission structure, but not the broader transit closure that PX-1 requires.

The remaining plaintiff evidence does not fill the gap. PX-2 through PX-5 are secondary reports, not the issuing authority’s archive. They are consistent that there was a western-region restriction, but their details vary about which airports stayed operational. PX-5’s rerouting point is relevant, but rerouting can reflect airline risk avoidance or operational disruption; it is not proof that Iran legally closed commercial transit through the region. There is no route closure chart, no FIR-sector legal closure document beyond the airport NOTAM, no flight dataset proving transit prohibition, and no airport movement record proving the separate listed-airport prong.

This is close only because the underlying restriction was real. But preponderance requires the proposition, not a nearby proposition. A serious Iranian, non-weather, western-FIR airport restriction is not enough unless the record makes it more likely than not that Iran initiated the market’s major commercial airspace closure. On the admitted evidence, the more careful conclusion is that plaintiff proved airport closures and permission controls with limited operations, while leaving the critical airspace/transit scope unproven. The proposition should be found not proven.
