### Openings

[plaintiff] Council members, the plaintiff’s case is that this market turns on a concrete aviation event, not on market governance: by the May 24, 2026 deadline, Iran itself had initiated a non-weather restriction broad enough to count as a “major closure” under the rule.

The admitted case packet is limited but important. The packet’s market rule says “Yes” if Iran initiated a major closure of its airspace, not solely because of weather, by 11:59 PM ET on the listed date. It defines a qualifying major closure as a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, or departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. It also gives an alternate airport-based path for a complete non-weather suspension of commercial arrivals and departures affecting at least two named airports. The rule excludes mere warnings, no-fly zones, or restrictions imposed by airlines or foreign countries, and it identifies the key sources as official Iranian aviation information and a consensus of credible reporting. That rule text is visible in evidence item ev_e61df84283cc_market-page.

That framing matters because the defense will likely try to make this about two narrower points: first, that a restriction with exceptions is not “major”; second, that third-party warnings or airline reroutings do not count. Plaintiff accepts the second point. We do not need foreign warnings. The proof should focus on whether Iran’s own civil aviation authority or air navigation system issued a binding closure or suspension covering a major region of Iranian airspace, and whether commercial IFR passenger operations were broadly stopped or conditioned on new Iranian permission.

The present record proves the governing rule and the deadline. It does not yet, by itself, prove the underlying May 2026 closure event. That gap is why the decisive next evidence should be the source material behind the aviation action: the raw Tehran FIR NOTAM or equivalent official Iranian aviation notice; any official Civil Aviation Organization or airport-company statement confirming scope, timing, and exceptions; and credible contemporaneous aviation or news reporting that corroborates the same action. If those sources show that Iran closed the western part of the Tehran FIR or suspended prior civil flight permissions through May 25, with only limited approved exceptions, that satisfies the rule’s broad “major Iranian airspace region” prong even if a handful of airports or pre-approved flights could operate.

The expected adverse issues are also clear. The council should ask whether the source chain is authentic, whether the notice was actually issued or accepted as an Iranian NOTAM, whether the closure was effective by the market deadline, whether it was non-weather, and whether exceptions were so broad that the action became only a limited delay rather than a major closure. Plaintiff’s position is that exceptions for specific flights or daylight operations do not defeat a broad state-initiated closure; the packet rule itself says limited exceptions to a broad closure do not disqualify it.

So the case will be decided by provenance, scope, and timing. If the admitted source record shows an Iran-issued aviation notice in force before 11:59 PM ET on May 24, covering western Tehran FIR or another major Iranian airspace region and suspending ordinary commercial operations for non-weather security reasons, the proposition is more likely than not true. If instead the record ultimately shows only foreign warnings, voluntary airline reroutes, weather restrictions, or an unverified social-media description with no official or credible source chain, plaintiff should not prevail. Plaintiff expects the former, and will seek admission of the source materials in the next evidentiary phase.

[defendant] Council members, the defense opening begins with the one thing the present record actually proves: the rule. The admitted market-page packet, ev_e61df84283cc_market-page, sets a demanding factual condition. Plaintiff must show, by a preponderance, that Iran itself initiated a non-weather “major closure” by May 24, 2026, 11:59 PM ET.

That packet does not prove the underlying May 2026 event. It proves the standard the event must meet. A qualifying closure must be broad: a closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region, generally applicable across Iran or that major region. The packet also creates a separate airport path, but only for a complete non-weather suspension of commercial arrivals and departures affecting at least two listed airports: IKA, THR, MHD, SYZ, or IFN. Limited cancellations, delays, partial closures, foreign warnings, airline reroutes, or restrictions imposed by countries other than Iran do not satisfy the rule.

That distinction is the defense case. The plaintiff will likely point the council toward a reported aviation notice, perhaps described in later phases as affecting the western part of the Tehran FIR, cancelling some permissions, limiting some airports, or requiring fresh Iranian approval for IFR passenger operations. Even if such material is eventually admitted, the council must ask what it actually says. An airport-operation restriction is not automatically an airspace closure. A permission requirement is not automatically a general suspension. A daylight or airport-specific regime is not automatically a broad closure of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from a major Iranian airspace region. And if the listed major airports continued commercial arrivals and departures, the separate airport prong fails.

The packet’s “limited exceptions” clause does not erase those limits. It prevents a true broad closure from failing merely because a few flights are permitted. It does not convert every partial restriction, regional airport notice, or security advisory into a market-resolving major closure. Plaintiff must first establish the broad closure; only then do exceptions become secondary.

Source quality will matter as much as wording. The packet identifies official Iranian aviation information and a consensus of credible reporting as the primary resolution sources. Social-media descriptions, search snippets, reposted graphics, foreign government warnings, and flight-tracking impressions may be leads, but they are not enough unless tied back to an authentic Iranian aviation notice or reliable contemporaneous reporting that fixes scope, timing, and cause. The council should also watch for conflicts: whether the alleged notice was new or merely an extension, whether it closed airspace or only airports, whether overflights were barred, whether commercial service at the named airports actually stopped, and whether the effective time was before the market deadline.

So the defense does not need to prove there were no disruptions in Iran. The defense position is narrower and stronger: disruptions are not the proposition. Plaintiff must prove a rule-qualifying, Iran-initiated, non-weather major closure. On the current admitted record, that proof is absent. In the evidentiary phases, the decisive materials should be the raw official NOTAM or equivalent Iranian aviation notice, authenticated source-chain evidence, credible reporting that corroborates the same exact scope, and operational evidence for the listed airports. If those materials show only partial airport restrictions, approval requirements, foreign rerouting, or an ambiguous source chain, the proposition has not been established.

### Arguments

[plaintiff] Council members, plaintiff now has the evidence the opening identified. The proposition is true by a preponderance.

Start with the rule. The market packet, PX-1, requires an Iran-initiated, non-weather “major closure” by May 24, 2026, 11:59 PM ET. It defines that term to include a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, or departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. It also states that limited exceptions to a broad closure do not disqualify it.

PX-2 is the core source. It captures NOTAM A1010/26 for OIIX/Tehran FIR. The raw NOTAM states: “A1010/26 NOTAMR A0799/26”; “A)OIIX B)2605222046 C)2605250830EST”; and, in substance, that all airports in the west part of Tehran FIR are closed except OITR, OIKK, OIAA, OISS, OIYY, OICC, OIGG, and OIAW, which are operational only sunrise to sunset; all previous permissions are suspended for all operators; and new CAA permission is required for civil IFR passenger flights on those airports.

That satisfies each element. Iran initiated it: the NOTAM is for OIIX/Tehran FIR and requires new permission from the CAA. It was timely: it began May 22 at 20:46 UTC and remained effective until May 25 at 08:30 UTC, covering the May 24 11:59 PM ET deadline. It was not weather: the NOTAM text contains no weather basis, and the corroborating reports place the action in a security and military-tension context. It was major: it covered the west part of the Tehran FIR, a major Iranian airspace region, closing all airports in that region except a short list operating only by daylight and only under renewed CAA permission.

PX-3 and PX-4 corroborate the source chain. PX-3 reports that Iran temporarily closed the airspace over its western half from May 22 to May 25, suspended civil flight permits in the western sector of the Tehran FIR, and that the Iranian Civil Aviation Authority issued NOTAM OIIX A1010/26. It further reports only eight airports remained operational, daylight-only, with fresh per-flight CAA approval required. PX-4 likewise reports that Iran’s CAA issued a NOTAM temporarily closing all airports in the western part of OIIX, except limited daylight operations, that the restrictions ran May 22 through May 25, and that prior permissions were suspended with fresh CAA approval required for civil IFR passenger flights. PX-5 adds aviation-context weight: SafeAirspace later described the western part of OIIX/Tehran FIR as remaining closed while eastern OIIX partly reopened, showing that western OIIX was treated in aviation risk reporting as a significant regional closure, not a trivial airport delay.

The defense says this is “airport operations,” not airspace. The rule is not so narrow. It expressly covers a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, or departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. Closing all airports in the west part of Tehran FIR, suspending all previous permissions, and requiring new CAA permission for civil IFR passenger flights is a broad suspension of commercial arrival/departure operations in that major region. Even if some daylight operations at named airports could proceed, those are exactly the limited exceptions the rule says do not defeat a broad closure.

Plaintiff is not relying on foreign warnings or voluntary airline reroutes. Nor does plaintiff need to prove the alternate listed-airport prong. The strongest path is the major-region prong: west Tehran FIR/OIIX. The admitted source record shows a state aviation restriction, in force by the deadline, with broad regional scope and only constrained exceptions.

Source weight favors plaintiff. PX-2 is a third-party NOTAM database capture rather than an official Iranian web page, so it is not perfect provenance. But it reproduces raw NOTAM fields and text, including the NOTAM number, FIR, times, and operational terms. PX-3 and PX-4 independently match the same authority, number, dates, region, exceptions, and CAA approval requirement. No admitted evidence contradicts that chain. On preponderance, the combined record is enough: by the deadline, Iran had initiated a major non-weather closure within the market rule.

[defendant] Council members, the plaintiff has now proved disruption, timing, and likely non-weather security context. But the proposition is narrower: a rule-qualifying “major closure” of Iranian airspace by the deadline. On that decisive element, plaintiff’s own raw NOTAM exhibit defeats the claim.

DX-1, the market packet, requires a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights “transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region.” It says limited cancellations, delays, and partial closures do not qualify. It also provides a separate airport route only for a complete suspension of commercial arrivals and departures affecting at least two listed airports.

PX-2 is the alleged core source. Read carefully, it is not an airspace-closure NOTAM. It is labeled “AD Airport Operations.” Its raw Q line is “Q)OIIX/QFALC/IV/NBO/A/000/999/.” DX-2, the FAA international NOTAM Q-code table, explains why that matters: in a five-letter Q code, the second and third letters identify the subject and the fourth and fifth identify status. “FA” means aerodrome; “AF” means flight information region; “LC” means closed. So QFALC decodes to an aerodrome closure, not a flight-information-region closure.

The raw text matches that code. PX-2 says: “ALL AIRPORTS IN WEST PART OF TEHRAN FIR ARE CLSD” except eight named airports, which are operational from sunrise to sunset. It then says prior permissions are suspended and new CAA permission is required for civil IFR passenger flights “ON THESE AIRPORTS.” It does not say western Tehran FIR airspace is closed to all flights. It does not say commercial overflights are barred. It does not say flights transiting the region are suspended. It says airports.

DX-3 shows what a true Tehran FIR airspace closure looks like in the same Notamify format and under the market packet’s own January qualifying example. The January NOTAM is labeled “ASP Airspace Restrictions,” has QAFLC, and says “TEHRAN FIR CLSD TO ALL FLIGHTS” except approved international civil arrivals and departures. That is the contrast the council needs: AF/FIR plus text closing the FIR to all flights versus FA/aerodrome plus text closing airports with listed operating exceptions.

Plaintiff’s secondary reports do not cure the mismatch. PX-3 opens with broad “airspace” wording, but its concrete NOTAM description says the shutdown was of “all airports in the western part of the Tehran FIR,” and it attributes the NOTAM fact to Gulf News rather than preserving the official source. PX-4 is even closer to the raw text: temporary closure of all airports in the western part, except a limited number operating during daylight. PX-5 is later June risk context; it is not the May 22 raw NOTAM and cannot expand A1010/26.

The alternate airport prong also fails on plaintiff’s evidence. The market requires complete suspension of commercial arrivals and departures affecting at least two listed airports: IKA, THR, MHD, SYZ, or IFN. PX-3 says Mehrabad, Imam Khomeini, Isfahan, Shiraz, and Mashhad remained operational, with daylight and fresh-approval restrictions. That may be burdensome, but it is not a complete suspension at two listed airports.

The limited-exception clause does not rescue plaintiff. It preserves a broad closure that has narrow carveouts. It does not transform a partial, airport-coded regime into a broad airspace closure. Otherwise the rule’s exclusion for partial closures would have no work to do.

Source weight reinforces this. Plaintiff has no official Iranian page, no capture of referenced A0796/26, and no A0799/26 source chain beyond the third-party Notamify page. The best record source is still PX-2, and PX-2 is an airport-operations notice. On preponderance, plaintiff has shown Iranian airport restrictions in western Tehran FIR, not the market-defined major closure of Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. The proposition should be found not established.

### Rebuttals

[plaintiff] Council members, defendant’s Q-code point is useful but not dispositive.

Plaintiff accepts what DX-1 shows: in the NOTAM Q-code table, FA means aerodrome, AF means flight information region, and LC means closed. So PX-2 is an aerodrome-closure notice, while DX-2 is an example of a full FIR airspace-closure notice. But the market rule does not say only an AF-coded “FIR closed to all flights” NOTAM can qualify.

The rule is broader. PX-1 defines a major closure as a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights “transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian Airspace region.” The phrase includes arriving and departing; it is not limited to overflights. A measure that closes all airports in the west part of Tehran FIR, suspends all previous permissions for all operators, and requires new CAA permission for civil IFR passenger flights is a broad suspension of commercial arrivals and departures in that major region.

Defendant’s reading would make the rule narrower than its text. If only a NOTAM coded AF and closing the entire FIR to all flights could qualify, the rule would not separately mention cancellations or suspensions of commercial flights arriving in or departing from a major Iranian airspace region. It also would not need to say that limited exceptions to a broad closure do not disqualify the closure, including exceptions for CAA-pre-approved flights.

That exception language fits PX-2 closely. PX-2 does not show normal operations. It shows all west-Tehran-FIR airports closed except a short list, those listed airports limited to sunrise-to-sunset operation, all previous permissions suspended for all operators, and new CAA permission required for civil IFR passenger flights. Those are not mere delays or voluntary airline choices. They are Iran-imposed restrictions that broadly suspend ordinary commercial flight operations in a major region while allowing limited approved exceptions.

DX-2 proves only that January’s event was even broader. It does not prove May’s event was too narrow. The packet’s rule gives qualifying examples, but the controlling definition reaches broad regional suspensions, not only total FIR closures. The packet also recognizes a western-airspace closure as a prior qualifying example, confirming that a regional event can count.

Defendant’s strongest point is that PX-2 is airport-coded and does not expressly bar overflights. That matters to weight, and plaintiff has not relied on the alternate complete-suspension-at-two-listed-airports prong. But preponderance does not require the most extreme closure imaginable. The admitted record shows an Iran CAA-linked NOTAM in force through the deadline, closing all airports in the west part of OIIX except limited daylight operations and suspending all prior permissions for civil IFR passenger flights. Under the rule’s major-region and limited-exception language, that is a major non-weather closure.

The remaining question is whether the market rule nevertheless treats this airport notice as a broad airspace closure. It should not.

The defense is not arguing that only a whole-country or whole-FIR AF-coded NOTAM can ever qualify. A different record might show a regional closure by other language. But this record does not. ### Closings

[plaintiff] Council members, the plaintiff has carried the preponderance burden.

The market rule is the anchor. It does not require the most extreme possible closure, nor does it require only a whole-country or whole-FIR overflight ban. PX-1 defines a major closure as a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. It also says limited exceptions to a broad closure do not disqualify the closure, including CAA-preapproved flights.

The admitted record shows that by the deadline Iran had done exactly what matters under that rule. PX-2 preserves NOTAM A1010/26 for OIIX. Its time window runs from May 22, 2026 at 20:46 UTC to May 25 at 08:30 UTC, so it was in force at May 24, 11:59 PM ET. Its operative text closes all airports in the west part of Tehran FIR except eight listed airports operating only sunrise to sunset, suspends all previous permissions for all operators, and requires new CAA permission for civil IFR passenger flights. PX-3 and PX-4 independently corroborate CAA issuance, May 22-May 25 timing, western Tehran FIR scope, limited daylight operations, and fresh approval requirements. They also place the action in a security and military-tension context, not weather. PX-5 supports that western OIIX was treated in aviation reporting as a significant regional closure.

Defendant’s best point is real: PX-2 is coded QFALC, an aerodrome-closure notice, while the January comparator is QAFLC, a full FIR closure notice. Plaintiff has not pretended otherwise. But the code distinction does not end the case because the rule’s first prong is broader than “AF-coded FIR closed to all flights.” It covers broad suspensions of commercial flights arriving in and departing from a major Iranian airspace region. A state aviation notice closing all west-Tehran-FIR airports except limited daylight operations, canceling prior permissions, and requiring fresh CAA approval for civil IFR passenger flights is a broad regional suspension of ordinary commercial arrivals and departures.

Nor do the exceptions defeat the claim. The packet expressly anticipates that a qualifying broad closure may allow limited CAA-approved exceptions. The May NOTAM’s exceptions were not normal operations: they were daylight-only and permission-only after all prior permissions were suspended. That is the difference between a mere delay and a major state-imposed closure regime.

Plaintiff does not need the alternate listed-airport complete-suspension prong. Defendant is right that some listed airports remained operational under restrictions. The stronger and sufficient basis is the major-region prong: western Tehran FIR/OIIX.

The source chain is not perfect; the raw NOTAM was preserved from Notamify rather than an official Iranian page. But preponderance does not demand perfect provenance when the raw NOTAM fields are reproduced and two independent reports match the authority, number, dates, scope, exceptions, and permission requirement. There is no admitted contrary source showing this NOTAM was false, weather-based, foreign-imposed, or outside the deadline.

The proposition should therefore be found true: by May 24, 2026 at 11:59 PM ET, Iran initiated a major non-weather closure within the market rule.

[defendant] Council members, the plaintiff proved a serious Iranian aviation restriction. It did not prove the proposition as written.

The proposition is not “did Iran restrict airports in western Iran?” It is whether Iran initiated a major non-weather closure of its airspace within the market rule. The packet defines that carefully: a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. It excludes limited cancellations, delays, and partial closures. It separately allows an airport route only for complete suspension of commercial arrivals and departures at at least two listed airports.

The best evidence is the raw NOTAM text. A1010/26 is labeled “AD Airport Operations.” Its Q-code is QFALC. The FAA table decodes FA as aerodrome and LC as closed. Its operative text says all airports in the west part of Tehran FIR are closed except eight named airports, operational sunrise to sunset, with fresh CAA permission required for civil IFR passenger flights on these airports. That is not the language of an airspace closure. It does not say western Tehran FIR is closed to all flights. It does not say commercial overflights or transiting flights are barred. It says airports.

The January comparator shows the difference. The market packet itself treats January 2026 as a qualifying example. In the same Notamify format, that notice is labeled “Airspace Restrictions,” uses QAFLC, and says “TEHRAN FIR CLSD TO ALL FLIGHTS” except approved international civil arrivals and departures. That is what a broad FIR closure looks like. A1010/26 is materially different.

Plaintiff’s strongest answer is that the rule includes arriving and departing, and that limited exceptions do not defeat a broad closure. But that assumes the conclusion. The exception clause saves a broad closure with narrow carveouts; it does not turn an airport-coded partial operating regime into a broad closure. And the rule says “transiting, arriving in, and departing from.” Plaintiff’s proof falls short on transiting and overflights, then asks the council to treat airport restrictions as enough.

The alternate airport prong does not help plaintiff either. Plaintiff expressly avoids it, for good reason: its own reporting says Imam Khomeini, Mehrabad, Mashhad, Shiraz, and Isfahan remained operational, albeit under daylight and fresh-permission limits. Restricted operation is not complete suspension.

Finally, source weight matters. There is no official Iranian page in the record, no captured A0796/26, and no independent A0799/26 chain. The secondary reports corroborate restrictions, but their concrete descriptions keep returning to airports. They cannot broaden the raw notice into something it does not say.

On preponderance, the record establishes timely, non-weather, Iran-imposed airport restrictions in western Tehran FIR. It does not establish the market-defined major closure of Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. The proposition should be found not established.
