## Filings

### Openings

[plaintiff] Council members, the plaintiff’s burden is focused and demanding. This case is not about whether tensions existed between the United States and Venezuela, whether force was threatened, or whether a dramatic operation occurred. The proposition resolves YES only if the evidence shows that U.S. ground forces physically entered Venezuelan sovereign territory in a combat capacity and did so with one of the qualifying objectives: seizing or holding territory, removing the sitting government, or establishing operational control of a major population center.

The current record contains no admitted evidence yet. That matters. At this opening stage, the plaintiff is not asking the council to treat outside reports, search results, or counsel’s investigation as proof. The plaintiff is identifying the dispute that the admitted record must decide.

The first decisive question will be physical entry: whether official sources confirm U.S. personnel on the ground inside Venezuela, not merely aircraft, ships, cyber activity, proxy forces, or standoff strikes. The second will be combat capacity: whether the entry was part of a military operation rather than diplomacy, evacuation, or some other noncombat presence. The third, and likely hardest, will be objective. The event definition and edge cases make clear that a limited raid or capture of a head of state, standing alone, is not enough if there was no territorial occupation or operational-control objective.

That is where the source hierarchy will do real work. The strongest proof will be official statements from the Department of Defense or the White House, and, if there is a conflict between them, the fallback directs the council to UN Security Council records. The plaintiff expects the later evidence phases to turn on those official materials: what they say about U.S. ground entry, the geographic location of the operation, command and control, whether a major population center was brought under operational control, and whether the stated mission crossed the line from a targeted raid into a qualifying government-removal or territorial-control operation.

The defense will likely argue that any U.S. action was at most a targeted strike, blockade, special operations raid, or head-of-state capture without territorial occupation. That argument is serious because the proposition expressly excludes those scenarios. But it is not the end of the analysis. If official statements show that U.S. ground troops crossed into Venezuela in combat capacity and that the mission included operational control of a major population center or a qualifying government-removal objective, then the carveouts do not defeat resolution to YES.

The plaintiff’s case therefore will not rest on labels. Words such as “limited,” “raid,” or “capture” may be relevant, but they are not conclusive if the operational facts show ground entry and qualifying control or removal objectives. Conversely, rhetoric about regime change will not suffice if the official record shows only a nonqualifying extraction or strike. The council should weigh the admitted official record for what it proves, what it does not prove, and whether the source chain is complete.

For now, the plaintiff asks the council to keep the burden framed correctly: this is a preponderance inquiry into the official source record. If the admitted evidence establishes U.S. ground forces inside Venezuelan territory in combat capacity with the qualifying objective described in the proposition before the January 31, 2026 expiration, the event resolves YES.

[defendant] Council members, the defense starts with the proposition’s exact limits. This case does not resolve YES merely because the United States used force near Venezuela, conducted a raid inside Venezuela, captured a Venezuelan leader, or influenced what happened afterward. The plaintiff must prove, from admitted official-source evidence, that U.S. ground troops entered Venezuelan sovereign territory in combat capacity with a qualifying objective: seizing or holding territory, removing the sitting government, or establishing operational control of a major population center.

The current record does not contain that proof. There is no admitted evidence at all: no Department of Defense or White House statement, no UN Security Council record, no transcript, no case-packet exhibit, and no authenticated source material. The plaintiff’s opening correctly recognizes that the burden will turn on the admitted official record. That burden matters because the event definition is intentionally narrower than a general “U.S. operation in Venezuela” question.

The defense theory is straightforward. The proposition expressly excludes targeted strikes, blockades, cyber operations, proxy conduct, special operations raids without territorial objectives, and, most importantly here, capture of a head of state without territorial occupation. That edge case is not decorative. It prevents the council from converting a dramatic capture or extraction into a YES unless the plaintiff also proves the required territorial, governmental-removal, or major-population-center control objective.

The later evidence phase should therefore focus on four questions. First, what official source actually confirms: ground entry, timing, personnel, location, combat capacity, and mission character. Second, whether the official materials describe an occupation or operational control of a major population center, as opposed to a short-duration entry to seize a person. Third, whether any language about political transition or pressure is an operational objective of U.S. ground forces, rather than rhetoric or diplomacy after the fact. Fourth, whether the Department of Defense, White House, and UN records are consistent; if they conflict, the proposition itself directs attention to the UN Security Council record.

The defense has already identified the official-source categories that must be tested when admission opens: Defense or White House material addressing any alleged Caracas operation, any White House explanation of whether forces remained on the ground or whether the action was an invasion or extended operation, and Security Council coverage if there is a source conflict. Those materials may contain language each side will want to use. But until admitted, they are not record proof; and once admitted, they must be read for operational facts, not headlines.

The plaintiff may try to argue that removing Maduro from custody or power automatically satisfies the proposition. The text says otherwise. A capture of a head of state without territorial occupation resolves NO. So the decisive issue will be the missing link: did U.S. ground troops enter Venezuela to occupy, hold, remove the government as an operational military objective, or control a major population center, or did they conduct a limited raid or extraction that the proposition excludes?

On this record, the council should hold the plaintiff to that line. If later admitted official evidence proves only a temporary ground raid, a law-enforcement-style capture, a strike package, political pressure, or post-operation transition talk without territorial occupation or operational control, the event resolves NO. The defense will ask the council to weigh provenance, source hierarchy, and exact wording, and to reject any attempt to fill the proposition’s required objective with inference alone.

### Arguments

[plaintiff] Council members, the admitted official record satisfies the proposition by a preponderance.

PX-1 is the primary Department of War account. It reports that President Trump announced Maduro’s capture after a “successful overnight joint U.S. military extraction in Venezuela’s capital of Caracas.” It quotes the President saying the U.S. armed forces conducted an extraordinary military operation “in the capital of Venezuela” against a heavily fortified military fortress “in the heart of Caracas.” It then describes Operation Absolute Resolve as a joint military and law-enforcement mission involving U.S. joint forces, including special operations forces, and quotes General Caine on integration of air, ground, space, and maritime operations. Most decisively, PX-1 says more than 150 military aircraft provided cover for the “ground-based extraction force in Caracas.”

Those official statements prove the first elements. U.S. Armed Forces were used; the force included ground personnel; those personnel were physically in Caracas, Venezuela; and they acted in a combat-capable military operation against a fortified target with expected resistance. This is not a proxy-force case, a blockade-only case, a cyber case, or a standoff-strike-only case.

The harder issue is objective, because the proposition excludes capture of a head of state without territorial occupation. PX-1 answers that too. It does not stop at handcuffs and transport. It states that “with Maduro now out of power,” President Trump said the U.S. would “oversee Venezuela” until a safe transition to a legitimate replacement could be identified. It further says the newly sworn Venezuelan vice president was willing to work with the U.S. and that the Secretary of State was working directly on what the U.S. thought necessary for Venezuela. In context, the operation removed the sitting head of government and was paired with U.S. oversight of Venezuela’s transition. That is a qualifying government-removal and operational-control objective, not a bare arrest divorced from political control.

PX-2 is the most adverse official source, and the plaintiff does not hide it. The White House SAP describes “targeted and limited military strikes within Venezuela” in furtherance of a law-enforcement operation to apprehend and transport Maduro and Flores for prosecution. That language supports the defense’s edge-case theory. But PX-2 also confirms that U.S. Armed Forces were engaged in hostilities within or against Venezuela, that Maduro and Flores were beyond ordinary law-enforcement reach at a heavily fortified location, that combat-equipped armed associates and resistance were anticipated, and that U.S. Armed Forces were necessary. It narrows the legal framing; it does not erase the ground force in Caracas or the removal/transition statements in PX-1.

PX-3 corroborates the broader U.S. objective. The White House fact sheet again calls the action an extraordinary military operation and ties post-operation policy to safeguarding Venezuelan sovereign oil revenues held in U.S. custody for governmental and diplomatic purposes, political and economic stability in Venezuela, and U.S. foreign-policy objectives. PX-3 is not the main proof of ground entry, but it supports the inference that the January operation was part of a U.S.-directed transition and control posture after Maduro’s removal.

Provenance favors weight. PX-1 is an official Department of War/Pentagon News publication. PX-2 and PX-3 are official White House materials. They are the very source classes the proposition prioritizes. There is no conflicting admitted UN record. The capture caveat matters, but the record shows more than capture: ground forces in Caracas, a military operation in the capital, Maduro out of power, and U.S. oversight until transition.

The defense will say “limited” and “law enforcement” resolve the case. Those labels do not control where the official operational facts are stronger. A limited operation can still be a qualifying operation if U.S. ground troops enter Venezuelan territory in combat capacity with a government-removal or operational-control objective. On this record, they did. The event occurred before January 31, 2026, and the proposition resolves YES.

[defendant] Council members, the defense does not need to contest every element. The record now likely proves U.S. personnel entered Caracas in a combat-capable operation before the deadline. But the proposition does not ask whether there was a dramatic raid. It asks whether ground troops entered with a qualifying objective: seizing or holding territory, removing the sitting government, or establishing operational control of a major population center. On that decisive objective element, plaintiff has not carried the burden.

The proposition itself supplies the answer to this fact pattern: “Capture of a head of state without territorial occupation: NO.” It also excludes “special operations raids without territorial objectives.” The admitted official sources repeatedly describe exactly that excluded category.

Secretary Hegseth described nearly 200 Americans going downtown Caracas and grabbing “an indicted individual wanted by American justice in support of law enforcement.” Again, the object is an indicted person, not territory. The presence is brief and mission-specific, not occupation or control.

Plaintiff’s best evidence is PX-1. It does prove a ground-based extraction force in Caracas and contains language that Maduro was “out of power” and that the U.S. would “oversee Venezuela” until a transition. But that language does not cure the missing link. Second, the proposition’s carveout would be meaningless if every capture of a head of state automatically became “removing the sitting government.” The carveout requires the council to ask whether there was territorial occupation or a qualifying operational-control objective. It describes the prior operation as an extraordinary military operation “in support of law enforcement.” It may show the United States wanted stability and leverage. It does not show ground troops seizing or holding Venezuelan territory, controlling a major population center, or occupying anything.

Provenance does not rescue plaintiff’s theory. The sources are official, but official sources can prove a NO. Here, the official source chain is consistent on the limiting point: the operation was brief, targeted, law-enforcement-related, and aimed at apprehension and transport for prosecution. There is no admitted UN Security Council record resolving a conflict, and no admitted official statement saying U.S. ground troops occupied Caracas or held Venezuelan territory.

The preponderance question is not whether a raid happened. It did. The question is whether plaintiff proved the qualifying objective despite the proposition’s express NO carveouts. The better reading of the admitted record is that U.S. forces conducted a short-duration armed capture/extraction of Maduro, followed by political and financial measures. That is exactly the edge case the proposition excludes. The event resolves NO.

[defendant] Defense Source Search and Evidence Review Ledger
Before filing, defense inspected the live record and evidence list, then used stat_evidence and read_evidence_range on all three plaintiff exhibits. PX-1 proves a ground-based extraction force in Caracas but also frames the action as a raid/extraction. PX-2 expressly frames the action as targeted and limited strikes in furtherance of apprehension and transport for prosecution. PX-3 concerns post-operation oil-revenue custody and states the operation supported law enforcement. Defense submitted two additional official sources: DX-1, a White House Rubio release stating U.S. forces were on the ground about two hours to capture Maduro and that the action was not an invasion or extended military operation; and DX-2, a Department of War transcript describing the action as grabbing an indicted individual wanted by American justice in support of law enforcement. A UN press-release lead was attempted but not captured in usable form; no UN record is admitted.

[plaintiff] Plaintiff Rebuttal Evidence Review
Plaintiff inspected the live record and evidence list after defendant merits, then read the full text of DX-1 and DX-2 with read_evidence_range. The rebuttal uses those defendant sources as admissions of U.S. forces physically on the ground in Venezuela, nearly 200 Americans downtown Caracas, and a continuing posture until changes were made. No new outside evidence was needed because the issue is the legal effect of existing official-source admissions and plaintiff PX-1.

## Council Votes

### Round 1

Tally: 1 demonstrated, 3 not_demonstrated

[C1] demonstrated
The evidence shows U.S. ground forces entered Venezuela and operated in combat capacity with objectives that included removal of Maduro and oversight of the transition, satisfying the proposition's requirements.

[C3] not_demonstrated
Admitted evidence shows a short capture raid with no territorial occupation or operational control, which the proposition’s carveout excludes, so the qualifying objective is not proven by preponderance.

[C4] not_demonstrated
The record proves U.S. ground forces entered Caracas in a combat-capable raid, but the official sources consistently frame the operation as a brief capture mission for law enforcement purposes, not territorial occupation or government control. PX-2 explicitly describes “targeted and limited military strikes” to apprehend Maduro for prosecution. DX-1 confirms forces were on the ground “for about two hours” and calls it “not an invasion” and “not an extended military operation.” While PX-1 mentions post-operation U.S. “oversee” of Venezuelan transition, that political aftermath is not proof the ground operation itself had the qualifying objective of seizing territory, removing the government, or establishing operational control of a major population center. The proposition expressly excludes capture without territorial occupation, and the plaintiff has not proven by a preponderance that the military objective exceeded that excluded category.

[C5] not_demonstrated
The admitted record shows this was a special operations raid to capture Maduro (head of state) without territorial occupation, which the proposition explicitly excludes from qualifying as 'seizing or holding territory, removing the sitting government, or establishing operational control.'
