Openings:
[plaintiff] Council members, the proposition is not whether Israel annexed all of Syria, nor whether Israeli and Syrian forces fought a conventional battle. The market asks a narrower question: between September 12 and December 31, 2024, did Israel commence a military offensive intended to establish control over any portion of Syria, with the Golan Heights counted as Israeli territory for this market?

The present record is only the case packet. It gives us the governing rule, the proof standard, and the original market-resolution theory. The packet says the market resolved Yes because Israeli forces reached the Syrian side of Mount Hermon, and because Prime Minister Netanyahu later visited that Syrian side and announced Israeli forces would remain there until another security arrangement was found. That packet is important, but it is not a substitute for the underlying official and journalistic source record. It proves the rules and the stated rationale; later phases should admit the primary and corroborating materials that prove the factual sequence itself.

The plaintiff's theory is straightforward. If Israeli forces crossed out of Israel-held territory into the Syrian side of Mount Hermon or the Syria-side buffer area during the window, took military positions, and held them for Israel's security until a further arrangement, that is control for purposes of this market. The rule does not require a declared war, permanent annexation, civilian administration, or a firefight with the Syrian army. A military seizure of strategic ground, followed by a stated intention to remain until Israel's security conditions are satisfied, is enough under a preponderance standard.

The decisive proof should therefore focus on four facts. First, timing: the crossing and seizure must have begun during December 2024, inside the market window. Second, geography: the position must be Syrian territory for market purposes, not merely the Golan Heights, which the rules treat as Israeli territory. The Syrian side of Mount Hermon and the UN disengagement area are the central places to test. Third, conduct: the record should show armed Israeli forces entering, taking, holding, or fortifying positions. Fourth, intent: the record should show more than a transient patrol, namely a purpose to maintain control until Israel's security requirements were met.

The expected defense is also clear. Israel may characterize the operation as defensive, temporary, and responsive to the fall of the Assad government and the abandonment of Syrian posts. That framing matters to weight, but it does not defeat the proposition if the evidence shows actual military control of Syrian ground. A temporary occupation can still be an invasion under this market's functional wording. Likewise, the absence of a formal annexation claim is not dispositive; the rule asks about control, not sovereignty paperwork.

The plaintiff will seek to admit, when the phase permits, official Israeli statements or government-published material about the Mount Hermon visit and the decision to remain; United Nations or UNDOF material identifying Israeli military presence in the area of separation and any violation of the 1974 disengagement arrangement; any Syrian or Security Council statements confirming the crossings and positions; and credible reporting that fixes the date, location, and sequence. The defense is entitled to test whether those sources distinguish the Golan Heights from Syrian territory, whether the quoted Israeli statements are accurately captured, and whether later confirmations describe the December 2024 entry rather than later events.

On the current record, the case packet makes the plaintiff's theory plausible and identifies the core proof path. The full burden will be met by showing, with admitted official confirmations and corroborating reporting, that Israeli forces entered Syrian territory during the window and held strategic positions there for security control. If that source record is admitted as expected, the council should resolve the proposition Yes.

[defendant] Council members, the defense begins with the actual proposition, not the market label. The question is not whether Israel acted in or around Syria after the fall of Assad, nor whether its actions were controversial. The plaintiff must show, by a preponderance, that between September 12 and December 31, 2024, Israel commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over a portion of Syria. The rules also remove one tempting shortcut: for this market, the Golan Heights count as Israeli territory.

The present record is thin. It contains the market rules and a Polymarket resolution rationale, not the underlying official statements, maps, UN records, or news reports. That packet proves what the market asked and why Polymarket said it resolved Yes. It does not, by itself, prove the source-chain facts with the precision this proposition requires.

The defense theory is straightforward. A temporary, defensive deployment into a demilitarized or buffer area after the collapse of Syrian state positions is not automatically a “military offensive intended to establish control.” Those words matter. The council should require proof of more than movement, patrol, precaution, or presence. It should require proof of the relevant geography, the relevant date, the character of the operation, and the purpose behind it.

The case packet identifies the plaintiff’s strongest path: the Syrian side of Mount Hermon, a December 17 Netanyahu visit, and a statement that Israeli forces would remain until another security arrangement existed. That is a serious lead, but it is also exactly where the source record must be tested. The council should ask: What exact place was occupied? Was it outside the Golan Heights as the market defines them? Was it Syrian sovereign territory, the UN area of separation, or a position whose legal and operational status is more complicated? Did Israeli forces seize and hold it as part of an offensive, or move into abandoned positions as a limited security measure? Did the official statement express an intent to establish control, or a temporary posture pending a replacement security arrangement?

The plaintiff will likely seek UNDOF material, Israeli government material, Syrian or Security Council statements, and credible reporting. The defense will not ask the council to ignore those sources when admitted. But their details will decide the case. An official confirmation that IDF units were present in an area of separation is not identical to confirmation of a market-defined invasion. A report that Israel occupied a strategic position may carry weight, but it must be tied to the market window, to territory not excluded as the Golan Heights, and to an intent to establish control rather than to prevent a perceived vacuum on Israel’s frontier.

Provenance and context will matter too. The best evidence should be the complete official statement, not a clipped quotation; the UN text itself, not a paraphrase; and maps or descriptions capable of distinguishing the Golan Heights, the Mount Hermon peak, the Syrian side, and the disengagement zone. The defense will also test adverse-to-plaintiff context: Israeli descriptions of the move as temporary and defensive, the timing immediately after the Syrian regime’s collapse, and whether later statements describe later conditions rather than a December 2024 commencement.

So the defense opening is not that nothing happened. Something may well have happened. The defense position is that the plaintiff’s burden is narrower and more exacting than the headline “Israel invaded Syria.” On the current admitted record, the council has a rule and a rationale, but not yet the underlying proof needed to resolve the decisive elements. If later admitted evidence establishes a Syrian-territory seizure during the window, held with an intent to control, the council can weigh that. Until then, the proposition should not be treated as proven merely because the market’s rationale says it was.

[plaintiff] Council members, the admitted record now answers the defendant's precision objections. The proposition is satisfied by a preponderance: in December 2024, inside the market window, Israeli forces moved into Syrian territory on the Syrian side of Mount Hermon and the adjoining buffer/area-of-separation, took and held positions there, and did so for continued Israeli security control.

It asks whether Israel commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over any portion of Syria between September 12 and December 31, 2024, and it counts the Golan Heights as Israeli territory. The packet's own rationale identifies the Syrian side of Mount Hermon as the decisive ground and distinguishes the Syrian peak from the Golan Heights. That matters because the defense cannot collapse 'Syrian side of Mount Hermon' into the market-excluded Golan.

That is within the market window. PX-1 also preserves Israel's own adverse framing: Foreign Minister Saar described the IDF as taking 'targeted and temporary control' of certain areas near the border. Geography is established by PX-1 and PX-2. PX-1 places troops in the buffer zone including the Syrian side of Mount Hermon. PX-2 reports that Netanyahu made his December 17 comments from the summit of Mount Hermon 'inside Syria,' about 10 km from the Israel-held Golan Heights, and describes Israel's capture of a demilitarized area in Syrian territory. Those details answer the defense request for a place outside the market-counted Golan Heights.

Control and intent are established several times over. PX-2 reports Netanyahu said Israeli forces would stay in the seized buffer zone until an arrangement ensured Israel's security. It also reports Defense Minister Katz instructed the military to establish a presence and fortifications in anticipation of a possible extended stay. A force that enters Syrian territory, captures or seizes strategic ground, fortifies it, and stays until its own security conditions are met is exercising control. The market does not require annexation, civilian administration, or a formal declaration of war.

The official-source requirement is also satisfied. PX-3, the UN Security Council transcript, records that UNDOF said the IDF had established multiple positions in the area of separation in violation of the 1974 Disengagement Agreement. It records the Syrian representative saying Israeli forces crossed the lines of the area of separation, seized large swathes around Quneitra and Jabal Al-Shaykh, and built bases, observation posts, engineering works, and a helipad. It also records France calling on Israel to withdraw and respect Syria's sovereignty and China urging Israel to withdraw from Syrian territory. PX-4 separately confirms the UN message that what was being seen violated the 1974 disengagement agreement. These are not just press characterizations; they are official UN, Syrian, and permanent Security Council member confirmations.

The defense's best point is that Israel called the operation temporary and defensive after Assad's collapse. That affects political characterization; it does not defeat the market elements. A temporary occupation can be an invasion if it establishes control. A security motive can explain why control was established; it does not mean control was absent. Indeed, PX-1's 'targeted and temporary control,' PX-2's 'stay until another arrangement,' and PX-3's multiple positions, bases, posts, and helipad all point the same way.

Nor is the later date of PX-3 a problem. PX-3 is used as official confirmation of facts and continuing positions, while PX-1 and PX-2 fix the December 2024 commencement within the window. Together they form the source chain the defense asked for: credible contemporaneous reporting on when and where the movement began, an Israeli-leader statement and fortification evidence showing intent to remain, and official UN/Syrian/P5 confirmation that Israeli forces crossed into and held Syrian territory.

The remaining gap is that the plaintiff has not admitted the original Israeli PMO or GPO page for Netanyahu's statement. But the standard is preponderance, not best-evidence absolutism. The record includes the market packet's rationale, AP's contemporaneous report using Government Press Office material, AP's separate timing report, and official UN/Security Council confirmation. That is more likely than not, and well beyond a speculative headline.

The council should resolve Yes: Israel invaded Syria in 2024 under this market's definition.

[defendant] Council members, the plaintiff has now supplied real evidence, and the defense should be candid about what it shows. The record supports that Israeli forces entered the demilitarized/buffer area in Syria, including the Syrian side of Mount Hermon, during December 2024. It also supports that Israeli officials spoke of remaining until a security arrangement existed. Those are serious facts.

But the proposition is narrower than “Israeli forces were in Syrian territory” or “Israel violated the 1974 disengagement arrangement.” It asks whether Israel commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over Syria. The defense position is that the record, read as a whole, shows a temporary border-security deployment into a collapsed buffer-zone situation, not a market-defined offensive intended to establish territorial control.

Start with plaintiff’s own strongest contemporaneous source, PX-1. It reports that Foreign Minister Saar described the action as “targeted and temporary control” of certain areas near the border to prevent an October 7 scenario from Syria. That language matters. It is a concession of some control, but it is also an official framing of the act as targeted, temporary, and border-protective. D-1 strengthens the same point. Reuters reported that Defense Minister Katz described a sterile defense zone to be enforced “without a permanent Israeli presence,” and that IDF spokesperson Nadav Shoshani said Israeli forces were not advancing toward Damascus, were not involved in Syria internally, were not a side in the conflict, and had no interest other than protecting Israel’s borders and citizens.

That does not make the move lawful under international law. It does make it materially different from the market’s phrase “military offensive intended to establish control.” An emergency deployment into an abandoned demilitarized area after the Syrian regime’s collapse can be a violation, an occupation in a colloquial or legal sense, and still not the kind of offensive control operation this market required.

PX-2 is also mixed. It records Netanyahu’s “until another arrangement” statement and Katz’s instruction to establish a presence and fortifications, which are adverse to the defense. But the condition itself is security-based and transitional: remain until another arrangement ensures security. It does not say annex, govern, absorb, or permanently control Syrian territory. D-1’s “without a permanent Israeli presence” and Shoshani’s “not advancing toward Damascus” statement are the best record evidence of the intended scope.

PX-3 is official and weighty, but the council should separate what it proves from what plaintiff asks it to prove. It proves that by February 2025 UNDOF and Security Council speakers described multiple IDF positions in the area of separation and condemned the presence as a violation. It also includes Syria’s adversarial account of large territorial seizures, bases, posts, works, and a helipad. Those statements support illegality and later facts on the ground. They do not, standing alone, establish that in December 2024 Israel commenced a military offensive, as opposed to a security deployment whose duration later became disputed. Much of PX-3 is diplomatic characterization after the market window, not direct evidence of December intent at commencement.

PX-4 is even narrower. It says the UN message was that the observed action violated the 1974 disengagement agreement. Again, violation is not the same as the market’s invasion definition. The proposition did not say “will Israel violate the 1974 agreement” or “will Israel enter the area of separation.” It chose a more demanding formulation: offensive plus intent to establish control.

The defense also asks the council to consider source provenance. The plaintiff still lacks the original Israeli PMO/GPO statement or full video. The AP reports are credible, but they are excerpts and characterizations of official events. The UN transcript is official, but it is two months later and includes statements by interested diplomatic actors. D-1 is likewise secondary reporting, but it captures direct official and military-spokesperson statements that narrow intent. On this record, the cleanest official intent language is temporary, defensive, non-permanent, and border-security focused.

The defense is not asking for an impossible standard. If the market had required any Israeli military presence in Syrian territory, plaintiff would win. If it required any violation of the separation agreement, plaintiff would win. But the text requires a military offensive intended to establish control. The admitted evidence leaves at least an equally plausible account: Israel moved into an abandoned border buffer and some adjacent points to prevent hostile forces from exploiting the vacuum, while publicly limiting its purpose to temporary security pending another arrangement.

Under preponderance, the side with the more likely reading wins. The plaintiff’s reading depends on treating temporary, targeted, security control as automatically satisfying “offensive intended to establish control.” The defense reading gives meaning to “offensive” and to “intended,” and it accounts for the record’s repeated temporary/defensive qualifications. The council should resolve No, or at minimum should decline to treat this record as proving the proposition by a preponderance.
