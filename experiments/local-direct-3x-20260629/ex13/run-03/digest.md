### Openings

[plaintiff] Council members, the plaintiff’s burden is defined by the case-packet rules, and those rules make this a narrow factual inquiry rather than a general debate about diplomacy.

The proposition is demonstrated if, by June 15, 2025 at 11:59 PM Eastern Time, the United States and Iran had agreed to a permanent peace deal. The admitted rules file tells us what that means. A qualifying deal must explicitly state that military hostilities between the United States and Iran have ended or will permanently cease, or use equivalent language clearly signaling a lasting end to those hostilities. The rules also tell us what does not count: a temporary extension, a ceasefire without a definitive lasting end, mere negotiations, progress statements, or non-final announcements.

The plaintiff’s theory is therefore straightforward. This case turns on whether the record can show a definitive bilateral commitment by the cutoff, not whether later events were calm, messy, or politically contested. Under the rules, the strongest proof would be either a written agreement signed or formally adopted by both sides, or clear public confirmation from both governments that a qualifying agreement had been definitively established. A treaty label is not required if the proof otherwise satisfies that standard. The words, timing, and source of the confirmation matter most.

At the opening stage, the current admitted record contains only the rules file. That file is important because it supplies the legal test, but it does not itself prove the historical fact of an agreement. For that reason, the plaintiff will not ask the council to infer the proposition from hope, market chatter, or general diplomatic movement. The plaintiff expects the decisive evidence to be official U.S. and Iranian government statements, any adopted text or instrument, and reliable reporting only insofar as it helps confirm what the governments themselves said and when they said it.

The plaintiff also recognizes the adverse questions. If the evidence shows only suspended nuclear talks, temporary ceasefire language, unilateral statements, post-cutoff developments, or a promise to keep negotiating, that would not satisfy the rules. If one government clearly denied that a qualifying agreement had been reached by the deadline, that would carry real weight. If a later agreement was necessary because no permanent peace deal existed earlier, the timing problem would have to be confronted directly.

But the rules set a preponderance standard, not a formalistic trap. If the admitted evidence establishes that both governments clearly confirmed, by the deadline, a definitive agreement to permanently end U.S.-Iran military hostilities, then the proposition is demonstrated even if implementation details, nuclear issues, or political objections remained unsettled. The plaintiff’s case will focus on that precise question: whether the source record proves mutual, timely, permanent commitment rather than temporary de-escalation or ongoing negotiation.

[defendant] Council members, the defense agrees with one point in plaintiff’s opening: this case is narrow. The proposition is not whether diplomacy with Iran was desirable, whether talks occurred, whether tensions later eased, or whether someone announced progress. The admitted case-packet rules require something much more specific by June 15, 2025 at 11:59 PM Eastern: a permanent peace deal between Iran and the United States.

The only evidence now in the record is the rules file, `ev_f1a176053ed8_rules`. That file proves the governing test and nothing more. It says a qualifying deal must explicitly state that U.S.-Iran military hostilities have ended or will permanently cease, or use equivalent language clearly signaling a lasting end to those hostilities. It also excludes the very categories most likely to be confused with proof here: temporary extensions, ceasefires without a definitive lasting end, negotiations, progress statements, and non-final announcements.

That distinction will decide the case. Plaintiff must do more than show contact between diplomats, nuclear discussions, optimism from one side, mediation by a third country, or later attempts to stabilize the relationship. Plaintiff must establish either a signed or formally adopted written agreement meeting the rules, or clear public confirmation by both governments that such a qualifying agreement had definitively been established before the cutoff.

The defense theory is straightforward: unless plaintiff can produce that bilateral, timely, permanent commitment, the proposition is not demonstrated. A unilateral U.S. statement would not be enough. A unilateral Iranian statement would not be enough. A planned meeting, a draft, an exchange through mediators, or a promise to continue talking would not be enough. A ceasefire or pause would not be enough unless it definitively ended U.S.-Iran military hostilities on a lasting basis. And any post-cutoff development would not cure the timing problem; if a later agreement was still needed, that tends to show the required agreement did not already exist by June 15, 2025.

The current record contains no written peace instrument, no adopted bilateral text, and no public confirmation from both governments. That absence matters at the opening stage because the rules make official U.S. and Iranian government information the primary resolution source. In later evidence phases, the defense expects the key proof to be official statements, dated government releases, and reliable reporting only insofar as it confirms what each government actually said and when. The most important adverse checks will be the full source chain for any claimed agreement: who spoke, whether they had authority, whether the other government matched the statement, whether the language was permanent rather than temporary, and whether it was made before the deadline.

So the defense asks the council to keep the burden centered. The rules do not allow a finding for plaintiff based on diplomatic smoke, market language, or after-the-fact framing. They require a definitive, bilateral, timely permanent peace deal. On this record, that historical fact has not been shown, and unless plaintiff supplies admissible evidence closing each of those gaps, the proper resolution is not demonstrated.
