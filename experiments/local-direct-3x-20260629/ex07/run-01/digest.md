### Closings

[plaintiff] Council members, the record resolves this case on a preponderance standard.

The proposition asks whether, by May 31, Russia and Ukraine had officially reached a publicly announced, mutually agreed halt in military engagement that constituted a general pause. It does not ask whether the war ended, whether the ceasefire was durable, or whether neither side later accused the other of violations.

The decisive record is straightforward. PX-1 defines the qualifying event as an official ceasefire agreement, publicly announced and mutually agreed, and permits a wide consensus of credible reporting that such an official agreement was reached. PX-3 reports that Russia and Ukraine confirmed a May 9-11 ceasefire, that Trump announced it as a ceasefire in the war between Russia and Ukraine, that it included suspension of all kinetic activity, and that it was confirmed by both sides. PX-2 independently preserves the Russian official-source path.

That evidence satisfies the elements: official/public, mutual, dated before the deadline, and general in the only sense the market required: a halt in military engagement in the war, not a sector-only Black Sea or energy arrangement, not a mere informal understanding, and not a future framework.

The defense’s contrary evidence is important, but it points mainly to performance. DX-4 and DX-5 show continued operations, violation accusations, unclear mechanisms, and weak monitoring. We should not ignore that. But both exhibits also preserve the formation facts: Trump announced the ceasefire, Zelenskyy confirmed, and both sides appeared to agree. A breached ceasefire is still a ceasefire agreement. The proposition turns on whether the agreement was reached, not whether it was perfectly obeyed.

The defense says the prisoner exchange and Victory Day context make the pause non-general. The better reading is that they explain why the parties accepted a temporary general pause. A prisoner exchange can occur during a broad ceasefire; it does not make the ceasefire humanitarian-only when the announced scope is suspension of all kinetic activity. Red Square non-targeting was an additional concrete operational condition, not the whole agreement. The record contains separate broader language: ceasefire in the war, ceasefire regime on May 9-11, suspension of kinetic activity, and both sides confirmed.

There are source-chain gaps: direct X, Truth Social, and some official pages were hard to capture. But the market rule expressly allows credible media consensus, and here the accepted sources converge with the case-packet official-source record. The defense’s own evidence does not break that convergence; it disputes execution.

On balance, plaintiff’s evidence proves more likely than not that Russia and Ukraine officially reached and publicly announced a mutually agreed May 9-11 halt in military engagement before May 31. The council should find the proposition true.

[defendant] Council members, this case is not about whether something happened on May 8. Something did. The record supports that a short May 9-11 truce was announced and that both sides were reported as accepting it. The question is whether plaintiff proved the market’s stricter proposition: an official, public, mutually agreed halt in military engagement that constituted a general pause in the conflict, not a humanitarian pause, limited arrangement, or future framework.

Plaintiff has not carried that burden.

Start with the governing rule. The market-page record requires a general pause and excludes humanitarian and limited agreements. That means the council cannot stop at the word “ceasefire” or at the fact of a public announcement. It must ask what kind of ceasefire the record proves.

The official-source packet supplies the best answer. On the Ukrainian official side, the direct presidential decree was not captured; the official-adjacent decree record is Red Square and parade-specific. Plaintiff’s own merits exhibits do not eliminate that limitation. PX-4 quotes Zelenskyy in the context of the 1,000-for-1,000 exchange and says a ceasefire regime must also be established. PX-5 reports the Trump announcement but also says Zelenskyy ordered no attack on Red Square and preserves the same prisoner-swap frame. Yes, those sources use broad language such as “suspension of all kinetic activity.” But broad media language must be weighed against the actual official-source detail, the missing primary captures, and what the parties did when the supposed general pause began.

That implementation evidence is decisive because it goes to character and scope, not merely breach. DX-4 reports that Russian and Ukrainian forces continued offensive operations across the frontline during the first day; combat intensity decreased but did not stop; Russian strikes continued in Kharkiv and Kherson; and the ceasefire lacked clearly defined conditions. The source-chain gaps reinforce caution. Direct X or Telegram, direct Truth Social or White House, and the direct Ukrainian decree remain absent. The rule allows credible media consensus, but consensus reporting does not override ambiguity in the official-source record and battlefield evidence. At most, plaintiff proved a fragile, short, U.S.-brokered truce label attached to Victory Day and a prisoner exchange. The market required more.

On a preponderance standard, if the evidence is equally consistent with a limited prisoner-exchange/parade-security pause and a qualifying general pause, plaintiff loses. Here the limiting evidence is stronger than equal. The council should find the proposition not proven.
