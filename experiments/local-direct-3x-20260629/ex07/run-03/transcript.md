### Openings

#### Plaintiff Opening

Council members, this case turns on a narrow but important distinction: whether Russia and Ukraine had, by the May 31 deadline, officially reached and publicly announced a mutually agreed halt in military engagement that was general enough to qualify, or whether the May arrangement was merely informal, humanitarian, sector-specific, or only a future framework.

The plaintiff's theory is straightforward. The record already points to an official, public, mutually accepted May 9-May 11 ceasefire arrangement. It was not a Black Sea-only, energy-infrastructure-only, or private diplomatic aspiration. The preserved market rule asks for a publicly announced and mutually agreed halt in military engagement, and it expressly allows resolution by either official announcements from both sides or a wide consensus of credible reporting that an official ceasefire agreement had been reached. The record contains both the beginning of an official-source chain and a wide credible-reporting chain.

The official-source record is significant. It preserves a Russian presidential-site source through Yury Ushakov stating that, on President Putin's instructions, the Russian side considered acceptable the U.S.-proposed initiative concerning a ceasefire between Russia and Ukraine, and that the key agreement was a May 9-May 11 prisoner exchange during the ceasefire period. That is not a rumor or trader commentary; it is an official Russian account of acceptance. On Ukraine, the present packet is less complete because the direct presidential decree URL was inaccessible in the environment, but the packet preserves official-adjacent Ukrinform reporting that a presidential decree existed and that Ukraine had received Russia's agreement to the 1,000-for-1,000 exchange, alongside reporting that President Zelenskyy confirmed the ceasefire arrangement.

The secondary-reporting file matters because the market rule itself permits a wide consensus of credible media reporting as an evidentiary path. The packet summarizes Reuters, Guardian, NPR, CBC, and ISW as reporting that Russia and Ukraine confirmed a U.S.-brokered three-day ceasefire from May 9 to May 11, with suspension of kinetic activity and a 1,000-for-1,000 prisoner exchange. This is not one stray article; the packet presents a consistent multi-source account, while also preserving adverse details for the council to weigh.

Those adverse details define the real defense case. The ceasefire was temporary. It was tied to Victory Day, Red Square/parade concerns, and a prisoner exchange. The Russian official statement frames the initiative as for the purpose of conducting that exchange. The Ukrainian decree material preserved through Ukrinform appears narrower as to Red Square targeting during the parade. The record also identifies missing direct captures of Zelenskyy's Telegram or X post, the Ukrainian presidential decree, Trump's original announcement or White House record, and battlefield monitoring or violation evidence. The plaintiff does not ask the council to ignore those gaps.

But those gaps do not defeat the proposition at the opening stage. A general pause can be temporary. A ceasefire can include a prisoner exchange and still be a halt in military engagement if the agreed pause is not limited to a single corridor, a single weapons class, one sea lane, or one infrastructure category. The key question will be whether the May 9-May 11 arrangement suspended military engagement generally, even if briefly and even if imperfectly observed. The preserved reporting of a suspension of all kinetic activity is therefore central. So is the official Russian acknowledgment of a ceasefire between the two states during the same dated period.

The plaintiff expects the later proof to focus on four issues: first, direct captures or faithful extracts of the Ukrainian and U.S. announcements; second, the full official Russian source already identified in the packet; third, credible reports or monitoring material about whether the announced halt was theater-wide in scope; and fourth, any post-period official statements or violation reports that show whether alleged breaches were treated as violations of a ceasefire rather than evidence no agreement existed.

At this point, the packet supports the plaintiff more than the defense. It shows an official Russian acceptance, an official-adjacent Ukrainian confirmation path, and a broad credible-media consensus that both parties agreed to a dated ceasefire suspending kinetic activity. The defense has real arguments about limited purpose and implementation, but on a preponderance standard the decisive question is whether an official, public, mutually agreed general halt was reached by the deadline. The plaintiff will show that it was.

#### Defendant Opening

Council members, the defense opening is simple: this case is not resolved by the word "ceasefire" appearing in headlines. The proposition requires an official, public, mutually agreed halt in military engagement that constituted a general pause in the conflict. It expressly excludes humanitarian pauses, informal arrangements, and limited agreements. On the present record, plaintiff has not yet carried that burden.

The starting packet gives plaintiff a surface-level story: several media summaries described a May 9-May 11 ceasefire, a prisoner exchange, and confirmations by both sides. But the same packet also identifies the problems that matter under the actual rule. The official Russian source preserved in the record frames the initiative as a ceasefire between Russia and Ukraine "for the purpose of conducting a prisoner exchange," with the key agreement being a 1,000-for-1,000 exchange during the May 9-May 11 period. That is material because a prisoner-exchange pause is exactly the kind of limited or humanitarian arrangement the rule tells the council not to count unless it also proves a general halt in military engagement.

The Ukrainian official-source path is weaker for plaintiff, not stronger. The packet identifies an official presidential decree path, but the captured substance through Ukrinform is narrow: it concerns allowing the May 9 Moscow parade and excluding Red Square from Ukrainian weapons targeting during the parade. The packet itself says that decree does not, by itself, state a theater-wide halt in military engagement. That is not a minor provenance issue. It is the missing link between a limited parade/security accommodation and the market's required general pause in the conflict.

The market-page evidence also deserves careful weight. It preserves the rule and reports that the platform outcome was Yes, but the packet warns that platform assertions and after-the-fact context are not independent proof of official mutuality, theater-wide scope, implementation, or media reliability. The arbitration is about the factual condition, not whether Polymarket or traders treated it as satisfied.

So the real dispute is scope and official mutuality. Did both governments publicly agree to stop military engagement generally, across the conflict, even briefly? Or did they agree to a three-day, Victory Day and prisoner-exchange arrangement, with a geographically narrow Ukrainian decree and ongoing accusations or operations around the period? The present record contains enough to investigate, but not enough to collapse those categories.

The defense expects the decisive proof to be direct captures of the Russian and Ukrainian official statements, the original U.S. announcement if plaintiff relies on it, Zelenskyy's original Telegram or X confirmation, and monitoring or official post-period material showing whether hostilities actually paused generally or continued in material ways. If those sources show a clear, mutual, theater-wide commitment, plaintiff may have a case. If they show a prisoner swap, parade security carveout, temporary symbolism, or violated/continued operations without a real general pause, the proposition fails.

At opening, the council should hold plaintiff to the rule's exclusions. A temporary ceasefire can sometimes qualify, but a temporary label is not enough. The record's primary-source chain presently points to limited purpose and incomplete custody, while the broader affirmative case rests mainly on secondary summaries and platform context. On a preponderance standard, the defense will show that plaintiff has not established the required official, publicly announced, mutually agreed general halt in military engagement by the deadline.
