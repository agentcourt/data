## Filings

### Openings

[plaintiff] Council members, the plaintiff’s burden is preponderance, and the proposition is narrow but not trivial. We do not have to prove a peace treaty, a permanent end to the war, or flawless compliance. We must show that by May 31, 2026, Russia and Ukraine had officially reached and publicly announced a mutually agreed halt in military engagement that constituted a general pause in the conflict, rather than an informal understanding, a sector-only carveout, a humanitarian pause, or a future framework.

The current packet already identifies the real dispute. The market rule preserved in ev_f841be6ff80d_market-page requires an official ceasefire agreement, publicly announced and mutually agreed, and it expressly allows either official announcements from both sides or a wide consensus of credible reporting that such an official agreement was reached. That same packet cautions, correctly, that the market’s final “Yes” outcome and platform context are not independent proof. The case should be decided from the source record, not from platform say-so.

On that source record, plaintiff’s theory is straightforward. The preserved official-source record, ev_959b165782b3_official-source-record, contains the Russian side of the mutual announcement: the Kremlin-published Yury Ushakov statement confirms Russia considered acceptable the U.S. President’s initiative concerning a ceasefire between Russia and Ukraine and a prisoner exchange, and describes the relevant understanding as reached through contacts with the U.S. administration. The same case packet records the Ukrainian side through Ukrinform and associated reporting: Ukraine received Russia’s agreement to a 1,000-for-1,000 exchange, and a ceasefire regime was to be established on May 9, 10, and 11. The plaintiff will show this was not merely a future aspiration; it was a dated ceasefire period before the May 31 deadline.

The strongest record support is not the market page, but the convergence described in ev_cf0c77f2fe52_secondary-reporting and ev_54e94624d245_primary-evidence. Reuters, the Guardian, NPR, CBC, and ISW are summarized as reporting that both Russia and Ukraine confirmed a U.S.-brokered three-day ceasefire from May 9 through May 11, paired with a large prisoner exchange, and including a suspension of kinetic activity. That kind of cross-source confirmation matters because the market rule itself permits a wide consensus of credible media reporting where it states that an official ceasefire agreement between Russia and Ukraine has been reached.

The defense will have serious points, and plaintiff should meet them directly. The Russian statement frames the ceasefire around a prisoner exchange and Victory Day timing. The Ukrainian decree captured through Ukrinform is narrow as to Red Square targeting during the parade. The unresolved-gaps file, ev_41c18aa39f56_unresolved-record-gaps, also notes missing direct captures of Zelenskyy’s Telegram/X confirmation, the Ukrainian official decree page, Trump or White House source material, and battlefield-monitoring evidence about whether hostilities actually paused. Those gaps affect weight and source custody. They do not erase what the admitted packet already tends to show: mutually public confirmation of a dated May 9-11 ceasefire arrangement.

The decisive question will be scope. If the evidence ultimately shows only Red Square immunity, a POW-transfer window, Black Sea or energy-infrastructure limits, or a humanitarian pause without a broader halt in military engagement, plaintiff should lose. But if the full source record confirms what the packet says the credible reporting reported, namely a May 9-11 ceasefire regime suspending all kinetic military activity between the two states, then the arrangement was a general pause even though temporary, politically timed, and allegedly imperfectly observed.

In later evidentiary phases, plaintiff will seek to put the council on the strongest sources: the full Kremlin text; the direct Ukrainian presidential, Telegram, or X confirmation if accessible; full Reuters and other reporting preserved in the packet; ISW’s assessment; and adverse implementation materials concerning alleged violations. At opening, the record already gives the council the right lens: this case turns on official mutuality and general scope, not on whether the ceasefire ended the war or whether each side later accused the other of violations.

[defendant] Council members, the defense opening begins with the market’s own limiting words. The proposition is not satisfied by showing that someone used the word “ceasefire,” that there was a prisoner exchange, or that a short symbolic pause was announced around Victory Day. The plaintiff must prove, by a preponderance, an official, publicly announced, mutually agreed halt in military engagement that amounted to a general pause in the conflict. The exclusions matter: humanitarian pauses and limited agreements do not qualify.

The current packet is enough to identify the dispute, but not enough to resolve it in plaintiff’s favor. The market-page record, ev_f841be6ff80d_market-page, gives the governing standard and also warns that the platform’s final “Yes” context is not independent proof. The case must therefore turn on the underlying source record: official mutuality, scope, and whether the arrangement was general rather than limited.

On those elements, the record is materially weaker than plaintiff’s opening suggests. The official-source record, ev_959b165782b3_official-source-record, says the Russian-side source was Yury Ushakov’s Kremlin-published comment. But that source is framed as an initiative “concerning a ceasefire between Russia and Ukraine for the purpose of conducting a prisoner exchange,” with the key agreement described as an exchange of 1,000 prisoners from each side during the May 9-May 11 ceasefire period. That is relevant, but it points toward a prisoner-exchange arrangement, not necessarily a general conflict pause.

The Ukrainian official-source material is narrower still. The same official-source record reports that the Ukrainian presidential decree, as captured through Ukrinform, authorized the May 9 parade in Moscow and excluded Red Square from Ukrainian weapons targeting plans for the duration of the parade. The packet itself states that this decree does not by itself state a theater-wide halt in military engagement. That distinction is central. A Red Square/parade targeting carveout is not the same thing as a general pause between two armies across the conflict.

The plaintiff’s strongest material is the secondary-reporting summary in ev_cf0c77f2fe52_secondary-reporting and the source list in ev_54e94624d245_primary-evidence. Those files summarize Reuters, the Guardian, NPR, CBC, and ISW as reporting a confirmed three-day ceasefire, and some reports use broader language about suspending kinetic activity. The defense does not ask the council to ignore that. We ask the council to weigh it against the packet’s own caveats: several sources tie the arrangement to Victory Day, Red Square or parade security, and a prisoner swap; the direct Ukrainian Telegram or X confirmation is not preserved in the packet; the direct Trump or White House source is not preserved; and the implementation record remains incomplete.

The unresolved-gaps file, ev_41c18aa39f56_unresolved-record-gaps, is unusually important at this opening stage. It identifies exactly the missing links that should decide the case: direct captures of the Ukrainian confirmation, the official Ukrainian decree, the U.S. announcement, battlefield or monitoring evidence about whether hostilities generally paused, and post-period official violation or implementation statements. Those are not minor housekeeping gaps. They go to whether this was a general pause or a narrow, politically timed, humanitarian and exchange-linked arrangement.

So the defense theory is simple. Plaintiff may be able to prove that there was a public May 9-May 11 arrangement, and maybe even mutual public acknowledgement of some ceasefire regime. But the qualifying proposition requires more. It requires a general halt in military engagement. If the later admitted source record shows only a prisoner-exchange window, Red Square immunity, Victory Day parade security, partial silence, or a ceasefire label followed by continuing military operations, the proposition fails.

In the evidence phases, the decisive proof should be the full official Russian text, the direct Ukrainian presidential or social-media confirmation, the U.S. source for the alleged brokered terms, and reliable reporting or monitoring of what happened across the theater on May 9, 10, and 11. Until that record proves both general scope and official mutuality, the plaintiff has not carried the burden.

### Arguments

[plaintiff] Council members, plaintiff has met the preponderance standard. The proposition asks whether, by May 31, Russia and Ukraine had officially reached and publicly announced a mutually agreed halt in military engagement constituting a general pause. It does not require a permanent peace, a thirty-day truce, or perfect compliance after announcement.

The governing rule is in PX-1: the market required a publicly announced and mutually agreed halt in military engagement, and it expressly allowed proof either from official announcements by both sides or from a wide consensus of credible media reporting that an official ceasefire agreement had been reached. PX-1 also excludes merely informal, humanitarian, Black Sea, energy-only, or similar limited agreements. That makes the decisive issues mutuality and scope.

On mutuality, the record is now strong. PX-2, the packet's official-source record, captures the Russian side through the Kremlin/Ushakov statement: Russia accepted the U.S. initiative concerning a ceasefire between Russia and Ukraine and a prisoner exchange, with the understanding reached through contacts with the U.S. administration. PX-5, ABC News, reports Trump said he appreciated the agreement by both Putin and Zelenskyy, that Zelenskyy confirmed the ceasefire-regime terms for May 9, 10, and 11, and that the Kremlin confirmed it would observe the ceasefire. PX-6, the Guardian, likewise reports the arrangement was confirmed by both sides, quotes Zelenskyy's May 9-11 ceasefire-regime language, and reports Russian confirmation through Ushakov. PX-7, China Daily/Xinhua, independently reports Russia and Ukraine agreed to a three-day ceasefire and quotes both Ushakov and Zelenskyy. PX-8, Ukrainska Pravda, reports Russia agreed to extend the ceasefire with Ukraine until May 11 and conduct the exchange, and notes Zelenskyy confirmed the May 9-11 ceasefire with Russia.

On scope, the best evidence points to a general halt, not a sector carveout. PX-5 preserves Trump's statement that the ceasefire would include a suspension of all kinetic activity. PX-6 reports the same "all kinetic activity" formulation and describes it as between the warring states. PX-7 again records that Trump said the truce between Russia and Ukraine would include suspension of all kinetic activity. That phrase is broader than Red Square, the Black Sea, energy infrastructure, or POW-transfer corridors. It is ordinary military-engagement language for a theater-wide pause in firing and strikes.

The defense is right that the arrangement was short, politically timed, and connected to a prisoner exchange and Victory Day. Plaintiff does not hide that. PX-5 says Putin had sought a short pause over Victory Day. PX-7 records Zelenskyy's Red Square decree and Russian MOD Victory Day language. PX-8 says Ushakov tied the initiative to Victory Day. PX-4, the unresolved-gaps packet, flags the same issues. But purpose and timing are not the same as operational scope. A ceasefire may be motivated by a prisoner exchange or holiday and still be a general halt if the announced commitment is to suspend all kinetic activity between the states for specified dates. The market excluded humanitarian pauses; it did not exclude a general ceasefire merely because it was temporary or paired with humanitarian exchange.

Nor do alleged violations defeat the proposition. The question is whether an official, mutual, publicly announced halt had been reached by the deadline. PX-1 says a qualifying agreement counts regardless of whether the ceasefire starts after the resolution date; it follows that compliance evidence is relevant to weight and scope, but not a separate requirement of flawless implementation. The cited sources include adverse context about prior violations and skepticism, but still report the agreement and the all-kinetic-activity term.

Source weight is candidly mixed but preponderant. The direct Ukrainian official page and social post were not preserved as primary artifacts; direct U.S. social capture also remains imperfect. Yet the rule permits credible-media consensus, and the accepted evidence provides multiple independent reports preserving the same official statements from Trump, Zelenskyy, and Ushakov. Those reports also include the defense's limiting facts, which makes their convergence more, not less, reliable: they were not one-sided market blurbs.

The preponderance answer is therefore Yes. By May 31, and in fact by May 8-9, the public record more likely than not showed Russia and Ukraine had officially and mutually agreed to a dated May 9-11 ceasefire regime suspending all kinetic activity. Its short duration, Victory Day setting, prisoner-swap purpose, and imperfect source chain reduce weight, but they do not reclassify an announced all-kinetic ceasefire into a merely informal, humanitarian, sector-specific, or future-framework arrangement.

[defendant] Council members, plaintiff’s evidence proves less than plaintiff says. It probably proves that public reporting used the word “ceasefire,” that Trump announced a three-day arrangement, that both sides acknowledged some arrangement, and that a 1,000-for-1,000 prisoner exchange was paired with it. But the proposition is narrower and harder: it requires an official, mutually agreed halt in military engagement that constituted a general pause in the conflict, not a humanitarian pause or other limited arrangement.

Start with the official materials. The Russian-side official source in D-3 frames the initiative as a ceasefire “for the purpose of conducting a prisoner exchange,” and describes the key agreement as the May 9-May 11 exchange of 1,000 prisoners from each side during the ceasefire period. That is not nothing. But it is exchange-purpose language, not a bilateral operational instrument setting a theater-wide stop to fighting.

The direct Ukrainian official decree is even narrower. D-1 is the official presidential decree page. It is titled about holding a parade in Moscow. It states a humanitarian purpose, permits the May 9 Moscow parade, and excludes only the Red Square coordinate box from Ukraine’s weapons-use plan for the duration of that parade. D-1 does not say Ukraine ordered a general halt across the front. It is powerful precisely because it is a direct official Ukrainian source: the preserved decree is a parade and Red Square targeting carveout.

Plaintiff’s broadest scope evidence comes from secondary reporting, especially PX-5, PX-6, and PX-7, which preserve Trump’s “suspension of all kinetic activity” formulation and report Zelenskyy language that a ceasefire regime must be established on May 9, 10, and 11. The defense accepts that those exhibits have weight. They show why this is a close case. But they are still not the direct Ukrainian social post, direct Truth Social capture, or direct bilateral terms. Their own capture limitations say so. And the same exhibits preserve the limiting facts: Victory Day timing, prisoner-swap purpose, Red Square decree, Russian MOD Victory Day framing, prior violation history, and the absence of direct official/social source captures.

Implementation evidence matters because the rule asks for a “general pause in the conflict,” not just a press label. D-2, the Critical Threats/ISW May 10 assessment, reports that Russian and Ukrainian forces continued limited offensive operations across the theater throughout the second day of the May 9-11 ceasefire. It records mutual accusations of violations, claimed assaults, artillery and drone strikes, NASA FIRMS signatures showing hostilities decreased but did not cease, and persistent tactical combat missions. That evidence does not merely show imperfect compliance at the margins. It bears directly on whether the supposed arrangement ever functioned as a general pause rather than a short, loosely monitored, politically timed and exchange-linked truce framework.

Plaintiff argues that violations cannot defeat a reached agreement. Sometimes that is right. A party can breach a ceasefire after making one. But the proposition here does not ask only whether a document or announcement existed; it asks whether the official arrangement constituted a general pause in the conflict. When the direct Ukrainian official act is Red Square-specific, the Russian official framing is prisoner-exchange-specific, and contemporaneous monitoring reports theater-wide continued operations, the better inference is not a qualifying general pause. It is a limited Victory Day/prisoner-exchange/parade-security arrangement, announced with broader diplomatic language, but not shown by a preponderance to be the kind of general halt the rule required.

The source-chain gaps reinforce that conclusion. D-4 identifies missing direct captures of Zelenskyy’s Telegram/X confirmation, the U.S. source, battlefield monitoring, and post-period implementation statements. Plaintiff filled part of that with secondary reporting, but not the decisive primary terms. Plaintiff also did not submit a full direct Ukrainian source that says more than D-1. On this record, the broadest language is mediated through reporting; the most direct Ukrainian official source is narrow; and the best implementation evidence shows continued activity.

The council need not find that plaintiff fabricated anything. The more modest finding is enough: plaintiff has not carried preponderance on the qualifying element. A short announced ceasefire label, paired with POW exchange and parade security, is not necessarily a general conflict pause. The direct and adverse evidence makes the non-qualifying characterization at least as likely as plaintiff’s. Under the stated standard and exclusions, the proposition should be found not proven.

### Rebuttals

[plaintiff] Council members, the defense’s best new evidence does not defeat plaintiff’s showing. It proves two narrower points: Ukraine issued a Red Square/parade decree, and the ceasefire was violated or only partly observed. Neither point changes the preponderance answer on whether an official, public, mutual general ceasefire agreement was reached.

First, the Ukrainian decree in PX-R1 is narrower than the whole arrangement, but it is not inconsistent with the whole arrangement. It says Ukraine permitted the Moscow parade and excluded Red Square coordinates from its weapons-use plan for the parade period. That is a specific implementation act addressing the most politically sensitive target. It does not say Ukraine rejected, limited, or replaced the May 9-11 ceasefire regime that Zelenskyy was reported to have confirmed. The defense turns silence in one decree into contradiction. The better inference is additive: Ukraine made a specific Red Square order within a broader U.S.-brokered truce-and-exchange package.

Second, PX-R2 is powerful implementation evidence, but it is not agreement-formation evidence. It repeatedly describes the May 9-11 period as a ceasefire and treats continued operations as violations of that ceasefire. Its key takeaway is that forces continued limited offensive operations across the theater during the second day. That helps show breach, poor monitoring, and imperfect compliance. It does not show there was no official agreement. A violated ceasefire remains a ceasefire agreement; otherwise any party could erase a public ceasefire by breaching it immediately.

PX-R2 also contains facts the defense understates. It says NASA FIRMS signatures indicated hostilities further decreased on May 10. It reports no large Russian missile packages, only 27 drones in the specified overnight/early-morning window, all downed, and several localized operations with no advance. It even notes some sectors where no ground assaults occurred on May 9 or where operations continued at a reduced pace. That is not proof of a total, perfect pause, but the proposition does not require perfection. It supports the more modest conclusion that a general ceasefire was announced and had some theater-wide operational effect, while being violated in places.

The defense’s legal move is to convert implementation failures into a scope limitation. The record does not support that conversion. The strongest scope evidence remains the common phrase preserved in PX-5, PX-6, and PX-7: suspension of all kinetic activity between Russia and Ukraine. That phrase is not Red Square-specific, not Black Sea-specific, not energy-infrastructure-specific, and not merely a future framework. The prisoner exchange and Victory Day context explain why the ceasefire happened; they do not narrow the announced operational term.

Nor does the direct-source gap carry the defense over the line. PX-1 allows a wide consensus of credible media reporting that an official ceasefire agreement was reached. Plaintiff supplied multiple accepted reports preserving the same official statements from Trump, Zelenskyy, and Ushakov. Those reports include the adverse facts the defense relies on, which makes their mutual-confirmation and all-kinetic-activity reporting more reliable, not less.

So the rebuttal point is simple: PX-R1 proves a specific Ukrainian parade carveout, not the absence of a broader ceasefire; PX-R2 proves violations and reduced-but-continuing activity, not the absence of an official mutual agreement. On this record, it remains more likely than not that Russia and Ukraine publicly and mutually reached a dated May 9-11 halt in military engagement broad enough to constitute a general pause, even though it was short, politically timed, and imperfectly observed.

### Surrebuttals

[defendant] Council members, plaintiff’s rebuttal correctly identifies the hard question but answers the wrong one. The defense is not arguing the abstract rule that every violation erases every ceasefire agreement. A breached ceasefire can still be a ceasefire agreement. The point is narrower: this proposition requires a publicly announced and mutually agreed halt in military engagement that constituted a general pause in the conflict. Formation, scope, and classification cannot be separated as cleanly as plaintiff proposes.

D-1 is not merely an argument from silence. It is the direct Ukrainian official source now in the record, and it is affirmative about what it does: humanitarian purpose, permission for the Moscow parade, and a Red Square targeting exclusion during the parade. It does not contain the theater-wide commitment plaintiff needs. Plaintiff says this was just one implementation act within a broader arrangement, but the broader Ukrainian commitment remains proved through secondary reporting rather than the direct official Ukrainian source. Under preponderance, that matters.

D-2 is likewise not just “imperfect compliance.” It reports that Russian and Ukrainian forces continued limited offensive operations across the theater throughout the second day; that activity decreased but did not cease; that tactical combat missions persisted; and that the arrangement lacked explicit enforcement, monitoring, and dispute-resolution mechanisms. Those facts bear directly on whether the arrangement constituted a general pause. A reduction in tempo is not the same as a general halt in military engagement.

Plaintiff’s strongest phrase remains “suspension of all kinetic activity,” preserved through the secondary reports. But the official and implementation record pulls the other way: Russian official framing around the prisoner exchange, Ukrainian official action limited to Red Square/parade security, and contemporaneous reporting of continued theater-wide operations. The council can accept that there was a public May 9-11 ceasefire label and still find that plaintiff has not shown a qualifying general pause rather than a short, political, exchange-linked and only partially observed arrangement.

That is enough for defense. The exclusions in PX-1 would mean little if any temporary truce label plus broad diplomatic phrasing qualified despite direct narrow official acts and continued operations across the conflict zone. Plaintiff has not made the qualifying characterization more likely than the non-qualifying one.

### Closings

[plaintiff] Council members, the plaintiff asks for a Yes finding because the record makes the qualifying proposition more likely than not.

Start with the rule. PX-1 did not demand a peace treaty, a permanent halt, or perfect performance. It required a publicly announced, mutually agreed halt in military engagement that constituted a general pause. It also allowed proof either through official announcements from both sides or through a wide consensus of credible media reporting that an official ceasefire agreement had been reached. That last clause matters because the record contains exactly that kind of convergent reporting.

The central affirmative proof is consistent across independent exhibits. PX-5 reports Trump announced that Russia and Ukraine agreed to a three-day ceasefire and that the ceasefire would include suspension of all kinetic activity, with agreement by Putin and Zelenskyy; it also reports Zelenskyy’s May 9-11 ceasefire-regime language and Kremlin confirmation. PX-6 reports both sides confirmed the May 9-11 ceasefire, the prisoner swap, and the suspension of all kinetic activity. PX-7 independently reports Russia and Ukraine agreed to a three-day ceasefire, quotes Ushakov for Russian acceptance, quotes Zelenskyy for the May 9-11 ceasefire regime, and again records the all-kinetic-activity term. PX-8 and PX-2 support the Russian side of the same account. The evidence converges on mutuality, public announcement, dates before May 31, and a broad operational term.

The defense’s strongest point is that the arrangement was tied to Victory Day, a prisoner exchange, and a Red Square decree. That is true but not decisive. A general ceasefire can have a political occasion and a humanitarian purpose. The market excluded merely humanitarian pauses; it did not say a general all-kinetic ceasefire becomes non-qualifying because it is paired with a prisoner exchange. The question is scope. The record’s broadest and repeated scope term is not Red Square, Black Sea, energy infrastructure, or a POW corridor. It is suspension of all kinetic activity between Russia and Ukraine.

D-1, the Ukrainian decree, is the defense’s best direct official document. But it proves a narrow implementation act: Ukraine permitted the Moscow parade and excluded Red Square coordinates from its weapons-use plan during the parade. It does not say the broader ceasefire regime did not exist. It does not retract Zelenskyy’s reported May 9-11 ceasefire statement. Treating one specific decree as the exclusive Ukrainian commitment is an inference the document does not support.

D-2, the Critical Threats/ISW assessment, is serious adverse evidence on implementation. It says limited operations continued, activity decreased but did not cease, and mutual accusations persisted. Plaintiff has never hidden that. But D-2 also repeatedly calls the period the May 9-11 ceasefire and describes continued operations as violations of it. It reports decreased hostilities, no large missile packages, and some sectors with no advance or no ground assaults. That is evidence of breach and imperfect observance; it is not better evidence that no official general ceasefire was reached. A party’s violations do not rewrite the announced agreement into a narrow carveout.

The source chain is imperfect. Direct Ukrainian social capture and direct U.S. social capture are missing. But preponderance is not certainty, and PX-1 expressly accepts credible-media consensus. Here, multiple accepted sources preserve the same official statements while also preserving the defense’s limiting facts. That balanced convergence gives them real weight.

The best reading of the whole record is this: by May 8-9, Russia and Ukraine had publicly and mutually accepted a dated May 9-11 ceasefire regime, announced as suspending all kinetic activity, connected to a large prisoner exchange and Victory Day, and then imperfectly observed. That is still a general pause in military engagement, not merely an informal agreement, sector-specific ceasefire, Red Square-only carveout, or future framework. On a preponderance standard, the proposition is true.

[defendant] Council members, the defense closing is not that nothing happened. Something did happen: public reporting described a May 9-11 ceasefire, a 1,000-for-1,000 prisoner exchange, and statements by Trump, Zelenskyy, and Ushakov. Plaintiff’s best evidence is the repeated “suspension of all kinetic activity” phrase in PX-5, PX-6, and PX-7. That gives plaintiff a real case.

But the proposition is not “was there a ceasefire label?” or “was there a prisoner-exchange truce?” It asks whether Russia and Ukraine had officially reached a publicly announced and mutually agreed halt in military engagement that constituted a general pause in the conflict, excluding humanitarian pauses and limited arrangements. Plaintiff must make that qualifying characterization more likely than not.

The record does not get there. The most direct Ukrainian official source, D-1, is narrow. It is not an order for a theater-wide halt. It is a humanitarian/parade decree permitting the Moscow parade and excluding the Red Square coordinate box from Ukrainian weapons targeting during the parade. Plaintiff calls that additive, but the problem is evidentiary weight: the direct Ukrainian official act is limited, while the broader Ukrainian commitment is preserved only through secondary reporting.

The Russian official source in D-3 is also limiting. It frames the initiative as a ceasefire for the purpose of conducting a prisoner exchange and identifies the key agreement as exchanging 1,000 prisoners from each side during the ceasefire period. That is consistent with a temporary exchange-linked truce; it is not strong proof of a general pause in the conflict.

D-2 is the record’s best implementation evidence. It reports that Russian and Ukrainian forces continued limited offensive operations across the theater on the second day; that activity decreased but did not cease; that tactical combat persisted; that both sides made violation accusations; and that the arrangement lacked explicit enforcement, monitoring, and dispute-resolution mechanisms. Plaintiff says this is only breach evidence. But for this proposition, whether the arrangement constituted a general pause is the issue. Continued theater-wide operations and “activity did not cease” directly undercut that classification.

The source-chain gaps remain important. D-4 identifies missing direct captures of the Ukrainian social confirmation, the U.S. announcement, and fuller implementation records. PX-1 permits credible-media consensus, but it does not require the council to ignore the difference between broad secondary phrasing and narrower direct official acts.

The most balanced view is that the record proves a short, politically timed, Victory Day and prisoner-exchange arrangement, announced with broad diplomatic language and only partially observed. That is at least as likely as plaintiff’s qualifying “general pause” theory. Because plaintiff bears the preponderance burden, that tie or near-tie is a defense result. The proposition should be found not proven.
