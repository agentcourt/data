### Openings

#### Plaintiff Opening

Council members, the proposition is not asking whether the United States used harsh rhetoric toward Venezuela, or whether a short raid can be called an invasion in ordinary speech. It asks a narrower factual question: during the window from November 3, 2025 through January 31, 2026, did the United States commence a military offensive intended to establish control over any portion of Venezuelan territory?

The current record is empty. No case-packet evidence, source documents, exhibits, or submitted evidence are yet before you. For that reason, this opening is a roadmap, not a request to decide on assertions unsupported by admitted proof. The plaintiff’s burden is preponderance of the evidence, and the decisive proof will need to come from credible-source consensus, preferably including official U.S. statements, contemporaneous news reporting, and independent expert analysis.

Our theory is that this was an invasion under the proposition’s definition because the expected evidence will show an integrated U.S. military campaign, not an isolated arrest. The relevant facts will likely include the sequence of military buildup, maritime interdiction or blockade, strikes on or against Venezuelan territory, armed entry into Caracas or other Venezuelan sites, seizure and removal of the sitting effective ruler, temporary U.S. control over the places and routes needed to carry out that seizure, and follow-on statements or acts showing an intent to dictate Venezuela’s political transition or control Venezuelan resources. If that proof is admitted, it satisfies the proposition even if the United States did not permanently annex land or maintain a long occupation. The proposition requires an offensive intended to establish control over any portion of territory; temporary military control of a target area, base, airspace, port, route, or government seat can be enough if credible sources show it was part of the intended operation.

The defense’s best answer is predictable and important: it will say the United States conducted a brief law-enforcement or counter-narcotics operation, that any ground presence lasted only long enough to capture a criminal defendant, and that there was no intent to hold Venezuelan territory. That is the real dispute. A mere strike, a naval seizure outside Venezuela, or a short exfiltration with no intended territorial control would not, standing alone, meet the proposition. Nor would generalized political pressure or sanctions. The council should therefore focus on evidence of planned control: operational objectives, official descriptions of the forces and targets, whether U.S. forces controlled Venezuelan land or facilities even temporarily, whether the operation displaced Venezuela’s effective government, and whether U.S. officials claimed authority to administer the aftermath.

The plaintiff will seek to build the record with primary and near-primary sources: official White House, State, Defense, and Justice materials; transcripts of relevant press briefings and interviews; contemporaneous wire-service reporting; and independent legal or foreign-policy analysis that addresses the same facts. We will also test adverse material, especially any official claim that this was not an invasion and not an occupation. Those claims matter, but they are not conclusive if the underlying conduct and intent show a military offensive to establish control over Venezuelan territory.

At the end of the case, the question should be answered by the weight of credible sources, not labels. If the admitted record shows only a narrow arrest operation without territorial-control intent, plaintiff should lose. But if the admitted record shows that the United States used military force inside Venezuela as part of a planned campaign to seize control of places, routes, institutions, or resources necessary to impose a political outcome, then the proposition is more likely than not true.

#### Defendant Opening

Council members, the proposition is not whether the United States used force against Venezuela, whether officials used aggressive rhetoric, whether Congress debated war-powers limits, or whether an operation was lawful under domestic or international law. The proposition asks a narrower factual question: did the United States commence a military offensive, during the defined window, intended to establish control over any portion of Venezuelan territory?

The answer cannot be supplied by labels. “Hostilities,” “intervention,” “aggression,” “raid,” “capture,” “extraction,” and “regime pressure” may all matter as evidence leads, but none is the element the council must find. The plaintiff must prove by a preponderance, through a consensus of credible sources, both military action and the specific intent to establish territorial control. If the eventual record shows only strikes, maritime interdictions, sanctions enforcement, a law-enforcement capture operation, temporary tactical presence for entry and exit, or political pressure on Venezuela’s leadership, plaintiff has not met the proposition as written.

The current record contains no admitted evidence at all. There are no case-packet files, no exhibits, and no submitted source materials. The plaintiff’s opening properly recognizes that a mere strike or short exfiltration without intended territorial control would not be enough. That concession identifies the real dispute. The defense will hold plaintiff to it.

Our theory is that the decisive proof, if admitted, will show a counter-narcotics, interdiction, and extraction frame rather than an offensive to take and hold Venezuelan territory. The defense will not ask the council to ignore adverse evidence. If plaintiff later admits credible primary materials showing that U.S. forces set out to control ports, airfields, ministries, bases, roads, oil fields, neighborhoods, or other Venezuelan land as an object of the operation, that would be serious evidence for plaintiff. So would proof that the United States administered territory, installed a military authority over a place, maintained checkpoints for territorial governance, or claimed a continuing right to control Venezuelan facilities or resources.

But that is exactly the proof the plaintiff must produce. A military force can briefly control a doorway, route, landing zone, compound, airspace, or vessel as an incident of an arrest, strike, rescue, evacuation, or interdiction without intending to establish control over Venezuelan territory in the sense required by this proposition. Likewise, a government can seek a leader’s prosecution, weaken a hostile regime, interdict trafficking, or apply coercive pressure without crossing this proposition’s territorial-control line. The council should separate those categories carefully.

The coming evidence should therefore be tested around five questions. First, what was the actual operational objective, as shown by primary official materials, contemporaneous reporting, and reliable expert analysis? Second, what physical presence occurred inside land deemed Venezuelan territory as of September 6, 2025, and how long did it last? Third, did U.S. forces exercise control merely to complete a mission and leave, or did they seek to possess, administer, or deny Venezuela control of a place? Fourth, what did the United States do after the operation: withdraw, transfer custody, or govern territory? Fifth, do credible sources converge on territorial-control intent, or only on force, illegality, hostilities, or political consequences?

Provenance will matter. Official statements may understate conflict; hostile governments may overstate occupation; secondary commentary may collapse legal “aggression” into the different factual standard here. The most valuable evidence will be original government documents, full transcripts, congressional materials, after-action or oversight reports, and contemporaneous reporting that distinguishes what happened from what advocates called it.

At the close of this case, if the record shows the United States began an offensive meant to establish control over Venezuelan territory, the plaintiff should prevail. But if the record shows military force, even serious and controversial force, without that intended territorial-control objective, the proposition fails. On the present empty record, and under the plaintiff’s burden, the defense position is that the council should require that missing link before finding an invasion.

On this record, it is more likely than not that the United States commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over portions of Venezuela during the required period. The proposition should be found true.

The credible-source consensus is mixed: official sources say capture, law enforcement, counter-narcotics, no invasion, no occupation; secondary sources condemn the operation and infer broader control, but rely on thinly captured transition language and legal characterization.

The plaintiff bears the burden. Because the admitted record does not show that the United States intended to establish control over Venezuelan territory, as opposed to conducting a short capture/extraction and applying continuing non-territorial pressure, the council should find the proposition not proved.

### Rebuttals

#### Plaintiff Rebuttal

Council members, defendant’s argument narrows the proposition too far. It concedes the record proves a serious U.S. military operation in and against Venezuela: strikes, buildup, a Caracas raid, two-hour ground presence, capture/removal of Maduro and Flores, oil/tanker pressure, and political leverage. The remaining question is whether the object included control over any portion of Venezuela.

Defendant says plaintiff’s theory would make any brief commando raid an invasion. But this record is not about a doorway or landing zone. Defendant’s own DX-1 describes Operation Absolute Resolve as a U.S. military operation in Caracas against a heavily fortified military fortress in the heart of Venezuela’s capital. It followed months of planning by joint forces, special operations forces, and intelligence agencies. Gen. Caine described complex air, ground, space, and maritime operations. More than 150 military aircraft covered the ground extraction force. Hegseth called it a massive joint military and law-enforcement raid.

Those facts satisfy “any portion.” A planned armed domination of a fortified military site in Caracas, covered by 150 aircraft and integrated domains, is control over a portion of Venezuelan territory. It need not be permanent. The mission could not succeed unless U.S. forces intended to deny Venezuelan control over that site and surrounding operational environment long enough to seize and remove the effective ruler.

Defendant also separates regime-change pressure from territorial control. DX-1 defeats that separation. It reports that with Maduro out of power, Trump said the United States would oversee Venezuela until a safe transition could be identified; Rubio was working directly with Delcy Rodriguez; and Rodriguez was willing to do what the United States thought necessary. PX-5 independently captures that as U.S. administration of a transition under threat of more force. That is not merely prosecution in New York.

DX-2 does not rescue the defense. It frames Southern Spear as counter-narcotics and boat interdiction, but also admits major Navy/Marine deployments, lethal strikes, tanker interdictions, and a classified mission statement. It does not negate the specific intent of Operation Absolute Resolve in Caracas.

The defense imports an occupation requirement the proposition does not contain. The question is whether the United States commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over any portion of Venezuela, not whether it held checkpoints for weeks or annexed land. Rubio’s “not an invasion” label deserves consideration, but labels cannot outweigh the record facts: military force inside Venezuela, control of the necessary Caracas target area, removal of Maduro, and asserted authority to shape Venezuela’s transition and resources. That is enough under the actual proposition.

### Surrebuttals

#### Defendant Surrebuttal

Council members, plaintiff’s rebuttal usefully narrows the dispute to two points: the Caracas site and the transition language. Neither carries plaintiff’s burden.

First, plaintiff says the fortified military site in Caracas satisfies “any portion” because U.S. forces necessarily denied Venezuelan control long enough to seize and remove Maduro. That proves tactical control; it does not prove that the United States commenced an offensive intended to establish territorial control. Those are different things. A raid, arrest, rescue, or extraction at a defended facility always requires temporary domination of the target area. If that alone satisfies the proposition, the phrase “intended to establish control” does no work, and every cross-border commando raid becomes an invasion under this standard.

The record does not show the Caracas site was an object to be held, administered, possessed, governed, or transferred as territory. That is not the United States installing a military government over a place, holding facilities, drawing boundaries, manning checkpoints, or leaving forces in control after the raid.

Plaintiff says the defense imports an occupation requirement. No. The defense accepts that the proposition does not require annexation or a long occupation. But it does require an intent to establish control over territory. A short duration can qualify if the object is control of the place. Here the record shows a short duration whose object was capture and extraction. The admitted sources show force, pressure, and influence; they do not show a territorial-control objective.

DX-2 still matters for that reason. Plaintiff says it does not negate Operation Absolute Resolve. True, it does not decide the whole case. Plaintiff has not filled that classified gap with an operational order, full transcript, or post-operation facts showing that the Caracas raid changed into a territorial-control campaign.

The council can condemn the operation as unlawful, coercive, or interventionist and still find the proposition not proved. The question is not whether the United States used overwhelming force inside Venezuela. It did. The question is whether credible sources show, more likely than not, that the United States intended to establish control over Venezuelan territory. The record remains at most equivocal on that decisive element, so plaintiff has not carried the burden.

Plaintiff’s second strong point is transition language: DX-1 says the United States would oversee Venezuela until a safe transition could be identified, and PX-5 describes U.S. administration of a transition under threat of force. That evidence is troubling and deserves weight. But it remains ambiguous on the decisive issue. Political leverage over Delcy Rodriguez, pressure on resources, or demands about Venezuela’s policy direction are not the same as control over Venezuelan territory. The record does not show U.S. troops remained after the raid, that they held ministries, ports, bases, roads, neighborhoods, or oil fields, that they established checkpoints, that they drew controlled boundaries, or that they installed a military government over any place.

PX-1 likewise proves pressure, not territorial control. Custody of oil revenues in U.S. Treasury accounts and a blockade of sanctioned tankers may be coercive, even unlawful. But they are not possession or administration of Venezuelan land. PX-3 proves escalation and force, but its submitted extract does not provide the full content behind the linked “run Venezuela” item. PX-5 is credible expert commentary on international law, but it is not a primary operational order, full transcript, or after-action record proving that control of territory was the intended object.

The council can find that the United States violated Venezuelan sovereignty. It can find that the operation was aggressive, interventionist, coercive, and politically consequential. Those findings still do not answer the proposition unless the record also shows intended establishment of territorial control.

On that decisive element, the record is at best mixed. Plaintiff has powerful evidence of military force and temporary tactical domination. The defense has official evidence that the mission object was capture, extraction, law enforcement, and counter-narcotics, plus a record gap on any continuing territorial mechanism. Under a preponderance standard, ambiguity on the proposition’s central intent element means plaintiff has not carried its burden.

Find the proposition not proved.
