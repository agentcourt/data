### Openings

[plaintiff] Council members, the proposition is not about whether a prediction market, UMA, or any platform governance process reached the right result. It asks a factual question under the market rule: by May 24, 2026 at 11:59 PM ET, did Iran initiate a major non-weather closure of its airspace?

The current record contains one admitted case-packet item, ev_e61df84283cc_market-page. That packet proves the governing standard and the questions that will decide this case. A qualifying closure must be Iranian-initiated, not solely weather-related, and timely. It must also be major: a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region; or a non-weather complete suspension of commercial arrivals and departures affecting at least two listed airports: IKA, THR, MHD, SYZ, or IFN. The rule also draws two important lines. Limited cancellations, delays, or partial closures do not qualify. But limited exceptions to an otherwise broad closure, such as flights pre-approved by Iranian civil aviation authorities, do not defeat qualification. Third-party airline or foreign-government warnings are not enough.

That admitted packet does not yet prove the occurrence facts. It does not itself establish that an Iranian aviation authority issued a specific May 22-May 25 closure, which airports were covered, whether commercial arrivals and departures were suspended, whether overflights were barred, or whether any exceptions were limited enough to preserve a broad closure. Those facts must be proved with source material, not assumption.

Plaintiff’s theory is straightforward. The case will likely turn on whether the late-May Iranian aviation restrictions, if proved by official NOTAM or equivalent authority material and corroborated reporting, were a broad security-driven closure of a major Iranian airspace region rather than a mere localized delay or third-party avoidance. If the evidence shows Iran shut the western Tehran FIR or the airports within that region to ordinary commercial operations, while allowing only daylight operations or flights with fresh official approval, then the exception language matters: controlled permission for limited flights may be consistent with a qualifying broad closure.

The strongest opposing theory is also clear from the rule. Defendant will likely argue that the relevant May 24 condition failed because the restriction was only partial: some airports allegedly remained operational, some flights could proceed with approval, and the platform outcome for the May 24 market is listed as No. Those points deserve scrutiny, but none ends the factual inquiry by itself. The record standard is preponderance of the evidence, and the rule asks what Iran actually initiated, not what traders later priced or how a platform displayed its result.

The decisive proof should therefore be the source chain: the official Iranian aviation notice or NOTAM; its effective time, expiry time, affected FIR sector and airports; its treatment of commercial arrivals, departures, and transit; and credible reporting that confirms how the notice operated in practice. Equally important will be any contrary source showing that listed airports continued ordinary commercial arrivals and departures without a broad suspension, or that the restrictions were imposed by third parties rather than Iran.

At this opening stage, plaintiff asks the council to keep the dispute focused on those rule elements. If the evidence shows a broad, non-weather, Iranian-initiated regional closure by the deadline, limited official exceptions should not be allowed to turn a qualifying closure into a non-event. If the evidence instead shows only scattered delays or narrow localized restrictions, plaintiff will not have met the burden. The forthcoming record should decide that factual line.

[defendant] Council members, the plaintiff bears the burden to prove the rule’s factual condition, not merely to point to tension in the region, airline caution, or market chatter. The current admitted record is narrow. The only evidence item is ev_e61df84283cc_market-page, a case-packet text file with the market metadata, rule text, examples, and arbitration notes. It establishes the legal test. It does not establish that Iran actually initiated a qualifying closure by May 24, 2026 at 11:59 PM ET.

That distinction matters. The rule does not resolve “Yes” for any aviation disruption in Iran. It requires a major, Iranian-initiated, non-weather closure. The case packet defines a major closure as a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region, generally applying across Iran or such a region. It separately provides that a complete non-weather suspension of commercial arrivals and departures affecting at least two listed airports would qualify. But the same packet draws a limiting line: limited cancellations, delays, and other partial closures do not qualify. Third-party airline or foreign-government warnings are also insufficient.

Defendant’s opening theory is therefore straightforward: this case should turn on exact scope, not labels. Plaintiff must produce the official Iranian aviation source, or a reliably captured equivalent source chain, showing what was closed, when it became effective, when it expired, which airspace or airports were covered, and whether commercial transit, arrivals, and departures were actually suspended broadly enough to meet the rule. A reference to “western airspace,” “airport restrictions,” or “approval required” is not self-proving. It could describe a broad qualifying regional closure; it could also describe a partial, conditional, or airport-specific restriction that the rule expressly excludes.

The packet itself identifies the right source classes: official information from Iranian aviation authorities and a consensus of credible reporting. At this stage, none of that occurrence evidence is admitted. The record does not yet contain the NOTAM text, a Civil Aviation Organization notice, airport operational data, flight status records, or a complete chain showing that any reported restriction was official, timely, non-weather, and major within the rule’s terms.

Several factual questions will decide the case. First, did Iran itself initiate the restriction, as opposed to airlines, foreign regulators, or risk advisories reacting to conflict? Second, did the restriction bar ordinary commercial overflight, arrivals, and departures across Iran or a major Iranian airspace region, or did it leave substantial commercial operations available by daylight, route, airport, or per-flight approval? Third, if plaintiff uses the listed-airport pathway, were commercial arrivals and departures completely suspended at at least two of IKA, THR, MHD, SYZ, and IFN? Fourth, were any exceptions genuinely limited exceptions to a broad closure, or were they broad operating permissions that make the event partial rather than major?

Defendant does not ask the council to decide now that no closure happened. The defendant asks the council to hold plaintiff to the rule. If later admitted evidence contains an official, authenticated notice proving a broad regional shutdown with only narrow exceptions, plaintiff may have a serious argument under the broad-region language. But if the evidence shows only partial airport closures, daylight operations, approval-based continued service, airline avoidance, or ambiguous secondary reporting without the official source text, the proposition should fail.

The current record proves the standard and the burden. It does not prove the event. Until plaintiff supplies the source chain and operational facts, the preponderance remains against finding that the May 24 market condition was satisfied.

### Arguments

[plaintiff] Council members, the preponderance now favors the proposition.

PX-1 supplies the rule. It asks whether Iran initiated a major non-weather closure by May 24, 2026 at 11:59 PM ET. A major closure includes a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. Limited partial closures do not qualify, but limited exceptions to a broad closure do not defeat qualification.

PX-2 and PX-3 supply the occurrence facts. PX-2 reports that Iran temporarily closed the airspace over its western half from May 22 to May 25, suspending all civil flight permits in the western sector of the Tehran Flight Information Region, OIIX. It identifies the official act as Iranian Civil Aviation Authority NOTAM OIIX A1010/26, issued Friday, May 22, effective immediately and valid until May 25, ordering shutdown of all airports in the western part of the Tehran FIR. PX-2 further reports that only eight airports across the country remained operational, and even those were restricted to daylight hours with fresh per-flight approval from the Civil Aviation Authority. PX-3 corroborates the same core facts: NOTAM OIIX A1010/26 announced closure of airports across the western section of the Tehran FIR, only a limited number of facilities could operate in daylight, and airlines needed fresh Iranian CAA approval before passenger flights in affected areas.

Those facts satisfy each element. First, initiation: both reports attribute the restriction to Iran's aviation authority through a specific NOTAM, not to airlines, foreign regulators, or market commentary. Second, timing: the reported NOTAM began May 22 and remained valid until May 25, so it was in force before the May 24 deadline. Third, non-weather cause: the reports place the measure in a security and military-diplomatic context, not weather. Fourth, magnitude: western Tehran FIR is a major Iranian airspace region, and the reported measure was not a scattered airport delay. It suspended civil flight permits in the western OIIX sector and shut all airports in the western part of that FIR.

Defendant's best point is that some airports remained operational under daylight and fresh-approval limits. But that is not fatal under PX-1. The rule expressly says limited exceptions to a broad closure do not disqualify the closure. A system in which ordinary civil flight permits in the western sector are suspended, airports in the western FIR are shut, and the remaining national operations require daylight timing and fresh official approval is not normal commercial availability. It is a broad Iranian-controlled suspension with limited permissions.

Plaintiff does not need to prove the separate listed-airport pathway if the broad-major-region pathway is met. And that matters because PX-2 says several listed airports were among the eight still operating under restrictions. The better reading is therefore not that every listed airport was completely shut; it is that Iran closed a major airspace region and permitted only constrained exceptions elsewhere. That is exactly the distinction PX-1 preserves.

The proof is not perfect. The official NOTAM text itself is not in the record, and PX-2 appears to report the NOTAM detail through Gulf News. That limits weight. But preponderance does not require perfect custody where two admitted reports identify the same official notice number, same authority, same dates, same FIR, same closure of western-region airports, and same approval/daylight restrictions. No admitted contrary source shows the NOTAM was fake, weather-based, third-party-imposed, or merely a few isolated cancellations.

On this record, the more likely factual conclusion is that by May 24, Iran had initiated a non-weather broad closure of a major Iranian airspace region within the market rule. The council should find the proposition true.

[defendant] Council members, the full argument record still does not prove the proposition by a preponderance.

PX-1 is the controlling rule. It requires more than a disruption: Iran must initiate a non-weather “major closure” by the deadline. A major closure is a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. The rule also has a separate listed-airport path: complete suspension of commercial arrivals and departures at at least two named airports. It expressly excludes limited cancellations, delays, and other partial closures.

Plaintiff’s PX-2 and PX-3 are secondary reports, not the official NOTAM. They help identify a lead, but their own contents show why the lead is not enough. PX-2 says eight airports remained operational, including several listed in PX-1, and that those airports could operate in daylight with fresh per-flight CAA approval. PX-3 likewise describes closure of “airports” across the western section of the Tehran FIR, with a limited number of facilities permitted to operate during daylight and fresh CAA approval for passenger flights in affected areas. That is not proof of a complete suspension at two listed airports; it is the opposite.

DX-1, the Notamify capture of the raw NOTAM text, is the most specific evidence now in the record. It narrows, rather than expands, plaintiff’s case. A1010/26 is categorized as AD Airport Operations and says: all airports in the west part of Tehran FIR are closed except specified airports that are operational from sunrise to sunset, with new permission required for civil IFR passenger flights. It does not say the Tehran FIR is closed to overflights. It does not identify ATS-route closures. It does not say commercial flights transiting Iranian airspace are suspended. It is an airport-operations NOTAM with named operating exceptions.

DX-1 also shows that before the deadline A1015/26 replaced A1010/26 and expanded the exceptions. The replacement listed OITR, OIKK, OIAA, OISS, OIYY, OICC, OIGG, OIAW, OIFM, OITL, OISR, and OIAM as operational from sunrise to sunset, again with permission required for civil IFR passenger flights. OISS is Shiraz and OIFM is Isfahan; PX-2 also lists IKA, Mehrabad, Mashhad, Shiraz, and Isfahan among airports that remained operational in some form. On the listed-airport pathway, plaintiff has not shown complete suspension of commercial arrivals and departures at two named airports.

Nor has plaintiff proved the broad-region pathway. The rule’s phrase is not merely “some airports in a region were restricted.” It requires broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. The admitted raw text speaks in terms of airports in the west part of the FIR, and then carves out numerous operating airports. It permits civil IFR passenger flights with new CAA permission. Plaintiff asks the council to treat that as a broad regional airspace closure with limited exceptions, but the record equally, and more precisely, supports a partial airport restriction with substantial conditional operations.

DX-2 adds a provenance and conflict problem. Iran International reported that Iran’s Civil Aviation Organization denied a new official aviation notice imposing restrictions on the country’s airspace and said flights were operating according to schedule, while also acknowledging an earlier western-Tehran-FIR airport activity suspension with exemptions. That is not a clean defense win, because it confirms some airport restrictions. But it defeats plaintiff’s attempt to turn two article summaries into a settled official source chain proving a countrywide or major-region airspace closure.

The source chain remains weak. No official Iranian CAA page, ICAO bulletin, original NOTAM feed record, airport movement record, or flight-data exhibit is admitted. PX-2 even attributes the key official-NOTAM claim to Gulf News rather than reproducing the underlying source. DX-1 is an aggregator capture, useful for text, but even that text supports conditional airport operations and does not prove overflight or route closure.

The plaintiff’s best point is the rule’s “limited exceptions” clause. But exceptions are limited only against an otherwise broad closure. The council cannot assume the predicate. Here the admitted evidence shows named airports operational, permissions available for civil IFR passenger flights, an expanded exception list before the deadline, and no admitted proof that commercial transit through a major airspace region was broadly suspended.

On this record, the proposition is not more likely true than false. The council should find that plaintiff proved a partial, conditional western-airport restriction, not a major non-weather closure of Iranian airspace within the market rule.

### Rebuttals

[plaintiff] Council members, defendant’s new evidence does not defeat plaintiff’s case; it supplies the specificity defendant said was missing.

DX-1 is now the most exact source in the record. Its raw A1010/26 text says: all airports in the west part of Tehran FIR are closed except listed airports operating from sunrise to sunset; all previous permissions are suspended for all operators; new permission must be requested from the CAA for operation of civil IFR passenger flights on those airports. A1015/26 replaces A1010/26 and repeats the same structure: all airports in the west part of Tehran FIR are closed except a listed set, all previous permissions are suspended for all operators, and new permission must be requested from Iran CAA for civil IFR passenger flights.

That is not ordinary commercial operation. It is a general closure in a defined major Iranian aviation region, with named exceptions and an official permission regime. PX-1 expressly anticipates this situation: limited exceptions to a broad closure, including flights pre-approved by Iranian civil aviation authorities, do not disqualify a closure.

Defendant tries to make overflight-route closure the decisive test because DX-1 is categorized as airport operations and does not list ATS-route closures. But PX-1 does not require every route, altitude, or overflight track to be separately closed. It requires a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. The west part of the Tehran FIR is a major Iranian airspace region. Closing all airports in that west part, suspending all previous permissions for all operators, and requiring fresh CAA permission for civil IFR passenger flights is a broad suspension of commercial aviation in that region, especially as to arrivals and departures.

Defendant is right that plaintiff should not lean on the separate listed-airport complete-suspension pathway. The stronger and sufficient pathway is the broad-major-region clause. The presence of exceptions, and even A1015’s expanded exception list, does not convert the measure into a few scattered delays. The operative rule distinguishes partial closures from broad closures with limited exceptions. DX-1’s phrase “all airports in west part of Tehran FIR are closed except...” is the language of a broad closure with exceptions.

DX-2 is also narrower than defendant suggests. It denies a new official notice imposing countrywide airspace restrictions and refers to a social-media notice. But the proposition does not require countrywide restrictions. More importantly, DX-2 acknowledges that Iran’s Civil Aviation Organization had said Friday that activity at airports in the western part of the Tehran FIR had been suspended until Monday, with only a limited number of airports allowed to operate sunrise-to-sunset. That confirms the core event; it only disputes an overbroad nationwide version.

The final factual line is therefore clear. If the rule required total nationwide shutdown or complete closure of two listed airports, defendant would have a stronger case. It does not. It allows a major-region closure and preserves limited official exceptions. On the admitted raw NOTAM text, Iran initiated a non-weather closure of the west part of the Tehran FIR by the deadline, suspended prior permissions for all operators, and allowed only constrained CAA-approved passenger operations. That meets the rule by a preponderance.

PX-1 is demanding in two ways. That leaves only the broad-region pathway. Plaintiff’s best evidence is DX-1, the raw Notamify text. Defendant agrees it carries the most weight. But read it carefully. A1010/26 and A1015/26 are airport-operations notices. They say airports in the west part of the Tehran FIR are closed except named airports operating sunrise-to-sunset; prior permissions are suspended; new CAA permission is required for civil IFR passenger flights. They do not say the western Tehran FIR is closed. They do not close overflight routes. They do not identify airways, altitude blocks, or an FIR-wide suspension. Plaintiff asks the council to bridge that gap by inference: airport closures plus permission requirements should be treated as a broad regional airspace closure with limited exceptions. DX-2 reinforces caution. The evidentiary gaps also matter. PX-2 and PX-3 are secondary summaries; DX-1 is an aggregator capture. The council can credit them for the airport restriction they show, but should not inflate them into proof of facts they do not contain.

Preponderance means more likely than not. The record makes it more likely that Iran imposed a significant, non-weather, permission-based restriction on western Iranian airport operations. It does not make it more likely that Iran initiated a major closure of its airspace or a major airspace region as PX-1 defines that term. The proposition should be found not proven.
