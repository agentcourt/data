### Openings

[plaintiff] Council members, this case is about a narrow factual proposition, not whether Polymarket's early resolution process was ideal: between April 20, 2026 and December 31, 2026 at 11:59 PM ET, did Clavicular make a credible announcement that he and a partner were expecting a baby through pregnancy?

The current packet already establishes the governing rule and the real dispute. The Polymarket market-page record preserves the operative language: the announcement must be by Clavicular, must concern him and a partner expecting a baby through pregnancy, must occur in the stated window, and must be credible; jokes or other non-credible pregnancy announcements do not qualify. That same official-source material records the market as finally resolved Yes after proposal and dispute rounds. The final market state is not conclusive by itself, but it is important context: the live oracle process reached the same factual side plaintiff advances.

The strongest current plaintiff evidence is the secondary reporting packet. Forbes described the disputed market as turning on what counts as a credible pregnancy announcement from a streamer who told his audience he had gotten a girl pregnant. That description, if supported by the underlying statement or by a definitive consensus of credible media reporting, matches the rule's core: an announcement from Clavicular concerning a pregnancy with a partner. The market rule expressly allows credible-media consensus as a possible resolution source, so the case does not fail merely because a council later receives the proof through media reporting rather than a preserved livestream file.

But the packet is candid about what it does not yet prove. It does not contain the original livestream clip, VOD, transcript, representative statement, or complete source chain. It also contains adverse reporting: Times of India says no video or Clavicular statement confirmed a pregnancy, and other reports frame the available remarks as a controversial pregnancy-contest proposal or shock-content performance. Those materials matter because the rule excludes jokes and non-credible claims. A statement like wanting children soon, standing alone, would not satisfy the proposition; neither would a rumor about a girlfriend if Clavicular did not credibly announce it.

So the decisive question is not whether there was internet chatter about Clavicular and pregnancy. There plainly was. The decisive question is whether the best source material shows a credible announcement of an existing expected baby through pregnancy, rather than a hypothetical contest, satire, or unconfirmed rumor. Plaintiff's burden is only preponderance. The final Yes resolution, the official rule allowing credible media consensus, and Forbes's direct characterization give plaintiff a concrete path to meeting that burden. The defense path is also visible: show that the only preserved statements were jokes, hypotheticals, or reports explicitly denying confirmation.

In the evidence phases, the council should expect the case to turn on source-chain proof: the original stream or clip if it can be preserved; the Forbes article and its cited sources; the platform clarification Forbes references; the Domahhhh thread and related X posts only to the extent they reveal source material rather than trader opinion; and the adverse fact-checks or later clarifications. If that source chain confirms that Clavicular told his audience he had gotten a partner/girlfriend pregnant, plaintiff should prevail. If it shows only a pregnancy-contest stunt or unsupported rumor, plaintiff should not. On the current packet, plaintiff's theory is that the Yes resolution and Forbes reporting point to a qualifying announcement, and the remaining proceedings should test that source chain rather than treat the missing original clip as the end of the inquiry.

[defendant] Council members, the defense position is simple: this market did not ask whether Polymarket eventually paid Yes, whether traders argued about Clavicular, or whether the internet repeated a pregnancy rumor. It asked whether Clavicular made a credible announcement that he and a partner were expecting a baby through pregnancy. On the current admitted record, that decisive fact remains unproved.

The market-page record is important, but it helps define the burden more than it satisfies it. It preserves the rule: a qualifying announcement must be credible; jokes and non-credible pregnancy announcements do not suffice; and the source should be statements from Clavicular or representatives, with a definitive consensus of credible media reporting only as a possible substitute. That same file records a final Yes resolution after proposal and dispute rounds. The defense does not ignore that. But a final platform state is not the announcement itself, and the case packet does not preserve the source chain that would let the council test whether the final state tracked the rule.

The packet's own primary-evidence file makes that distinction explicit. The preserved primary/platform material proves the contract wording and the platform's final Yes state. It does not itself prove that a qualifying credible announcement occurred. The official-source record likewise says no primary statement from Clavicular or a representative has been preserved in the packet. That is not a technical quibble. It is the core missing link, because the difference between a qualifying announcement and a non-qualifying stunt depends on exact words, context, speaker, timing, and credibility.

The secondary reporting is conflicted. Forbes is the strongest current support for plaintiff because it characterizes the dispute as involving a streamer who told his audience he had gotten a girl pregnant. If a reliable source chain ultimately confirms that statement, in context, as a serious announcement about Clavicular and a partner expecting a baby through pregnancy, plaintiff may have a case. A statement of aspiration, a proposed contest, a joke, a viral inference, or a trader's interpretation is not enough. Before this opening, defense reviewed the packet and ran targeted source searches to identify what should be tested in the evidence phase. The same gaps remain the decisive ones: the original livestream clip or VOD, a transcript or faithful excerpt, the Forbes source chain, the referenced Polymarket clarification, the Domahhhh thread and any embedded source material, and the UMA/proposer/disputer records if they explain what evidence the oracle actually relied on. Opening rules do not allow us to admit those materials now, but the council should understand why they matter.

So the defense opening is not that plaintiff can never prove the proposition. It is that the admitted record presently does not carry plaintiff's burden. It proves a market rule, a final Yes outcome, and a noisy dispute. It also proves missing primary evidence, unresolved source-chain problems, and adverse reporting that frames the relevant statements as non-confirming or non-credible. In the evidence phases, plaintiff should be required to produce the actual statement or a reliable captured chain showing a credible announcement of an existing partner pregnancy. If the record remains where it is now, the proposition should be rejected.

First, the governing rule is undisputed. The market-page and official-source records say this market resolves Yes if Clavicular announces that he and a partner are expecting a baby through pregnancy in the window; jokes and non-credible announcements do not count; statements from Clavicular or representatives are the primary resolution source, but a definitive consensus of credible media reporting may be considered. On a preponderance standard, the proposition should be found true.

### Rebuttals

[plaintiff] Council members, the defense is right about one thing but draws the wrong conclusion. If the only record were the “pregnancy contest” remarks, plaintiff would not have carried the burden. But the record is not limited to those remarks, and the defense’s new articles do not rebut the separate Forbes-reported pregnancy statement.

The defense treats all sources as describing the same event. They do not. Times of India, Hindustan Times, and The Express Tribune focus on a backlash story: Clavicular talked about wanting children, a contest involving many women, and becoming a dad in the next few months. Those reports are useful adverse context, and plaintiff admitted TOI for that reason. But they show that one category of public remarks may be non-qualifying; they do not prove there was no other qualifying statement. The Forbes packet and Forbes extraction address the exact Polymarket/UMA dispute and preserve the key characterization that the controversy involved a streamer who told his audience he had gotten a girl pregnant. That is materially different from “I want children” or a proposed contest.

The defense also overstates the effect of the missing VOD. The rule does not require the council to possess the original stream or a representative statement in every case. It expressly says a definitive consensus of credible media reporting may be considered. That matters because markets like this are often resolved from public reporting where original livestreams, clips, or deleted posts are not cleanly preserved. The missing VOD reduces confidence; it does not make credible media evidence legally irrelevant.

Nor does the Forbes adverse fragment prove No. “Market description and platform clarification say No; price says Yes; oracle...has not closed the gap” shows a dispute existed. It does not establish the platform clarification was correct, and it predates or describes the very conflict that ultimately ended in a final Yes state. The Gamma API shows the market resolved Yes with outcome prices Yes 1 / No 0 after proposal and dispute rounds. That is not just “price”; it is primary platform evidence of final resolution under this rule.

The defense says the fuller-context articles “preserve actual reported words.” They preserve actual words for the contest/future-fatherhood remarks, not for the Forbes-described “gotten a girl pregnant” statement. That distinction is the whole case. The council should not let adverse evidence about a non-qualifying category swallow separate market-specific evidence about a qualifying category.

So the correct weighing is this: plaintiff has imperfect but market-specific evidence of a direct pregnancy announcement, supported by Forbes and final platform resolution; defense has credible evidence that related viral contest/future-fatherhood clips were non-confirming. Under preponderance, the question is which account more likely explains the final Yes market-specific dispute. Plaintiff’s account still does: Forbes’s direct pregnancy characterization and the resolved Gamma record are more probative of the actual Polymarket proposition than later backlash articles about an adjacent contest controversy. The proposition should be found true.

### Closings

[plaintiff] Council members, the proposition should be found true on a preponderance of the evidence.

The question is narrow: did Clavicular make a credible announcement that he and a partner were expecting a baby through pregnancy during the window? The rule itself supplies the key evidentiary standard: statements from Clavicular or representatives are the resolution source, but a definitive consensus of credible media reporting may be considered. That language matters. It means the council should not demand perfect preservation of the original livestream as the only possible proof. The missing VOD is important; it is not automatically fatal.

The strongest evidence for plaintiff is market-specific. The official market record and Gamma API establish the exact rule, the creation window, and the final Yes resolution after proposal and dispute rounds. The Forbes packet and admitted Forbes extraction then supply the factual link: Forbes covered this exact Polymarket/UMA dispute and described it as turning on a streamer who told his audience he had gotten a girl pregnant. That statement, if credited, satisfies the proposition in ordinary language. It is not merely future aspiration, not merely “I want children,” and not merely a contest proposal.

The defense evidence deserves weight, but it answers a different question. Times of India, Hindustan Times, and The Express Tribune show that one set of public remarks involved a pregnancy contest, wanting children, and becoming a dad in the next few months. Plaintiff has never asked you to treat those remarks alone as sufficient. They are adverse because they show why this market was disputed and why “credible” matters. But they do not disprove the separate market-specific report that Clavicular told his audience he had gotten a girl pregnant.

The defense’s closing theme is absence: no original stream, no full transcript, no full clean Forbes body, no Polymarket clarification, no UMA records. Those gaps are real. But preponderance asks what is more likely on this record, not whether every ideal source was preserved. The record contains one source tied directly to the market dispute and the asserted direct pregnancy claim: Forbes. It also contains primary platform evidence that the market ultimately resolved Yes under a rule that excluded jokes and non-credible announcements. Against that, the defense has credible evidence about adjacent non-confirming contest remarks and a fact-check of viral girlfriend-pregnancy rumors, but no primary denial, no preserved platform clarification, and no source proving that Forbes’s “gotten a girl pregnant” account was merely the same contest remark.

That is the decisive distinction. If the council collapses the Forbes-reported pregnancy statement into the contest remarks, defendant wins. But the admitted record does not require that collapse. It shows conflicting public narratives: a non-qualifying contest/backlash narrative, and a market-specific qualifying-statement narrative. Because the Polymarket rule permits credible media reporting, because Forbes addressed the exact market dispute, and because the final official platform record resolved Yes after disputes, the qualifying-statement narrative is more likely than not.

So plaintiff’s position is candid and limited: the case is not perfect; the missing original clip reduces confidence; the adverse sources prevent certainty. But the standard is preponderance. The better reading of the record is that Clavicular made a public, market-moving announcement that he had gotten a girl/partner pregnant, and that the subsequent dispute arose because others characterized adjacent remarks as jokes, contest talk, or rumor. On this record, the Yes side has the greater weight. The council should find the proposition true.

[defendant] Council members, the proposition should be found false because plaintiff has not proved the required announcement by a preponderance.

The rule is precise. It requires a credible announcement that Clavicular and a partner are expecting a baby through pregnancy. It excludes jokes and non-credible pregnancy announcements. It permits statements from Clavicular or representatives, and may consider a definitive consensus of credible media reporting. The record must therefore prove more than a market price, a final resolution, or internet controversy. It must prove the qualifying announcement.

The record does not do that. The primary/platform evidence proves the market wording and final Yes state. It does not prove the underlying factual predicate. The case-packet primary-evidence file says exactly that. The unresolved-gaps file identifies the missing materials that would decide the case: original livestream clip, VOD, transcript, representative statement, Polymarket clarification, and UMA records. Those gaps are not cosmetic. This case turns on exact words and context because “credible” is the disputed term.

Plaintiff’s strongest point is Forbes. If the record contained a full article with a source chain, or if several credible sources converged on the same direct Clavicular statement, plaintiff might meet the burden despite the missing VOD. But the admitted Forbes item is a partial extraction from a metered page. Its own limitation says it preserves snippets from a serialized payload, not a full clean article body and not the original stream, VOD, or transcript. The favorable fragment, “gotten a girl pregnant,” is not enough without context to decide whether Forbes was reporting a confirmed statement, summarizing a disputed rumor, or describing a joke or non-credible claim. The same Forbes exhibit also preserves an adverse point: the market description and platform clarification said No while price said Yes.

The Gamma API likewise cannot carry the burden. It proves final Yes and market metadata. Its narrative context actually highlights why the matter was not a clean pregnancy announcement: public vows to become a father, a fertility-stack reveal, a dating-show or baby-mama contest pitch, viral backlash, and debunked girlfriend-pregnancy rumors. The fuller admitted reporting points the other way. Hindustan Times and The Express Tribune preserve the actual reported words: wanting children, getting children today, becoming a dad in the next few months, and a contest involving hundreds of women. Plaintiff’s closing asks you not to collapse the Forbes-reported statement into the contest remarks. But the problem is that the separate Forbes-reported statement is not actually preserved. Plaintiff bears the burden to prove it, not merely to posit it. The media-consensus clause does not mean one incomplete fragment defeats three adverse source captures and the packet’s own source-chain warnings. A “definitive consensus” is absent; the admitted media conflict is the record.

So the decisive weighing is this: on plaintiff’s side, final platform Yes plus an incomplete Forbes fragment. On defense’s side, admitted primary-source gaps, no preserved Clavicular statement, no preserved transcript, an adverse platform-clarification fragment, a fact check finding no confirmation, and two additional reports tying the actual quoted remarks to a contest or future-fatherhood stunt. That is not 50.1% for plaintiff.

The council should decide the proposition on the evidence actually admitted. That evidence shows controversy and an early Yes resolution, but it does not show a credible announcement that Clavicular and a partner were expecting a baby through pregnancy. Find the proposition false.
