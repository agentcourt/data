Title: A judge unsealed Epstein's purported 2019 suicide note. More documents could follow. NPR. By Rachel Treisman. Page metadata datePublished: 2026-05-07T14:56:15-04:00.

Relevant captured text:
- NPR metadata description: "At the request of the New York Times, a judge unsealed a goodbye note that Jeffrey Epstein's former cellmate says he found after the convicted sex offender's first suicide attempt in July 2019."
- Article lead: "A suicide note purportedly written by Jeffrey Epstein in prison has been publicly released, nearly seven years after Epstein's former cellmate said he found it."
- NPR reports: "District Judge Kenneth Karas unsealed the one-page note Wednesday in response to a legal petition from the New York Times, which reported on its existence last week."
- NPR caveat: "NPR has not independently verified the authenticity of the note."
- NPR reports the note read in part: "It is a treat to be able to choose one's time to say goodbye. Watcha want me to do -- Bust out cryin!! NO FUN -- NOT WORTH IT!!"
- NPR reports Nicholas Tartaglione said he discovered the note while sharing a cell with Epstein at the Metropolitan Correctional Center, where they overlapped for about two weeks in July 2019.
- NPR page image alt text identifies "A copy of a suicide note purportedly written by Jeffrey Epstein in 2019," and a caption states a judge unsealed a note purportedly written by Epstein after a suspected suicide attempt in July 2019, a month before he was found dead.
- NPR quotes or paraphrases attorney Bruce Barket as saying he believes the note genuine but did not officially authenticate it. Captured quote: "We didn't ever authenticate it [with] any real handwriting analysis or something like that," while saying surrounding circumstances, Tartaglione's account, and similar writing led them to be comfortable with Epstein authorship.
- NPR reports DOJ told NPR by email that the note had not yet been authenticated and that this was the first time DOJ was seeing it. It also reports DOJ lawyers did not object to release and disclaimed knowledge of the accuracy of Tartaglione's factual narrative.
- NPR reports Judge Karas gave parties one week to propose redactions to other filings and that Barket said those documents could answer more questions about the note.

Limits/caveats: NPR did not authenticate the note and reports DOJ had not authenticated it; the probative value is credible public reporting of public release and the character of the reported note, with explicit adverse authentication limits.
