Material extracted passages:

At the start of the order, the court identifies the New York Times request and the item as an alleged, purported note rather than an authenticated finding: "Non-Party newspaper, the New York Times (the 'Times'), has filed a request to unseal certain materials that, the Times claims, were filed in this case." The order states that the Times' request to unseal an alleged "suicide note purportedly authored by Jeffrey Epstein, recovered by . . . Tartaglione, and then reportedly submitted by . . . Tartaglione's attorneys in this case (the 'Note')" should be granted.

Background on the source chain: while Tartaglione was awaiting trial he was briefly cellmates with Jeffrey Epstein at the Metropolitan Correctional Center in Manhattan. The Times sought four sets of documents, first including "A suicide note purportedly authored by Jeffrey Epstein, recovered by Mr. Tartaglione, and then reportedly submitted by Mr. Tartaglione's attorneys in this case (the 'Note')."

The order explains that, according to the Times, Tartaglione repeatedly referenced the Note in detail in interviews with the Times and another outlet and that a Department of Justice document referenced the Note. According to that document, the Note predated Epstein's death by over a week and predated Epstein being placed in a different cell from Tartaglione.

Party response context: the Government's letter indicated it was unaware of the subject matter of the Curcio proceedings beyond what appeared publicly on the docket. Susan Wolfe, serving as co-counsel for Tartaglione, submitted a sealed letter; according to the order, Tartaglione took the position that information he had shared about the Note with third parties was no longer privileged and he did not object to the Note being unsealed.

Right-of-access reasoning: the court discussed common law and First Amendment access principles, concluding that public access to the Note promotes accountability and public confidence in the administration of justice. The court also considered third-party privacy interests and concluded they did not justify continued sealing, including because Epstein was deceased and because Tartaglione had already repeatedly discussed the Note and its asserted contents in a national news outlet.

Disposition: "For the reasons set forth above, the Times' request is granted as to the Note. The Clerk of Court is respectfully directed to docket a copy of the material that was placed in the vault on May 20, 2021, and to unseal the original material placed in the vault on May 20, 2021. The Court reserves decision as to the remainder of the materials requested by the Times."

Limitations relevant to the arbitration: this order establishes the official court unsealing/docketing action and describes the requested item using alleged/purported/reported language. The order is about public access and sealing; it does not state that the court authenticated the handwriting, resolved chain of custody, found that Epstein wrote the note, or determined that the note was intended as a suicide note or final communication.
