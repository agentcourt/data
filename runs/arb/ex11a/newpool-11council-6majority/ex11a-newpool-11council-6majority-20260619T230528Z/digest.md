## Filings

### Openings

[plaintiff] Council members, the proposition asks a narrow factual question: by May 24, 2026, 11:59 PM ET, did Iran itself initiate a major non-weather closure of its airspace within this market's rule?

The current case packet fixes the governing rule and the burden. It is not enough to show ordinary delays, isolated cancellations, foreign-government warnings, airline avoidance, or weather disruption. But the rule also does not require a hermetically sealed national sky. It expressly counts a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. It also separately counts a non-weather complete suspension of commercial arrivals and departures affecting at least two listed airports: IKA, THR, MHD, SYZ, or IFN. Limited exceptions to a broad closure do not defeat qualification.

Our theory is that this case will turn on source-chain proof of an Iranian aviation action, not market commentary. The decisive proof should be an official Iranian aviation notice, NOTAM, airport-company or civil-aviation statement, and then credible reporting or operational records confirming its scope and timing. If that evidence shows an Iran-issued closure or suspension in force on or before May 24, for security or conflict-related reasons rather than weather, and shows either a major Iranian region or at least two listed airports were covered for commercial arrivals and departures, the proposition is satisfied by a preponderance of the evidence.

The packet itself already helps separate the real dispute from distractions. First, the relevant actor is Iran. Restrictions imposed by other countries or voluntary airline rerouting may corroborate risk, but they cannot by themselves win the case. Second, the relevant scope is commercial aviation across Iran, a major Iranian airspace region, or at least two specified airports; a local drill area or VFR-only restriction would not be enough. Third, the relevant time is May 24, 2026, 11:59 PM ET, not a later platform-resolution step. Fourth, the primary source hierarchy favors official Iranian aviation material and a consensus of credible reporting.

What the current record does not yet prove is equally important: it does not, standing alone, establish the exact closure notice, affected FIR sector, affected airports, hours of effect, exceptions, or whether arrivals and departures were completely suspended at the qualifying airports. Those are the facts the plaintiff will need to put into the record when evidence admission opens.

The expected defense will say any May 24 disruption was partial, regionally limited, or merely foreign avoidance. That defense fails if the admitted source record shows an Iran-issued NOTAM or equivalent official action closing a major part of the Tehran FIR or suspending commercial operations at qualifying airports through May 24. Conversely, if later evidence shows only warnings, limited route restrictions, weather, VFR/general-aviation limits, or isolated airport cancellations, then the plaintiff should not prevail.

So the council should evaluate this case through the rule's own filters: source, timing, non-weather cause, and operational breadth. The plaintiff will seek to prove those elements with admissible source material, and not by asking the council to infer a major closure from market price, rumor, or unrelated regional disruption.

[defendant] Council members, the defendant’s opening is simple: the proposition is not satisfied by a mood of regional danger, by foreign warnings, by rerouted flights, or by a headline saying “closure.” The plaintiff must prove, with admitted evidence, that Iran itself initiated a qualifying major non-weather closure by May 24, 2026, 11:59 PM ET.

The only evidence now in the record is the market-page case packet. That packet helps the council because it draws several hard lines. It requires an Iran-initiated closure. It excludes weather-only events. It excludes limited cancellations, delays, and other partial closures. It also excludes restrictions imposed by airlines or countries other than Iran. And it identifies the strongest source class: official information from Iranian aviation authorities, supported where appropriate by a consensus of credible reporting.

The current record does not yet prove the operative event. It gives the rule, the deadline, the examples, and the source hierarchy, but it does not establish an official closure notice, a NOTAM text, a predecessor or replacement NOTAM chain, the issuing authority, the affected geography, the affected airports, the hours of effect, or the commercial-flight consequences. It also does not establish whether any exception, daylight operation window, or permission process left enough commercial operations available to make the event non-qualifying under the rule.

My investigation before this opening indicates that the later fight will likely center on an alleged Tehran FIR NOTAM around May 22 to May 25. But that is exactly where the council should be careful. A NOTAM aggregator, a social-media image, or a secondary article may be useful as a lead, but it is not the same thing as official Iranian aviation proof. If the plaintiff relies on that kind of material later, the council should ask for the source chain: who issued it, whether it was active through the market deadline after converting times correctly, whether it was superseded, and whether the text is complete rather than excerpted or paraphrased.

Scope will matter just as much as provenance. A closure of “all airports in the west part of the Tehran FIR,” if that is what the evidence eventually says, is not automatically the same thing as a broad closure of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. The packet’s own rule distinguishes qualifying broad closures from partial closures. The council will need exact evidence showing whether the affected western sector was a major Iranian airspace region for commercial aviation, whether transit was actually closed, and whether any listed exceptions were narrow enough not to defeat a broad closure theory.

The alternative airport path also needs proof, not assumption. The packet says a complete non-weather suspension of commercial arrivals and departures at at least two listed airports can qualify. Those listed airports are specific: IKA, THR, MHD, SYZ, and IFN. The plaintiff must show complete commercial arrival-and-departure suspension at at least two of them, not merely restrictions at other airports, reduced schedules, daylight-only operations, or a requirement for new permission.

So the defense theory is not that nothing happened in Iranian aviation. It is that this market has a particular rule, and that rule demands more than disruption. The plaintiff must connect an official Iranian action to the required deadline, non-weather cause, commercial scope, and qualifying breadth. If admitted evidence later supplies that chain, the council can weigh it. If the record remains a collection of secondary reports, foreign advisories, aggregator text, or ambiguous airport restrictions, the plaintiff has not carried the preponderance burden.

For now, the proper opening posture is disciplined skepticism. The case should turn on the exact Iranian aviation source material and its operational effect, not on inference from regional conflict or market commentary.

### Arguments

[plaintiff] Council members, the plaintiff has now carried the preponderance burden.

PX-1 is the market packet. It supplies the rule: Iran must have initiated, by May 24, 2026 at 11:59 PM ET, a major airspace closure not solely caused by weather. A major closure includes a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. The rule also says limited exceptions to a broad closure do not disqualify it.

PX-2 and PX-3 supply the core event. PX-2 reports that Iran's Civil Aviation Authority issued NOTAM OIIX A1010/26 on May 22, closing airspace over Iran's western half from May 22 to May 25, suspending all civil flight permits in the western sector of the Tehran FIR, and ordering shutdown of all airports in the western part of that FIR. It further reports that only eight airports remained operational, including Tehran's Mehrabad and Imam Khomeini, Isfahan, Shiraz, and Mashhad, and that even those flights were daylight-only and required fresh per-flight approval from the Civil Aviation Authority. PX-3 corroborates the same NOTAM code and gives precise timing: May 22, 2026 at 00:16 Iran time through May 25, 2026 at noon Iran time. It states that all airports in the western Tehran FIR were closed; only specified airports had limited sunrise-to-sunset operations; prior permissions were canceled; and new Civil Aviation Organization authorization was required for IFR passenger flights.

That satisfies each rule element. First, initiation: this was an Iranian Civil Aviation Authority/Civil Aviation Organization NOTAM, not a foreign warning or voluntary airline boycott. Second, timing: the NOTAM began May 22 and ran until May 25 noon Iran time. That includes May 24, 2026 at 11:59 PM ET. Third, non-weather cause: the submitted reports describe the measure in a military/security context, with PX-3 expressly tying it to continuing security conditions and military control over western Iran's airspace. There is no weather explanation in the record.

Fourth, and most important, scope: this was not a local delay or a VFR-only measure. The western sector of the Tehran FIR/western Iranian airspace is a major Iranian airspace region. PX-2 describes the closure as Iran's western half and the western sector of the Tehran FIR. PX-4, an aviation operations source, independently explains why that western region is operationally major: western Iran remained heavily restricted, many western airports were closed or daytime-only, east Tehran FIR was only partially reopened under recovery procedures, and the old Iran/Iraq central corridor was not restored. The rule does not require total national closure; it expressly allows a major Iranian airspace region. And it expressly tolerates limited exceptions. The daylight-only, fresh-approval operations at a short list of airports are exactly the kind of limited exceptions that do not defeat a broad closure.

The defense's best point is provenance. We have not located and submitted the raw official NOTAM database record. That matters to weight, and the plaintiff does not pretend otherwise. But preponderance does not require perfect custody where the submitted record is specific, internally consistent, and corroborated. PX-2 names the issuing authority, NOTAM code, geography, dates, operational effect, and limited exceptions. PX-3 independently names the same code, gives specific start/end times, and describes the same closure-plus-exceptions structure. PX-4 supplies aviation-operations context confirming continued western-Iran restrictions and closed/daytime-only western airports. Taken together, these sources make the claimed Iranian NOTAM and its broad regional effect more likely than not.

The airport-specific alternative is not the plaintiff's strongest path because the record shows some listed airports had limited daylight operations with new approval. But the plaintiff does not need that alternative. The rule's first path is enough: a broad Iran-initiated suspension of commercial civil flight permissions and airport operations across western Tehran FIR/western Iran, a major region, in force through the deadline, for security rather than weather.

For those reasons, the council should find the proposition true.

[defendant] Council members, plaintiff has not carried the preponderance burden.

The rule in the market packet requires more than disruption. It requires that Iran initiated a major non-weather closure: either a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region, or a complete suspension of commercial arrivals and departures at at least two listed airports. The packet also identifies the primary source class: official Iranian aviation authority information and a consensus of credible reporting.

Plaintiff’s proof falls short at the first source-quality step. PX-2 is a secondary article that says the NOTAM details came through Gulf News and the Iranian Civil Aviation Authority; its own custody note concedes it is not the original NOTAM. PX-3 is another secondary article; its custody note concedes it does not include a raw NOTAM image or official AIP/NOTAM database record. PX-4 is useful aviation operations context, but its own custody note concedes it is not the Iranian issuing authority and is not primary proof of the May 22 NOTAM. No official Iranian AIS record is in evidence. No predecessor NOTAM A0796/26 or A0799/26 is in evidence. That matters because the market rule expressly prefers official Iranian aviation sources.

The record also contains internal source conflict. PX-2 says the eight operational airports included Mehrabad, Imam Khomeini, Isfahan, Shiraz, Mashhad, Yazd, Tabriz, and Bandar Abbas. PX-3 instead lists Tabriz, Kermanshah, Ahvaz, Shiraz, Yazd, Kerman, Kish, and Abadan. D-1’s displayed raw NOTAM text uses airport codes matching the latter structure, not PX-2’s list. This is not a trivial discrepancy. Plaintiff asks the council to infer the scope and operational effect of a NOTAM while its own submitted sources disagree about the exceptions.

D-1 is not official custody either, and the defense offers it with that caveat. But it is useful because it displays the raw wording plaintiff has not supplied from an official source. The displayed NOTAM is coded and described as airport operations: “all airports in west part of Tehran FIR are closed” except eight named airports operating from sunrise to sunset, with new CAA permission required for civil IFR passenger flights. That wording does not itself say that all commercial flights transiting the western Tehran FIR were suspended, nor that all arrivals and departures across a major airspace region were broadly suspended. It shows a restriction on airport operations with exceptions and a permission process for civil IFR passenger flights.

The market rule allows limited exceptions to a broad closure, but the plaintiff still must prove the broad closure first. Here the record shows an ambiguous airport-operations restriction, not a clean closure of commercial transit, arrival, and departure traffic across Iranian airspace or a major airspace region. PX-4 actually reinforces the complexity: it describes a “recovery period,” says some overflights above FL285 were possible via specific temporary routes, and notes eastern Tehran FIR had reopened while western Iran remained heavily restricted. That is evidence of a partial, changing operational picture, not proof that the rule’s major-closure threshold was met by May 24.

Plaintiff’s alternative airport path is also unproved. The rule requires complete suspension of commercial arrivals and departures at at least two of IKA, THR, MHD, SYZ, and IFN. Plaintiff effectively concedes that is not its strongest path, and the record does not prove it. PX-2 says all five of those listed airports were among those still operational, albeit daylight-only and with fresh approval. PX-3 and D-1 list only Shiraz among the rule’s five listed airports as an exception, while omitting IKA, THR, MHD, and IFN from the exception list. The sources are inconsistent, and none supplies airport-by-airport arrival/departure data proving a complete suspension at two listed airports.

The non-weather element is plausible but still not enough. The submitted reports connect the restrictions to security and military conditions; that may satisfy non-weather if the underlying event is otherwise proved. But a non-weather restriction is not automatically a qualifying major closure.

At most, the admitted record proves that secondary and aggregator sources reported some Iran-linked restriction affecting western Tehran FIR airports from May 22 to May 25, with daylight and permission exceptions. It does not prove, by the rule’s preferred source hierarchy and by a preponderance, the exact official notice, the predecessor chain, the precise airport exceptions, the commercial-transit effect, or a complete suspension at two listed airports. Those missing links are decisive. The proposition should be found not proven.

### Rebuttals

[plaintiff] Council members, the defense's strongest argument is that plaintiff lacked the raw NOTAM text. But the defense then supplied D-1, and D-1 materially strengthens plaintiff's case.

D-1 displays the raw A1010/26 text. It matches the plaintiff's evidence on the important points: NOTAM A1010/26, Tehran FIR/OIIX, May 22 through May 25, CAA permission required, and a closure with only daylight/permission exceptions. Most importantly, D-1 says: "ALL AIRPORTS IN WEST PART OF TEHRAN FIR ARE CLSD" except a listed set operating sunrise to sunset; it also says all previous permissions are suspended for all operators and new permission must be requested from CAA for civil IFR passenger flights on those airports. That is not a mere foreign warning, market rumor, airline preference, or weather event. It is an Iran-linked aviation restriction, with a CAA permission regime, covering the west part of Tehran FIR.

The defense tries to turn the word "airports" into a defeat. The rule does not support that. A major closure is defined as a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. A regional shutdown of airports, prior permissions, and civil IFR passenger operations across the west part of the Tehran FIR is a broad suspension of commercial aviation in that major region. It covers arrivals and departures at the regional airport level, and PX-4 gives operational context showing western Iran/western Tehran FIR was the critical restricted part of the airspace system, not a trivial local field closure.

Nor do exceptions defeat the rule. PX-1 expressly says limited exceptions to a broad closure do not disqualify it, including pre-approved flights. D-1 describes just that structure: all airports in the west part of Tehran FIR closed, except a defined list operating only sunrise-to-sunset, with all prior permissions suspended and new CAA approval required for civil IFR passenger flights. That is narrower than normal commercial operation and fits the rule's example of a broad closure with permission exceptions.

The defense also points to differing exception lists between sources. That is not decisive. The proposition does not turn on whether every secondary source rendered the exception list identically. The key facts are consistent across PX-2, PX-3, and D-1: same NOTAM number, same May 22-May 25 period, same western Tehran FIR/western Iran geography, same closure of west-part airports, same limited daylight exceptions, same requirement for new CAA authorization. Any discrepancy about which airports were in the exception list affects the airport-specific alternative, which plaintiff has not relied on as the primary path. It does not defeat the broad-major-region path.

The custody caveat remains, but the record now contains more than plaintiff's secondary reports. It contains defendant-submitted raw NOTAM wording from an aggregator, and that wording confirms the exact closure-plus-exceptions structure. Under a preponderance standard, the combined record makes it more likely than not that Iran initiated a non-weather major regional closure by the deadline.
