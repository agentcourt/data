## Proceeding

### Openings

#### Plaintiff Opening

Council members, this case is not mainly about whether the syllables were spoken. The case-packet transcript says they were. The real dispute is the market boundary: when Kalshi asked what Trump would say "during the FIFA World Cup Draw," did that phrase clearly exclude his public, event-linked remarks at the World Cup Draw venue immediately before the formal program? On this record, it did not.

The market page fixes the contract at issue: the public title was "What will Trump say during the FIFA World Cup Draw?"; the contract family was PRESMENTION / NEWMENTION; the strike was "Ronaldo"; Kalshi challenged the resolution as No; and the requested resolution is Yes. That means the council's task is to apply the mention-market terms to that title and strike, not to import a narrower limitation that the market did not plainly announce.

The reporter-exchange packet is direct evidence on the word and setting. It is titled as remarks in an exchange with reporters prior to the 2026 FIFA World Cup Draw, dated December 5, 2025. It places the President at the John F. Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts, speaking with FIFA President Gianni Infantino and reporters in a sequence repeatedly labeled around the 2026 World Cup and the Draw. In that exchange, in a soccer discussion, Trump says he met Ronaldo, praises him, and uses "Ronaldo" as a distinct standalone word. Under the mention rules, a distinct standalone appearance of the strike is the kind of use that counts if it falls within the time period.

The terms matter because they do not say that an event market is limited only to scripted remarks from a formal stage segment. They define a time period as potentially an event or context, and they give the Exchange power to specify what is included when a time period refers to a specific event. They also distinguish official/public event content from behind-the-scenes, rehearsals, and unofficial recordings. That distinction helps plaintiff: the packet is not a rumor, rehearsal, private soundcheck, or leaked recording. It is a sourced public transcript of remarks at the event venue, immediately tied to the Draw. If Kalshi meant "only the formal televised draw program after doors close" or "only remarks in the separate formal archive," it needed to say so clearly.

I also want to be candid about what the packet does not yet prove. The formal-draw archive note says a separate archived transcript page exists for Trump's formal attendance remarks, but that the session could not retrieve the full text because the page was password-protected. So, at opening, plaintiff is not asking you to infer that the formal program itself contained "Ronaldo." Nor does plaintiff need that inference. The Yes theory is that the qualifying period reasonably includes the public pre-program reporter exchange that was part of Trump's World Cup Draw appearance. The formal archive is relevant because it marks defendant's likely narrower boundary; it does not resolve the boundary in defendant's favor.

Provenance and weight also favor treating the packet seriously at this stage. The market and terms files are system case-packet records. The reporter exchange includes a source URL to a public presidential transcript source and notes a corrected transcript date, while also acknowledging that some audio could not be verified because the audio was incomplete. That caveat may affect inaudible portions, but it does not erase the visible, contextual Ronaldo passage. Later, when evidence admission opens, the parties should preserve the official transcript source, any govinfo or Presidency Project copy, the market page capture, and any accessible formal-program transcript or video. The adverse check should ask whether the formal program omits the word, but omission there would matter only if the council first accepts the defense's artificially narrow reading of "during."

The plaintiff's burden is preponderance. The record already points to a straightforward path to Yes: Trump, the specified entity, said the specified strike as a standalone word in public remarks linked to and immediately preceding the World Cup Draw at the venue; the market title and governing terms did not clearly exclude that event-linked exchange. On a fair reading of the contract the trader saw, the challenged No resolution should give way to Yes.

#### Defendant Opening

Council members, the defense opening is simple: this case is not won by proving that the word "Ronaldo" appears somewhere in a public transcript. The plaintiff must prove, by a preponderance, that the word was said during the market's specified event: the FIFA World Cup Draw.

The current packet leaves that critical link unproven. The market page does not say "while Trump was at the venue," "during a World Cup press availability," or "during any remarks connected to the Draw." It says: "What will Trump say during the FIFA World Cup Draw?" The challenged resolution was No. The requested Yes theory therefore needs more than event-adjacent context; it needs a boundary showing that the reporter exchange was part of the Draw period the market asked traders to price.

The plaintiff's own best transcript cuts against that boundary. The case-packet file is titled "Remarks in an Exchange With Reporters Prior to the 2026 FIFA World Cup Draw." It says the President's remarks were joined in progress, and the source note says portions could not be verified because the audio was incomplete. It places Trump at the Kennedy Center and includes World Cup topics, and yes, it contains "Ronaldo" as a standalone word in a soccer exchange. But its title and structure identify those remarks as an exchange with reporters prior to the Draw, not the formal Draw itself.

The terms do not rescue plaintiff from that distinction. They define a mention market around words used by the entity during the time period. A time period may be a specific event, and when it is a specific event, the Exchange may specify what is included. The technical details also distinguish official presentation content from behind-the-scenes, rehearsals, sound checks, and unofficial recordings unless those are officially broadcast or published as part of the time period. Those provisions do not mean every public, nearby, event-related conversation becomes part of the event. They make the event boundary the central question.

The formal-draw archive note reinforces why the distinction matters. It reports that there is a separate archived transcript page for Trump's formal attendance remarks at the FIFA World Cup Drawing, but that the full text was not retrieved because the page was password-protected. That note does not prove the formal program omitted Ronaldo. It also does not prove the formal program included it. What it does show, at opening, is that the record itself separates the pre-event reporter exchange from the formal drawing remarks.

So the defense will focus the council on proof of inclusion, not mere association. If later phases produce a market page provision, official broadcast record, program transcript, video metadata, or source-agency material showing that the red-carpet reporter exchange was officially part of the FIFA World Cup Draw presentation for this market, the council can weigh that. If the later evidence instead shows a formal program with a separate pre-program press availability, then the correct result is No even though the word was spoken nearby.

On this opening record, plaintiff has shown a Ronaldo mention in remarks prior to the event. Plaintiff has not shown that those remarks occurred during the market event. The defense position is that Kalshi's No resolution follows the market wording and the mention-market terms unless plaintiff supplies that missing boundary proof.

### Arguments

The fair resolution is Yes.

### Rebuttals

Defendant’s argument treats the title “Prior to the 2026 FIFA World Cup Draw” as if “prior to” automatically means outside the Kalshi market’s event period. But PX-1 does not say “formal transcript only,” “after the draw host begins,” “after 00:00:50,” or “excluding press availability at the venue.” It asks what Trump would say “during the FIFA World Cup Draw.” The source label tells us sequence; it does not tell us the market’s legal boundary.

DX-2 actually illustrates why defendant’s bright line is chosen after the fact. In defendant’s own formal extraction, the page begins before the mechanical draw: Mark Carney speaks at 00:00:00, and Infantino says at 00:00:50, “we are now going to do the draw.” If “during the FIFA World Cup Draw” can include ceremony and preamble on the formal page before the draw itself begins, then the phrase is not self-defining down to a single moment or transcript. The question is what the market clearly specified. It did not specify defendant’s transcript-only rule.

The terms do not fill that gap for defendant. PX-3 excludes hidden categories such as rehearsals, sound checks, unofficial recordings, and behind-the-scenes content unless officially published as part of the period. PX-2 and PX-5 are not those categories. Defendant says plaintiff needs a bridge. The bridge is the market text plus the terms’ failure to draw the narrow line defendant proposes. A No resolution can stand only if the contract fairly told traders that “during the FIFA World Cup Draw” meant only the separate formal-program transcript. On this record, the more likely reading is broader: public, official remarks at the venue, immediately tied to and immediately preceding the World Cup Draw program, were within the event-linked period unless clearly excluded.

So DX-2 narrows one factual issue but does not decide the case. It shows no formal-stage Ronaldo mention. It does not show that the market excluded the official pre-program event remarks where Trump did say Ronaldo. Under the preponderance standard, the Yes resolution remains the fair one.

### Surrebuttals

#### Defendant Surrebuttal

Council members, plaintiff’s rebuttal usefully narrows the dispute. Plaintiff accepts that the formal Roll Call/Factba.se drawing transcript contains no “Ronaldo” or “Messi.” So the case remains entirely about whether a separate reporter exchange titled “Prior to the 2026 FIFA World Cup Draw” should be treated as inside the market period.

Plaintiff says “prior to” is only sequence, not legal boundary. But sequence is exactly what the market phrase makes material. The contract asks what Trump would say “during the FIFA World Cup Draw.” A source-agency title identifying the Ronaldo transcript as an exchange with reporters prior to that Draw is not a stray label; it is the best record description of when and what that source is. Plaintiff cannot convert that source into the event itself by saying the market failed to list every excluded pre-event setting.

Plaintiff’s formal-preamble point also does not carry the burden. Yes, the formal transcript begins before the mechanical ball selection, and Infantino says at 00:00:50 that “we are now going to do the draw.” But that supports the defense distinction. A host’s preamble inside the formal drawing transcript is part of the program. A separate press exchange, separately titled and sourced as prior to the Draw, is not made part of the program simply because it happened nearby and discussed the same event.

Nor does official publication settle the issue. PX-5 strengthens provenance: Trump did say Ronaldo in an official public transcript. But provenance answers whether the words were said, not whether they were said during the market’s specified event. The terms require both: the word by the entity, and the word during the time period. Plaintiff has the first element. The second is still missing.

The remaining bridge plaintiff proposes is essentially: public, official, event-linked, and not expressly excluded. That is not enough under a preponderance standard when the same record supplies a cleaner boundary: the Ronaldo source is prior to the Draw, and the formal Draw source omits the strike. The challenged No resolution should stand.

### Closings

#### Plaintiff Closing

Council members, the record has narrowed to one real issue.

The word is proven. The market page identifies the strike as “Ronaldo” and the challenged resolution as No. The case-packet transcript and the GovInfo/OFR excerpt prove that Trump said “Ronaldo” as a standalone word in public remarks at 11:13 a.m. at the John F. Kennedy Center, in a World Cup Draw setting, with FIFA President Infantino present, and amid repeated discussion of the Draw, the Kennedy Center, ticket sales, the audience, and the imminent event. That is strong provenance: GovInfo, NARA/OFR, DCPD202501162, matching the packet source and note.

Defendant has also proved its best fact. The Roll Call/Factba.se formal-program extraction does not contain “Ronaldo” or “Messi.” Plaintiff does not ask you to find otherwise. If the market had said “only the formal drawing transcript,” defendant would win. But that is not the market traders saw.

The market asked: “What will Trump say during the FIFA World Cup Draw?” It did not say “during the formal transcript only,” “after the host announces the draw has begun,” “excluding public press remarks at the venue,” or “excluding remarks immediately before the program.” The governing terms do not add that limitation. They define words used by the entity during the time period, allow event/context time periods, and exclude categories such as rehearsals, sound checks, behind-the-scenes content, and unofficial recordings. Plaintiff’s source is none of those. It is an official public presidential transcript of event-linked remarks at the venue.

Defendant’s formal extraction shows why a bright line cannot simply be assumed. Even on that page, before balls are drawn, there is ceremony, introduction, and Infantino saying “we are now going to do the draw.” Event boundaries are not self-defining. The question is whether Kalshi clearly chose defendant’s narrower boundary. The record contains no such choice.

So the burden is met. More likely than not, a reasonable reading of the market and terms includes Trump’s official, public, venue-based remarks immediately tied to the World Cup Draw. The strongest contrary evidence proves only that a narrower formal transcript omits the strike. It does not prove the contract excluded the remarks where the strike was actually said.

The challenged No resolution should be overturned. The strike “Ronaldo” should resolve Yes.

#### Defendant Closing

Council members, plaintiff’s closing concedes the decisive factual split: the formal World Cup Drawing transcript contains no “Ronaldo” or “Messi.” The only Ronaldo evidence is the separate official transcript titled “Remarks in an Exchange With Reporters Prior to the 2026 FIFA World Cup Draw.”

That is enough to decide the case. The market did not ask what Trump would say at the Kennedy Center, on World Cup Draw day, in public presidential remarks, or in event-linked press availability. It asked what Trump would say “during the FIFA World Cup Draw.” The terms require the word to be used by the entity during the specified time period. Plaintiff has proven the word and the speaker. It has not proven the time period.

Plaintiff’s strongest point is that the market did not expressly say “formal transcript only” or “exclude pre-program remarks.” But plaintiff has the burden. A challenged No resolution does not become Yes merely because the contract could have been even more verbose. The record must make it more likely than not that the pre-event reporter exchange was inside the market period. The record does the opposite.

The source labels matter. PX-2 and PX-5 are official and probative, but they are official proof of remarks prior to the Draw. The formal Roll Call/Factba.se extraction is the source for Trump’s formal attendance at the FIFA World Cup Drawing; it begins the program, includes the draw presentation, and omits the strike. Plaintiff’s theory asks you to treat a separate prior reporter exchange as “during” the Draw even though the formal Draw source exists and does not contain the word.

The terms do not collapse that distinction. They recognize event/context time periods and official presentation content, but they still require the word to occur during the chosen time period. Official publication proves provenance. Event connection proves relevance. Neither proves inclusion. A public official transcript can be accurately preserved and still be outside the market window if it is, as titled, prior to the event.

So the fair synthesis is narrow and grounded: Trump said Ronaldo in event-linked public remarks before the FIFA World Cup Draw; Trump did not say Ronaldo in the formal drawing transcript preserved in this record; and no admitted market term or source-chain evidence bridges the pre-event exchange into the market’s “during the FIFA World Cup Draw” period.

Under the preponderance standard, plaintiff has not carried the proposition. The challenged No resolution should stand.
