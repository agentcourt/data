## Filings

### Openings

[plaintiff] Council members, the plaintiff's case is about the precise wording of the proposition, not about proving beyond doubt that Jeffrey Epstein actually wrote the document. The question is whether, by May 8, 2026 at 11:59 PM ET, a note or message had been made publicly available and had been credibly reported to have been written by Epstein and intended as a suicide note, final message, or equivalent communication. On the current packet, the answer is more likely than not yes.

The market rule preserved in the packet is important. It does not demand a definitive handwriting authentication, a government finding, or a single official resolution source. It asks for a message or note that was publicly available, credibly reported as Epstein's, and treated as a suicide note/final-message equivalent, with resolution by consensus credible reporting. That framing matters because the public record is cautious but not empty: the packet's reporting map identifies Reuters, NPR/AP, BBC, Guardian, and New York Times leads around the same court release.

The evidence we have now already shows the core chronology. A federal judge, Kenneth Karas of the Southern District of New York, unsealed or released a one-page note connected to Nicholas Tartaglione, Epstein's former cellmate. The reporting captured in the packet places that release before the May 8 deadline. Reuters described a document released by the judge as a suicide note purportedly written by Epstein and quoted language about choosing one's time to say goodbye. NPR/AP reported that the judge unsealed the one-page note after a New York Times petition and quoted the same goodbye language. BBC and Guardian reporting likewise described an alleged or purported suicide note, publicly released by a federal judge, and described its contents in terms consistent with a final communication.

Those reports are not marginal internet rumor. They are mainstream, named news organizations reporting on a court-unsealed document. The note's public availability is therefore the strongest element: multiple reports describe the court release itself before the deadline, and the Polymarket packet separately records that the market page understood the disputed question as the May 8 grouped market.

The hard issue is weight, not existence. The same packet preserves the caveats. The court did not vouch for authenticity. Several outlets used words like alleged or purported. NPR, BBC, and Guardian noted they had not independently verified authorship, and NPR/AP reported uncertainty about who wrote the note. The official-source file says the packet does not yet include the SDNY docket PDF, the unsealing order PDF, or the note image as local official exhibits. Those gaps should make the council cautious about any argument that Epstein's authorship has been conclusively proved.

But the proposition does not require conclusive proof of authorship. It requires that a note was credibly reported to have been written by Epstein. Responsible hedging in credible reports does not erase credibility; it often demonstrates it. The reports identified a court release, a claimed source chain through Tartaglione and his lawyers, and the note text. That is enough at opening to frame the plaintiff's burden as a preponderance question: did credible public reporting, before May 8, make available and describe the note in the qualifying way? The packet points strongly toward yes.

Intent is similar. The note need not be a formal, signed will or declaration. The rule allows a suicide note, final message, or equivalent communication. The reported language about choosing one's time to say goodbye, together with the context of Epstein's suspected July 2019 attempt and later death, is why credible outlets characterized it as a suicide note or alleged suicide note. The defense may argue the short, unsigned text is ambiguous. That is a fair limitation, but it does not defeat the more modest proposition that it was credibly reported as such.

In later evidentiary phases, the decisive documents should be added directly: the SDNY unsealing order and any released note image or court handout, the full Reuters/AP/Guardian/NYT materials, and the best adverse materials showing the limits of authentication and chain of custody. For now, the opening theory is straightforward: the record already shows a public court release before the deadline and a consensus of credible reporting that treated the document as a purported Epstein suicide or final-message note. The remaining disputes affect weight; they do not change the plaintiff's burden from preponderance into certainty.

[defendant] Council members, the defense opening is simple: the plaintiff must prove the whole proposition, not merely that a provocative document was released by a court before May 8.

The record likely supports one element. The packet describes a public release or unsealing connected to Judge Kenneth Karas and the Tartaglione criminal case before the listed deadline. We do not need to pretend otherwise. But the market rule asks for more: a message or note credibly reported to have been written by Jeffrey Epstein and intended as a suicide note, final message, or equivalent communication. The packet itself flags why those added elements are not established on this record.

Start with provenance. The official-source packet says the local record does not contain the SDNY docket entry, unsealing order, or the released note image as a primary court exhibit. More importantly, it says the public reporting preserved here reports that the court unsealed the note but did not authenticate it as Epstein's writing or decide its intent. Reuters, as summarized in the packet, reported that Judge Karas released the note, treated it as a judicial document subject to public access, found no legal reason to keep it sealed, and did not vouch for authenticity or assess chain of custody. That is a public-access ruling, not proof that the note was Epstein's.

The reporting evidence has the same problem. The packet's strongest plaintiff sources repeatedly use limiting words: purported, alleged, described as, said to have been found. NPR says it had not independently verified authenticity and adds a material DOJ limitation: the note had not yet been authenticated and this was the first time DOJ was seeing it. BBC likewise says it had not verified the note was written by Epstein and reports that authorities had not commented. Guardian says it had not verified whether the letter was written by Epstein. Those caveats are not incidental. They go directly to the proposition's phrase "credibly reported to have been written by Jeffrey Epstein."

Intent is also unresolved. The packet quotes language about choosing one's time to say goodbye, but it also preserves the rest of the reported text: complaints about being investigated, references to old charges, and emotional language about crying and not being worth it. The BBC summary says the note was only seven lines and left uncertainty over its purported meaning. A short, unsigned, unauthenticated text can be later characterized as a suicide note without having been shown to be intended as one. The rule includes final message or equivalent communication, but it still requires intent, not merely a news label attached after release.

The plaintiff will argue that consensus credible reporting is enough and that the rule does not require conclusive authentication. That is partly right but incomplete. The standard is preponderance, not certainty, and the source rule is credible reporting, not an official government determination. But credible reporting of uncertainty is still uncertainty. When the same credible reports say they cannot verify authorship, that DOJ had not authenticated it, and that the judge did not assess custody, the council should not convert cautious reporting into affirmative proof.

The current packet is best understood as a roadmap, not a completed proof. It identifies the sources that matter: the court order and docket material, the released note image, the full reporting from Reuters, NPR, BBC, Guardian, and New York Times, and especially any authentication follow-up. Those materials may eventually show whether there was enough consensus to resolve yes. But at opening, the defense theory is that the admitted packet proves a public dispute over an alleged note, while leaving the decisive links missing: authorship, chain of custody, and intent. If those links remain missing, the proposition should not be found proved by a preponderance.

### Arguments

[plaintiff] Council members, the admitted record now proves the proposition by a preponderance.

Start with the governing condition. PX-1 states the market would resolve yes if any message or note written by Jeffrey Epstein, intended as a suicide note, final message, or equivalent communication, was made publicly available by the listed date, and that the resolution source would be a consensus of credible reporting. The rule does not require a handwriting trial, a DOJ authentication letter, or an official source as the only path. It asks whether there was credible public reporting of the qualifying note by May 8, 2026.

Public availability is established. PX-4, the SDNY order extraction, is an official court source. Judge Karas described the requested item as a suicide note purportedly authored by Epstein, recovered by Tartaglione, and reportedly submitted by Tartaglione's attorneys. The order granted the Times' request to unseal the Note and held that it was a judicial document subject to public access. PX-5 then supplies the Reuters Connect image-caption record: a document including the line about choosing one's time to say goodbye was seen after release by Judge Karas in New York City on May 6, 2026, with the source identified as U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York/Handout via Reuters. PX-6 and PX-7 independently report the same public release on May 6, before the May 8 deadline.

Credible reporting of Epstein authorship is also established, in the exact qualified sense the proposition uses. PX-5 describes the document as a suicide note purportedly written by Epstein. PX-6 reports that Epstein's former cellmate claimed he found the note after Epstein's suspected July 2019 jail suicide attempt, and that Karas ordered its release after the Times sought unsealing. PX-7 reports a federal judge unsealed an alleged suicide note written by Epstein and identifies the alleged note image as from the United States district judge/Southern District of New York. These are not anonymous posts or market chatter; they are an official court order plus Reuters metadata, AP/PBS, and Guardian reporting.

Intent is satisfied by both text and reporting context. PX-5 identifies the key line: "It is a treat to be able to choose one's time to say goodbye." PX-6 reports the same language and the surrounding statements, including "NO FUN" and "NOT WORTH IT!!" PX-7 likewise quotes the goodbye language and describes the release as an alleged suicide note. The rule allows not only a formal suicide note but also a final message or equivalent communication. A short note saying it is a treat to choose one's time to say goodbye, reported in connection with Epstein's suspected suicide attempt and later death, easily crosses the preponderance threshold for being credibly reported as a suicide/final-message equivalent.

The defense is right about one thing: the record does not conclusively authenticate the note as Epstein's handwriting. The plaintiff does not need to hide that. PX-3 and the newly admitted sources preserve the limitations. PX-4 is a public-access order, not a handwriting finding. PX-6 says it is unclear who wrote the note, that it was not mentioned in government reports, and that Epstein had reportedly accused Tartaglione. PX-7 says the note lacks a signature and that Guardian had not independently verified authorship. Those caveats would matter if the proposition asked, "Did Epstein actually write an authenticated suicide note?" It does not.

The actual proposition asks whether a note was credibly reported to have been written by Epstein and intended as a suicide note/final message/equivalent, and whether it had been made public by the deadline. Hedged words like alleged and purported are not fatal under that standard; they are how responsible outlets report an attributed document whose authenticity has not been finally adjudicated. The credible-reporting consensus is that a court-released document, sourced to SDNY/Reuters handout and reported by AP/PBS and Guardian, was publicly available on May 6 and was described as an Epstein suicide note or alleged suicide note.

The missing links therefore reduce certainty about actual authorship, not the reporting-standard proposition. By May 8, the public had the court release and mainstream reporting describing the note in qualifying terms. On this record, yes is more likely than no.

[defendant] Council members, after plaintiff's new evidence, the defense does not contest the easy part: a document was publicly released before the May 8 deadline. The question is whether the record proves the qualifying proposition as written: a note credibly reported to have been written by Epstein and intended as a suicide note, final message, or equivalent communication. The record still falls short.

The plaintiff's best evidence proves public access, not authenticity. PX-4, the SDNY order extraction, describes the item as an alleged suicide note purportedly authored by Epstein, recovered by Tartaglione, and reportedly submitted by Tartaglione's attorneys. The court's legal holding was that the Note was subject to the presumption of public access because it had been submitted in connection with a Curcio hearing in Tartaglione's case. That explains why the public could see it. It does not decide who wrote it, whether its custody was reliable, or what the writer intended.

The source chain is unusually weak for a preponderance finding on authorship. The asserted chain is not Epstein to jail officials to investigators. It is Tartaglione, a convicted murderer and Epstein's former cellmate, saying he found a note in a book, then giving it to his lawyers, after which it became part of sealed attorney-client/conflict proceedings in Tartaglione's case. DB-1, the direct AP report, adds the central official limitation: DOJ's explanation was that it never had the note, that the note had not yet been authenticated, and that this was the first time DOJ was seeing it. AP also reports that if DOJ lawyers saw material in the sealed Tartaglione-attorney proceedings, they were barred from disclosing it. That is not just a missing authentication stamp; it is a source-chain problem.

The mainstream reports do not create the consensus plaintiff needs. PX-6 says it is unclear who wrote the note, that it was not mentioned in the government reports examining Epstein's death, and that it did not surface in DOJ's recent files release. PX-7 says the note has no signature and that the Guardian had not verified whether the letter was written by Epstein. PX-5, the Reuters Connect caption, is useful proof that an image/caption circulated, but it expressly calls the image third-party supplied and processed by Reuters, with an unprocessed version elsewhere. A caption saying "purportedly written" is not a finding that credible reporting established authorship.

Plaintiff says hedged words like alleged and purported are normal reporting practice. Sometimes they are. Here they are doing real evidentiary work. Every key source preserved in the record either avoids endorsing authorship or reports affirmative reasons for doubt: no signature, no authentication, DOJ had not seen it, no appearance in official Epstein-death reports, no appearance in DOJ's files release, and a chain originating with Tartaglione. The proposition uses the phrase "credibly reported to have been written by Jeffrey Epstein." On this record, the credible reporting consensus is that a note was claimed or described as Epstein's, while authorship remained unresolved.

Intent is no stronger. The reported text includes a line about choosing one's time to say goodbye, but it also includes complaints about investigations, old charges, crying, "NO FUN," and "NOT WORTH IT." DB-1 describes the note as short, hard to decipher in places, and not authenticated. PX-6 and PX-7 connect it to Epstein's July 2019 suspected attempt, but the same record says Epstein told a guard Tartaglione had attacked him, later recanted, and was placed briefly on suicide watch before being downgraded. A later news label of "suicide note" cannot substitute for proof that this unsigned ambiguous document was intended as a suicide note, final message, or equivalent communication.

This is not an argument for certainty as the standard. It is an argument for applying all elements of the rule. The plaintiff has shown public availability and public controversy. It has shown that several credible outlets responsibly repeated a characterization while preserving doubts. It has not shown that the consensus of credible reporting made it more likely than not that the note was written by Epstein and intended as the qualifying suicide/final-message communication.

The council should weigh plaintiff's exhibits for what they actually prove. PX-4 proves unsealing. PX-5 proves a Reuters/SDNY handout caption. PX-6 and PX-7 prove timely public reporting, but also preserve the core doubts. DB-1 gives the most direct official-source-chain limit: DOJ never had it, had not authenticated it, and was seeing it for the first time. On the complete record, the proposition remains unproved by a preponderance.

### Rebuttals

[plaintiff] Council members, the defense argument proves too much by quietly changing the question.

The proposition does not ask whether a court, DOJ, or handwriting expert finally authenticated Epstein's authorship. It asks whether, by May 8, a publicly available note was credibly reported to have been written by Epstein and intended as a suicide note, final message, or equivalent communication. That is why PX-1 makes consensus credible reporting the resolution source. The defense's evidence attacks a different proposition: actual authorship certainty.

PX-R1 is useful, but not in the way the defense claims. It says DOJ never had the note, that it had not yet been authenticated, and that DOJ was seeing it for the first time. Those facts explain why careful outlets used words like alleged and purported. They do not erase the fact that AP, Reuters Connect, Guardian, and the SDNY order all publicly described the same court-released item in the qualifying terms before the deadline.

Indeed, PX-R1 confirms several plaintiff points. It says the note was made public, that The New York Times petitioned Judge Karas to release it, that Judge Karas agreed, and that DOJ did not object. It repeats Tartaglione's claim that he found the handwritten note after Epstein was found in their cell with a bedsheet around his neck. It gives the reported text, including choosing the time to say goodbye. That is not proof against credible reporting; it is another credible report that the note existed, was released, and was being publicly reported in the suicide-note frame.

The defense says the source chain is weak because it runs through Tartaglione and his lawyers. That would be decisive if the question required the council to find, as a historical fact, that Epstein wrote it. But the actual standard is whether the note was credibly reported to have been written by Epstein. Qualified reporting is still reporting. When multiple mainstream sources identify a court-released document as a purported or alleged Epstein suicide note, while openly preserving authentication caveats, the word "purported" does not nullify credibility. It defines the level of claim being credibly reported.

The same is true of intent. The defense isolates ambiguity in the short text, but the admitted reports did not label a random note after the fact. They described a document found after a suspected jail suicide attempt, released by a federal judge, containing the line about choosing one's time to say goodbye. Under a rule that includes final message or equivalent communication, that is enough for credible reporting of intent by a preponderance.

So the council can accept every defense caveat and still find for plaintiff. No official authentication; no signature; source-chain limits; DOJ first saw it in 2026. Those are reasons not to overstate actual authorship. They are not reasons to deny the narrower proposition that, by May 8, a publicly available note had been credibly reported as a purported Epstein suicide/final-message note. The record satisfies that proposition.

### Surrebuttals

[defendant] Council members, plaintiff's rebuttal says the defense is demanding actual-authorship certainty. That is not our position. The rule does not require a handwriting trial. But it does require more than credible reporting that someone claimed, labeled, or captioned a document as an alleged Epstein suicide note.

PX-1 asks whether a qualifying note was written by Epstein and intended as a suicide note/final message/equivalent, with credible reporting as the resolution source. The phrase "credibly reported to have been written by Jeffrey Epstein" cannot mean "credibly reported to have been alleged or purported to be written by Epstein" no matter how weak the source chain is. Otherwise the authorship requirement vanishes.

That is why DB-1/PX-R1 matters. Plaintiff treats it as harmless context for cautious wording. It is more than that. AP reports the government's explanation that it never had the note, that the note had not yet been authenticated, and that DOJ was seeing it for the first time. It also places the note in an unrelated Tartaglione attorney-client dispute, in a court vault since 2021, with disclosure limits on any DOJ lawyers in those proceedings. Those facts bear directly on whether the reporting consensus established Epstein authorship, not merely whether conclusive proof was absent.

The same point applies to intent. A line about choosing one's time to say goodbye is relevant, but the record also shows a short, hard-to-decipher, unsigned document with grievance language and a disputed jail incident involving Tartaglione. The public reports responsibly used the suicide-note frame while preserving that uncertainty. That is not the same as a preponderance showing that the note was intended as a suicide note or final message.

So the defense is not asking the council to ignore credible reporting. We ask the council to read it accurately. The consensus is: a court released a document before May 8; credible outlets reported it as alleged or purported; and the same record preserved serious authorship, custody, and intent caveats. That proves public availability of a disputed document. It does not prove the full qualifying proposition by a preponderance.

### Closings

[plaintiff] Council members, the proposition should be found true by a preponderance.

The defense ultimately concedes the first and most concrete element: a document was publicly released before the May 8 deadline. That concession is compelled by the record. PX-4, the SDNY order extraction, shows Judge Karas granted access to the Note. PX-5, the Reuters Connect/SDNY handout caption, dates the released document image to May 6, 2026. PX-6 and PX-7 report the release the same day. There is no serious timing dispute left.

The real dispute is what level of certainty the proposition requires. The defense asks you to treat missing official authentication as fatal. But PX-1 does not make authentication the test. It sets a reporting-standard market: the source is consensus credible reporting, and the operative phrase here is that a note was credibly reported to have been written by Epstein and intended as a suicide note, final message, or equivalent communication.

That standard is satisfied. PX-4 identifies the court-filed item as a suicide note purportedly authored by Epstein, recovered by Tartaglione, and submitted by Tartaglione's attorneys. PX-5 describes the released image as a document including the line about choosing one's time to say goodbye, described as a suicide note purportedly written by Epstein, sourced to U.S. District Court/SDNY handout via Reuters. PX-6, AP/PBS, reports the public release, the Tartaglione discovery account, and the same goodbye language. PX-7, Guardian, independently reports the federal judge unsealed an alleged suicide note written by Epstein, quotes the same text, and identifies the image as from the Southern District of New York.

That is not one stray allegation. It is an official court-access order plus Reuters image metadata and two independent mainstream reports, all before the deadline, all describing the same public document in substantially the same qualifying frame.

The defense's best contrary evidence, DB-1/PX-R1, is important but not dispositive. It says DOJ never had the note, had not authenticated it, and was seeing it for the first time. It explains why the reports used careful words like alleged and purported. It does not erase the reports. In fact, it confirms the public release, the New York Times petition, Judge Karas's agreement, DOJ's non-objection, Tartaglione's discovery claim, and the note language.

So the council can accept every caveat and still answer yes. No signature; no official authentication; unclear actual authorship; source-chain limits through Tartaglione and his lawyers. Those are reasons to avoid saying "Epstein definitely wrote it." The proposition does not ask for that. It asks whether the note had been publicly available and credibly reported as Epstein's suicide/final-message note. The record shows that it had.

Intent is proved at the same reporting-standard level. The note's reported language about choosing one's time to say goodbye, its connection to Epstein's suspected July 2019 jail suicide attempt, and repeated public characterization as a suicide note or alleged suicide note are enough for a suicide note, final message, or equivalent communication. The defense can point to ambiguity in the short text, but preponderance does not require eliminating ambiguity; it requires the more likely reading of the admitted record.

If the rule had required official authentication, the defense would have the stronger case. But that is not the rule. Under the actual wording and the evidence admitted here, the plaintiff has shown public availability by May 8 and a consensus of credible, qualified reporting that the document was a purported Epstein suicide/final-message note. The proposition is more likely true than false.

[defendant] Council members, the final record proves one thing clearly: a document was publicly released before May 8. The defense has never needed to deny that. The proposition, however, asks for more. It asks whether a message or note had been made public and had been credibly reported to have been written by Jeffrey Epstein and intended as a suicide note, final message, or equivalent communication. Plaintiff has not carried that full burden.

The best way to see the gap is to compare plaintiff's closing formulation to the actual rule. Plaintiff closes by saying the record shows a publicly available note was credibly reported as a "purported Epstein suicide/final-message note." But "reported as purported" is not the proposition. The proposition requires credible reporting that the note was written by Epstein and intended as the qualifying communication. The word "purported" matters because the sources used it to preserve uncertainty, not to resolve it.

The record evidence repeatedly stops short of plaintiff's needed inference. PX-4 is an official court-access order, but it describes an alleged note purportedly authored by Epstein and decides public access, not authorship, custody, or intent. PX-5 is a Reuters Connect caption for a third-party supplied, Reuters-processed image; it confirms circulation and a caption, not authenticity. PX-6 says it is unclear who wrote the note, that it was not mentioned in government reports examining Epstein's death, and that it did not surface in DOJ's files release. PX-7 says the note has no signature and that the Guardian had not verified whether Epstein wrote it.

DB-1/PX-R1 is the strongest limiting evidence. AP reports DOJ's explanation that it never had the note, that the note had not yet been authenticated, and that DOJ was seeing it for the first time. It also places the note in an unrelated Tartaglione attorney-client dispute, in a court vault since 2021, with disclosure limits from sealed proceedings. That is not a minor caveat. It is the central provenance problem.

Intent is likewise not proved by a preponderance. The line about choosing one's time to say goodbye is relevant, but the same record shows a short, hard-to-decipher, unsigned note with grievance language, a disputed jail incident, and no authentication. Labeling that document a suicide note in headlines or captions does not establish that it was intended as a suicide note, final message, or equivalent communication.

The defense is not asking for certainty or an official authentication requirement. We are asking the council to enforce all words of the proposition. Credible reporting can be the resolution source, but the reporting here is credible precisely because it preserves the unresolved issues: alleged, purported, unclear author, no signature, not verified, not in government death reports, not in DOJ files, not authenticated, and first seen by DOJ only after release.

So the right finding is narrow and faithful to the record: by May 8, a disputed document was publicly available and credibly reported as an alleged or purported Epstein suicide note. That is not enough. The full proposition remains more likely unproved than proved, and the council should answer no.
