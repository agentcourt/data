CFR analysis quoted Shannon K. O'Neil: "Maduro is gone but the repressive elements of the regime are still there and in control. It is a huge country, so it is hard to control territorially," and noted that armed groups including military, secret police, colectivos, and National Liberation Army militias would need to be brought to heel for U.S.-friendly leaders to gain physical control of the streets, a basic prerequisite to governing.

Since early September 2025, Trump mounted an escalating pressure campaign against the Maduro regime, which U.S. officials accused of leading a drug cartel that the State Department designated a foreign terrorist organization in November. Washington significantly increased its air and naval presence in the region as part of Operation Southern Spear, a U.S. military campaign that it says targets drug trafficking in the Caribbean. It authorized more than twenty lethal strikes on alleged drug trafficking boats.

The Trump administration framed the operation as necessary to curb the flow of drugs from Latin America to the United States, but some experts said the campaign's scope and intensity went beyond counternarcotics objectives, long raising the possibility of a broader effort to force regime change in Venezuela. Other heightened U.S. pressure on Venezuela, including a naval blockade on all sanctioned oil tankers into the country, had also raised concerns about escalation and broader regional instability.

Section: What is Operation Southern Spear? On November 13, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth announced the formal launch of Operation Southern Spear, which he said was aimed at targeting "narco-terrorist" organizations and disrupting illegal drug trafficking in the Western Hemisphere. The Pentagon described the effort as a U.S. military "counter-narco-terrorism campaign" intended to safeguard U.S. national security.

Hegseth's announcement came shortly after the Senate voted down a bipartisan war power resolution that would have blocked the Trump administration from using military force within or against Venezuela without congressional authorization. Another vote occurred on December 17, in which the U.S. House of Representatives rejected a pair of Democrat-backed resolutions that would have reined in Trump's military campaign toward Venezuela.

Section: How is the operation being carried out? Before the United States' January 3 strike and capture of Maduro, the operation, led by U.S. Southern Command and Joint Task Force Southern Spear, involved a regional buildup of extensive air and naval assets, including bombers, drones, aircraft carriers, and amphibious assault ships.

The effort was later expanded into a broader, formal military operation that the United States said was focused on interdicting drug cartels in the Caribbean and Eastern Pacific.

CFR quoted Roxanna Vigil as noting that when it came to the stated goals of disrupting illegal drug trafficking and dismantling "narco-terrorist" networks in the Western Hemisphere, there was "a lot of mismatch," because the United States was using "a huge naval deployment against small, little drug boats." CFR also noted that approximately fifteen thousand U.S. military personnel reportedly had been deployed in 2025, one of the largest U.S. military buildups in the region since the Cold War.
