Source: Brookings Institution, Making sense of the US military operation in Venezuela. URL: https://www.brookings.edu/articles/making-sense-of-the-us-military-operation-in-venezuela/

Extracted relevant page text:
- Brookings states: On January 3, 2026, the United States launched a special operation to exfiltrate Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro and his wife from Caracas.
- Scott R. Anderson writes that the operation to capture Maduro underscores the broad authority modern presidents exercise over military force and potentially its limits.
- Brookings notes the executive branch position that the president has constitutional authority to use military force without congressional authorization if supporting U.S. interests and not expected to result in prolonged and substantial military engagements.
- Anderson writes it is not surprising for the administration to argue that the president can take targeted military action like the Maduro operation, even where consequential for Venezuela and contrary to international law.
- Brookings records Secretary Rubio's response when asked why congressional authorization was not needed: This was not an invasion, we did not occupy a country. Brookings treats that as suggesting broader campaigns might exceed unilateral authority.
- Brookings states a second wave of the sort threatened by President Trump still might encounter limits.
- Brookings separately states that Maduro is gone and what comes next is unclear; maintaining Delcy Rodríguez indefinitely would undermine the expressed will of Venezuelans, and threatening force to keep the Rodríguez government in line may work in the short term but is not sustainable.
