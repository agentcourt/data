### Openings

[plaintiff] Council members, the plaintiff’s case is straightforward, but it is source-sensitive. The proposition does not turn on whether the United States applied pressure to Venezuela, whether strikes or naval activity occurred, or whether Venezuelan proxy forces acted. It turns on a narrower question: did U.S. ground forces physically enter Venezuelan sovereign territory in combat capacity, before the January 31, 2026 expiration, in an operation whose objective was to seize or hold territory, remove the sitting government, or establish operational control of a major population center?

At this opening stage the AAR record is empty. There is no admitted case-packet evidence, no submitted source material, and no exhibit whose custody or provenance can yet be weighed. That matters. Plaintiff will not ask the council to decide from headlines, snippets, or counsel’s private investigation. The proof that should decide this case is the proof named in the proposition itself: official Department of Defense or White House confirmation, with United Nations Security Council records as the binding fallback if official U.S. statements conflict.

The real dispute will likely be over characterization, not merely geography. Physical entry by U.S. personnel into Venezuela is necessary, but not alone sufficient if the record shows only an excluded category: a targeted strike, naval blockade, cyber operation, proxy action, or a special operations raid without a territorial objective. The edge case is especially important: capture of a head of state without territorial occupation resolves NO under this market. Plaintiff therefore expects to carry the burden by showing not just boots on Venezuelan soil, but combat-capacity ground forces tied to an objective within the proposition: removal of the sitting government or operational control/territorial control, rather than a freestanding raid.

The evidence we will seek to place before you in the next phase should answer four questions. First, source: is there an official DoD or White House statement confirming deployment of U.S. ground forces, and if any official sources conflict, what do Security Council records say? Second, location and personnel: did U.S. personnel, not only proxy forces or remote systems, cross into Venezuelan sovereign territory? Third, capacity: were those personnel operating as combat ground forces rather than merely advisers, evacuees, or remote-support personnel? Fourth, objective: did the operation aim at one of the qualifying territorial or governmental-control objectives, and does the official record avoid the market’s express exclusions?

Because the current record contains no admitted evidence, provenance issues are for later source admissions. When evidence is offered, the council should give greatest weight to primary official records preserved with dates, retrieval provenance, and faithful text or transcript captures. Secondary reporting, if any, should be used only to locate and test those primary materials. The plaintiff’s position is that the decisive official materials will satisfy the preponderance standard for YES, but the council should require the source chain and the mission-objective link before crediting that conclusion.

[defendant] Council members, the defense begins with the narrow terms of the proposition. This case is not about whether the United States opposed Venezuela’s government, used force in the region, supported partners, imposed pressure, conducted strikes, or generated international controversy. It resolves YES only if the plaintiff proves a much more specific event: U.S. ground forces physically entered Venezuelan sovereign territory in combat capacity before January 31, 2026, and did so with a qualifying territorial or governmental-control objective.

The current AAR record matters. After inspection, there is no admitted evidence, no case-packet file, no submitted source material, and no exhibit. The plaintiff’s opening properly acknowledges that the burden is source-sensitive, but it does not itself prove any of the required facts. At this stage the council has no official Department of Defense statement, no White House confirmation, and no Security Council record admitted as evidence. The plaintiff therefore has not yet supplied the source foundation that the proposition itself requires.

The defense theory is straightforward: each element is independent, and a missing link defeats YES. First, the source must be an official DoD or White House confirmation, with UN Security Council records becoming binding only if those official U.S. sources conflict. Second, the confirmation must establish U.S. personnel physically entering Venezuelan territory, not merely aircraft, ships, cyber tools, advisers outside the country, or proxy forces acting under U.S. direction. Third, those personnel must be ground forces in a combat capacity. Fourth, the mission objective must be one of the qualifying objectives: seizing or holding territory, removing the sitting government, or establishing operational control of a major population center. Fifth, it must have occurred by the expiration date.

The exclusions are not footnotes; they are central. Targeted strikes resolve NO. Naval blockades resolve NO. Cyber operations resolve NO. Special-operations raids without territorial objectives resolve NO. Proxy forces resolve NO unless U.S. personnel themselves are physically present. Even capture of a head of state without territorial occupation is expressly carved out as NO. Those carveouts prevent the council from collapsing a dramatic military or diplomatic episode into this proposition unless the record proves the specific ground-entry and territorial-objective requirements.

The strongest possible plaintiff case will likely try to turn a reported U.S. action in Venezuela into proof of boots on the ground and a government-removal objective. The defense will test that characterization against the official source chain and the market language. A public controversy, a Security Council debate, or a broad phrase such as “action in Venezuela” would not be enough unless the admitted record connects it to U.S. ground troops, combat capacity, Venezuelan sovereign territory, and a qualifying objective.

When evidence is later offered, the council should weigh provenance and custody carefully: dated official pages or faithful captures should matter most; secondary reporting should be useful only as a path to primary sources; and any source-access gaps, conflicting statements, or ambiguous mission descriptions should reduce weight. On this record, the disciplined starting point is NO unless and until plaintiff admits primary evidence satisfying every required element by a preponderance.

### Arguments

[plaintiff] Council members, the plaintiff now offers the official source proof the openings identified as decisive.

The controlling source requirement is satisfied by PX-1, an official War.gov/Department of War article. It reports President Trump’s Jan. 3, 2026 announcement of a successful overnight joint U.S. military extraction in Caracas. It quotes Trump saying that, at his direction, “the United States armed forces conducted an extraordinary military operation in the capital of Venezuela” against a fortified military fortress in Caracas. PX-1 further states that Operation Absolute Resolve involved U.S. joint forces, including special operations forces from multiple service branches, and that more than 150 military aircraft provided cover for the “ground-based extraction force in Caracas.” That is official U.S. confirmation of U.S. ground personnel physically inside Venezuelan sovereign territory, in combat capacity, before the Jan. 31 expiration.

The objective element is also met by a preponderance. PX-1 does not describe a mere remote strike, blockade, cyber operation, or proxy action. It describes a U.S. military operation in Caracas that captured Maduro, after which he was “out of power.” More importantly, PX-1 records the President saying the U.S. would “oversee Venezuela until a safe transition to a legitimate replacement” could be identified and that a group of people was “running [Venezuela]” until it could be put back on track. That official language ties the ground operation to removal of the sitting ruler and temporary operational control over the governmental transition. The proposition does not require permanent occupation; it requires a qualifying objective. Removing the sitting government and controlling the transition are qualifying objectives.

PX-2, the White House Rubio release, supplies both corroboration and the best adverse context. It repeatedly characterizes the event as a “raid,” “capture,” and “arrest,” and Rubio says it was not an invasion or extended military operation. Plaintiff does not hide that language. But those words do not defeat YES. The market’s required event is not “invasion” or “extended war.” It is ground forces entering Venezuelan territory in combat capacity with a qualifying objective. PX-2 also confirms the broader policy objective: Venezuela was to “move in a certain direction”; the U.S. retained options after “this raid and this capture and this arrest” until “changes are made”; and continued U.S. pressure would remain until the U.S.-specified conditions were addressed.

The defense will lean on the edge case: capture of a head of state without territorial occupation is NO. The admitted record is materially stronger than that edge case. PX-1 says Maduro was out of power, identifies a replacement-transition process, and says the U.S. would oversee and have people running Venezuela in the interim. PX-2’s denial of “invasion” limits the form and duration of the operation, but it does not erase the ground-based force in Caracas or the official transition-control statements. This was not just a person seized and flown away with no territorial or governmental consequence; it was a U.S. operation in Venezuela’s capital that removed the ruler and asserted operational control over what came next.

What the evidence proves: official U.S. sources confirm U.S. armed forces conducted Operation Absolute Resolve in Caracas; U.S. ground forces were present through the ground-based extraction force; the event occurred before expiration; and official statements link the operation to Maduro’s removal from power and U.S. oversight of the transition. What it does not prove: permanent occupation, prolonged invasion, or seizure of all Venezuelan territory. Those are not required. The record’s provenance is strong: both exhibits are official U.S. government sources, submitted with URLs, retrieval timestamps, and hashes. There is no DoD/White House conflict requiring resort to UNSC fallback.

On this record, the proposition resolves YES by a preponderance.

[defendant] Council members, the plaintiff’s own official exhibits defeat the proposition. The defense does not need to dispute that PX-1 and PX-2 are official U.S. sources, or that PX-1 describes some U.S. personnel in Caracas. The decisive issue is the market’s limiting language: capture of a head of state without territorial occupation is expressly NO, and special-operations raids without territorial objectives are also NO.

Start with what PX-1 proves. It describes “the capture” of Nicolas Maduro after an overnight joint U.S. military extraction in Caracas. It quotes President Trump saying the operation was against a fortified military fortress “to bring outlaw dictator Nicolas Maduro to justice.” It says Maduro and his wife would face criminal court proceedings tied to a U.S. indictment. It describes Operation Absolute Resolve as a joint military and law-enforcement mission “to capture the Maduros,” involving special operations forces, aircraft cover, and a “ground-based extraction force in Caracas.” Secretary Hegseth called it a “massive joint military and law enforcement raid.” Those words establish a capture/extraction/arrest mission, not an operation whose objective was to seize or hold territory or establish operational control of Caracas.

PX-2 makes the limiting characterization even clearer. The White House release says the action was “not an invasion” and “not an extended military operation.” It calls the event “this raid and this capture and this arrest.” It discusses continuing U.S. pressure, oil quarantine, sanctions-related vessel seizures, drug-boat targeting, and policy demands. That is not territorial occupation. It is coercive policy pressure after a raid.

The proposition’s edge case was written for exactly this dispute: “Capture of a head of state without territorial occupation: NO.” Plaintiff tries to avoid that clause by pointing to PX-1’s post-capture statements that Maduro was out of power and that the U.S. would “oversee Venezuela” until a transition. But the event element is not “a raid followed by U.S. political influence over a transition.” It is ground troops entering Venezuela with the objective of seizing or holding territory, removing the sitting government, or establishing operational control of a major population center. The admitted sources identify the operational objective as capture, extraction, arrest, and bringing Maduro to justice. They do not state that U.S. ground forces seized, held, occupied, administered, or controlled Caracas or any Venezuelan territory.

Nor does “removing the sitting government” save plaintiff’s case on this record. PX-2 says Maduro “was not the head of state” and “was an illegitimate president.” PX-1 says Vice President Delcy Rodriguez was just sworn in following Maduro’s ouster and was willing to work with the U.S. The admitted evidence therefore shows, at most, the capture of a person the U.S. described as illegitimate, followed by pressure on a successor or transition. That is precisely why the edge case separates capture from territorial occupation and resolves the former NO.

What the evidence proves: official U.S. sources describe a Jan. 3, 2026 U.S. military/law-enforcement raid in Caracas involving a ground-based extraction force and Maduro’s capture. What it does not prove: territorial occupation; seizure or holding of territory; operational control of a major population center; or that the ground-entry objective was anything beyond capture/extraction/arrest. Provenance and custody give these official exhibits weight, but that weight favors applying the exclusions, because the adverse limiting language is in the official sources themselves. There is no DoD/White House conflict requiring a UN fallback.

By a preponderance, plaintiff has shown an excluded raid and capture, not the qualifying territorial or governmental-control operation required for YES. The proposition should resolve NO.

### Rebuttals

[plaintiff] Council members, the defense correctly identifies the hardest issue, but it reads the exclusion too broadly and the official record too narrowly.

The edge case says capture of a head of state without territorial occupation resolves NO. Plaintiff’s case is not that every capture of Maduro is automatically YES. The point is that this record does not stop at capture. PX-1 says the U.S. armed forces conducted a military operation in Venezuela’s capital; that the mission used a ground-based extraction force in Caracas; that Maduro was then “out of power”; and that the U.S. would oversee Venezuela until a safe transition, with a group of people “running [Venezuela]” in the interim. Those are not stray political comments unrelated to the event. They are the official explanation of what Operation Absolute Resolve accomplished and what control followed it.

The defense tries to make “raid,” “capture,” and “arrest” mutually exclusive with a qualifying objective. They are not. A raid can be the method. Capture can be the immediate tactical action. Arrest can be the law-enforcement disposition. The proposition asks for the objective of the military operation. PX-1’s stated results and transition-control language show that the operation’s objective was not merely to seize a body and leave Venezuela unchanged; it was to remove Maduro from power and place the transition under U.S. oversight.

Nor does plaintiff need to prove permanent occupation of territory. The event definition is disjunctive: seizing or holding territory, removing the sitting government, or establishing operational control of a major population center. Defense focuses on the first and third forms while minimizing the second. If U.S. ground forces enter Caracas in combat capacity, remove the ruler, and official sources say the U.S. will oversee the country’s transition, that satisfies at least the “removing the sitting government” prong. The temporary-running language also supports operational control, but the removal prong alone is enough.

PX-2’s adverse wording does not change that. “Not an invasion” and “not an extended military operation” limit the scale and duration. They do not deny physical ground entry, combat capacity, Caracas location, or removal from power. PX-2 also says the U.S. retained options after the raid until “changes are made” and wanted Venezuela to move in a U.S.-specified direction. That corroborates, rather than negates, the transition-control objective.

Finally, defense’s reliance on Rubio’s statement that Maduro was not the legitimate head of state should not control the market’s “sitting government” language. The record shows he was the Venezuelan leader captured in Caracas and then out of power; the U.S. cannot avoid the removal prong by labeling the target illegitimate after removing him.

The official sources have strong provenance and no source conflict. Taken together, they prove more than an excluded bare capture: U.S. ground forces entered Caracas in combat capacity in an operation that removed Venezuela’s ruler and put the transition under U.S. oversight. That is YES by a preponderance.

### Surrebuttals

Plaintiff says raid, capture, and arrest can be the method while removal and transition control were the objective. But PX-1 itself supplies the stated operational purpose: the operation was against a fortified location “to bring” Maduro “to justice”; Maduro and his wife would face criminal proceedings; and the mission was a joint military and law-enforcement mission “to capture the Maduros.” PX-2 then calls the event “this raid and this capture and this arrest,” while also saying it was not an invasion and not an extended military operation. The market language anticipated exactly this ambiguity. It does not merely exclude “minor captures”; it says capture of a head of state without territorial occupation resolves NO. Plaintiff’s reading would erase that edge case whenever the captured person’s removal has political consequences. Every capture of a ruler can be described as affecting power or transition. The clause only has work to do if the council distinguishes the capture itself from a territorial operation.

Nor has plaintiff proven the separate “operational control of a major population center” prong. Caracas is the location of the raid, not a city the record says U.S. forces controlled. Aircraft cover for an extraction force is not operational control of Caracas. A group of people later “running Venezuela” and U.S. pressure until “changes are made” are not evidence that ground troops held the capital or administered it.

The “removing the sitting government” prong also cannot be read without the edge case. PX-2 says Maduro was not head of state; PX-1 says a vice president was sworn in and was willing to work with the United States. At most, the record shows U.S. capture of a disputed or illegitimate leader followed by influence over a successor process. Without territorial occupation or a qualifying territorial-control objective, that is the proposition’s express NO scenario, not a YES.

So even accepting plaintiff’s strongest facts, the decisive missing link remains: an official source saying the ground operation’s objective was more than capture, extraction, arrest, and pressure. The record has no such statement. It has the opposite. The proposition should resolve NO.
