## Filings

### Openings

[plaintiff] Council members, the proposition is not whether Polymarket, UMA, or any other market actor handled a resolution correctly. The question is factual: by May 24, 2026 at 11:59 PM ET, did Iran initiate a major non-weather closure of its airspace within the rule preserved in the case packet?

The admitted packet gives us the governing test. The May 24 market resolves Yes if Iran initiated a major closure of its airspace, not solely due to weather, by the listed deadline. A major closure is not every warning, reroute, foreign government advisory, airline choice, or narrow regional restriction. It must be a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, or departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. The rule also gives a concrete safe harbor: a non-weather complete suspension of commercial arrivals and departures affecting at least two of IKA, THR, MHD, SYZ, or IFN qualifies. Limited exceptions for pre-approved flights do not defeat an otherwise broad closure.

That framing matters because the real dispute is likely to be source-chain and breadth, not abstract regional risk. Plaintiff expects the proof to focus on whether an Iranian aviation authority, rather than a foreign regulator or private airline, issued or maintained an order before the deadline; whether the order was security or military related rather than weather; whether its effective period reached the May 24 ET cutoff; and whether its geographic and airport effects were broad enough under the rule.

The present case packet is candid about its limits. It preserves the market rule and identifies official Iranian aviation authorities and consensus credible reporting as the primary source classes, but it does not yet contain a merits packet proving the actual closure event. It also identifies the unresolved gaps: no admitted Iranian authority record, NOTAM, airport operations record, contemporaneous reporting packet, exact ET timing analysis, or definitive explanation of whether observed cancellations or route changes were Iran-initiated rather than foreign or airline-initiated. Those gaps affect weight, not the legal test.

Plaintiff’s theory is that if the record in the next phase admits an attributable Iranian aviation order or NOTAM closing western Iranian airspace through May 25, and if that order suspended ordinary commercial arrivals/departures or access across a major Iranian region or at least two listed airports before May 24 at 11:59 PM ET, the proposition is satisfied by a preponderance. Such a closure would be non-weather, Iran-initiated, and major even if limited pre-approved flights were permitted.

Plaintiff also recognizes the strongest opposing theory. Defendant may show that there was no new official Iranian NOTAM or order, that any western-flight suspension merely preexisted the market window and was not initiated by the deadline, that reports were mistaken or unattributed, that only foreign warnings or airline reroutes occurred, or that the restriction was too narrow to qualify. If the best admitted sources show that, plaintiff should not prevail.

So the opening burden is practical: follow the rule to the primary sources. The decisive evidence should be the official Iranian aviation record or NOTAM if available, airport-level operations evidence for the listed airports, credible contemporaneous reporting with exact attribution and timing, and any adverse official denial or correction. On that record, plaintiff will ask the council to find that a broad, non-weather, Iran-initiated closure before the deadline is more likely than not.

[defendant] Council members, defendant’s opening is simple: the present record does not yet prove the proposition. The market rule is demanding on purpose. It does not ask whether the region was tense, whether carriers avoided Iran, whether foreign governments issued warnings, or whether a market resolution was disputed. It asks whether, by May 24, 2026 at 11:59 PM ET, Iran itself initiated a major non-weather closure within the rule.

The case packet gives the operative test. A qualifying event must be Iran-initiated, not solely weather-related, timely, and major. “Major” is defined as a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, and departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. The rule’s safe harbor is also specific: a non-weather complete suspension of commercial arrivals and departures affecting at least two of IKA, THR, MHD, SYZ, or IFN qualifies. Limited exceptions do not defeat an otherwise broad closure, but limited disruptions, foreign warnings, airline reroutes, narrow regional restrictions, or military-drill notices do not become a Yes merely because they occurred in a dangerous environment.

That distinction is the defense case. Plaintiff must prove the source chain and the scope, not just point to atmosphere. The primary-evidence and official-source packet files expressly say that no merits packet has been prebuilt. The unresolved-gaps file is candid: the record lacks an admitted Iranian aviation authority record, NOTAM, airport operations record, contemporaneous reporting packet, exact ET timing analysis, and proof distinguishing Iran-initiated restrictions from airline or foreign-government conduct. Those are not clerical gaps. They are the elements of the proposition.

Defendant has already looked for the likely path of proof. The obvious targets are an official Iranian AIM/CAA/CAO NOTAM or order, airport-level operations evidence for the listed airports, and credible reporting that preserves exact attribution, timing, and scope. Public leads appear to point toward an alleged late-May restriction involving the western part of the Tehran FIR, exceptions for named airports, daylight-only operations, and fresh permissions. But until a reliable full source is admitted, that remains only a lead. Even if such a notice is later admitted, the council will still need to decide what it actually closed: airspace or airports; all commercial operations or only selected permissions; a major Iranian region or a narrower western-sector measure; and whether at least two listed airports were completely suspended rather than operating under exceptions.

Timing also matters. The deadline is May 24 at 11:59 PM ET, so the record must fix the relevant effective period against that cutoff. A notice valid into May 25 UTC may or may not satisfy the ET deadline depending on the exact timestamps and conversions. A report saying “until May 25” is not enough without the underlying time fields or a source capable of supporting them.

The strongest plaintiff case would be an authenticated Iranian aviation notice or order, preserved in the record, showing a non-weather security closure broad enough under the rule and effective through the deadline. If that is admitted and the exceptions do not undercut breadth or the safe harbor, defendant will confront it directly. But if the evidence instead shows only foreign advisories, private airline avoidance, flight-tracking gaps, regional risk, limited airport-hour restrictions, permission requirements, or unattributed media summaries, then plaintiff has not carried a preponderance burden.

So defendant asks the council to keep the burden anchored to the rule and the admitted record. This case should turn on primary source attribution, exact timing, and commercial-flight scope. On the opening record, those decisive facts remain unproven.

### Arguments

[plaintiff] Council members, the preponderance now favors Yes.

PX-1 supplies the rule. It asks whether, by May 24, 2026 at 11:59 PM ET, Iran initiated a non-weather major closure of its airspace. A major closure includes a broad closure, cancellation, or suspension of commercial flights transiting, arriving in, or departing from Iranian airspace or a major Iranian airspace region. Limited exceptions to a broad closure do not disqualify it.

The admitted source extracts prove the event. PX-2 reports that Iran temporarily closed the airspace over its western half from May 22 to May 25, suspending all civil flight permits in the western sector of the Tehran FIR. It identifies the instrument as Iranian Civil Aviation Authority NOTAM OIIX A1010/26, issued Friday, May 22, ordering the shutdown of all airports in the western part of the Tehran FIR, effective immediately and valid until May 25. It further reports that only eight airports remained operational, including Mehrabad, Imam Khomeini, Isfahan, Shiraz, and Mashhad, and even those flights were limited to daylight hours and required fresh per-flight Civil Aviation Authority approval.

PX-3 supplies the exact timing and confirms scope. It identifies the same NOTAM, OIIX A1010/26, and states that all airports in the western portion of the Tehran Flight Information Region were closed except limited daylight operations at designated facilities. It gives the effective period as May 22 at 20:46Z until May 25 at 08:30Z. The market deadline, May 24 at 11:59 PM ET, is May 25 at 03:59Z during U.S. daylight time. The NOTAM period therefore covered the deadline.

PX-4 and PX-5 corroborate the core facts. PX-4 reports NOTAM A1010/26 closed all airports in the western Tehran FIR except a few limited-operation airports; all previous permissions were canceled and airlines had to obtain new Civil Aviation Organization authorization for IFR passenger flights. PX-5 identifies NOTAM OIIX A1010/26 as closing airports across the western Tehran FIR, with only limited daylight operations and fresh Civil Aviation Authority approval.

Those facts satisfy each element. Iran initiated the restriction through its aviation authority. The cause was security and military-risk related, not weather. The timing reached the market cutoff. The closure was major because it covered the western sector of the Tehran FIR, described by PX-2 as Iran's western half and by the other reports as the western part of the Tehran Flight Information Region. The rule does not require a total national shutdown; a major Iranian airspace region is enough. Nor do limited exceptions defeat the result. Here, even the exceptions were narrow: daylight-only operations, suspended prior permissions, and fresh per-flight approval for passenger operations.

The defense is right to ask for source discipline. Plaintiff did not capture the original official NOTAM, and the admitted evidence is secondary reporting. That affects weight. But the reports are mutually reinforcing on the identifier, issuer, dates, geography, civil-flight suspension, and approval regime. Their agreement on the same NOTAM number and operational details is more probative than generic market chatter or foreign-airline avoidance would be.

Nor does the partial-closure objection defeat the claim. The plaintiff need not prove every Iranian airport was shut. PX-1 expressly allows a major airspace region, and the western Tehran FIR restriction was broad enough: airports in that region were closed, civil permits were suspended, and passenger operations required new Iranian approval. That is exactly the kind of Iran-initiated non-weather suspension the rule describes.

On this record, it is more likely than not that by the May 24 ET deadline Iran had initiated a major non-weather closure of its airspace within the market rule. The council should find the proposition true.

[defendant] Council members, plaintiff has now submitted useful leads, but not enough reliable proof to carry the rule as written.

Start with what the record proves. PX-1 requires an Iran-initiated, non-weather, timely, major closure. It also tells us what does not count: limited cancellations, delays, other partial closures, and restrictions imposed by airlines or countries other than Iran. The safe harbor is specific: complete suspension of commercial arrivals and departures affecting at least two named airports.

Plaintiff's new exhibits probably show that some Iranian aviation restriction existed in late May. PX-2 through PX-5 consistently refer to NOTAM OIIX A1010/26 or A1010/26, a May 22-May 25 period, western Tehran FIR restrictions, daylight-only operations at exceptions, and fresh approval requirements. Defendant does not ask the council to ignore that consistency.

But the proposition is not "was there a restriction?" It is whether Iran initiated a major closure of its airspace within the rule. On that question the record remains short.

First, there is still no original official instrument. PX-2 expressly says the NOTAM information was reported by Gulf News; PX-3, PX-4, and PX-5 are also secondary reports and do not reproduce the NOTAM. The case packet itself says primary resolution sources are official Iranian aviation information and credible reporting, and the official-source file told counsel to seek official Iranian records or NOTAM material. Defendant's DEF-1 official AIS check confirms the official source path exists and shows current official NOTAM/warning pages, but no accessible A1010/26 page was found through that check. DEF-1 is not proof the expired NOTAM never existed, but it underscores the custody problem: the council is being asked to decide the exact legal effect of a NOTAM whose original text is absent.

Second, plaintiff's own exhibits blur airspace closure with airport and permission restrictions. PX-2 uses broad language about airspace over Iran's western half, but then says the instrument ordered the shutdown of airports in the western part of the Tehran FIR. PX-3 says all airports in the western portion were closed to operations except limited facilities. PX-4 says restrictions and closure of airports in western Iran. PX-5 says closure of airports across the western section. The rule can be satisfied by a major Iranian airspace region, but not every airport-hour or permit restriction is automatically a broad airspace closure. Without the underlying NOTAM text, the council cannot know whether the operative legal restriction closed routes/airspace to transiting, arriving, and departing commercial flights, or instead regulated airport operations and prior permissions at affected airports.

Third, the exception evidence is not just a minor detail. PX-2 says only eight airports across the country remained operational, including Mehrabad, Imam Khomeini, Isfahan, Shiraz, and Mashhad. Those are five of the listed safe-harbor airports or their named equivalents. PX-3 lists OITR, OIKK, OIAA, OISS, OIYY, OICC, OIGG, and OIAW as selected sites. PX-4 lists Tabriz, Kermanshah, Ahvaz, Shiraz, Yazd, Kerman, Kish, and Abadan. These lists do not match. If the listed airports were operating during daylight with fresh approval, plaintiff has not proved a complete suspension of arrivals and departures at two listed airports. If only western airports were closed while multiple major airports remained open under permission, plaintiff must prove the alternative route: that this was still a broad closure of commercial flights across a major Iranian airspace region. The present article extracts do not let the council make that line-drawing confidently.

Fourth, the timing is plausible but not cleanly sourced. PX-3 gives 20:46Z May 22 to 08:30Z May 25, which covers the ET deadline. PX-4 gives a different start and end formulation in Iran time. Both may still cover the deadline, but the discrepancy reinforces why the original NOTAM matters.

Finally, no admitted airport operations record or flight-tracking evidence shows what actually happened at IKA, THR, MHD, SYZ, or IFN at the deadline, and no official Iranian source or authenticated NOTAM fixes whether the restriction was security-related in the precise way reported. Plaintiff's reports are enough to justify further investigation; they are not enough to transform an ambiguous western-FIR airport restriction with exceptions into a preponderant finding of a major airspace closure.

The defense position is narrow. If the original NOTAM were admitted and showed a broad commercial airspace closure through May 25, plaintiff's case would be stronger. But on this record, the decisive source is missing, the descriptions conflict, and the safe-harbor airport facts are adverse or unresolved. The council should find that plaintiff has not proven the proposition by a preponderance.

### Rebuttals

[plaintiff] Council members, the defense argument proves less than it needs to prove.

Plaintiff accepts the real weakness: the original NOTAM text has not been captured. But defendant's new AIS exhibit, PX-R1, does not fill that gap against plaintiff. It is a current public-site check performed on June 30. It reports a valid-NOTAM page updated June 29, a June safety warning, and a different current NOTAM page. It says searches for A1010/26 returned no results. But its own description is careful: this is not a complete historical NOTAM archive, and it does not establish that an expired May 2026 NOTAM never existed. That is exactly right. A current valid-NOTAM page's failure to expose an expired May 22-May 25 notice does not outweigh four mutually reinforcing reports identifying the same NOTAM number, issuer, date range, geography, and approval regime.

The defense also tries to turn the absence of the original NOTAM into a categorical bar. The standard is preponderance, not best evidence beyond all doubt. PX-2 through PX-5 are not generic rumors. They converge on OIIX A1010/26; Iranian Civil Aviation Authority or Civil Aviation Organization attribution; May 22-May 25 validity; western Tehran FIR scope; closure of airports in that region; suspension of prior permissions; daylight-only exceptions; and fresh Iranian approval for passenger operations. That consistency is probative.

The airport-versus-airspace point is also overstated. The rule expressly covers a major Iranian airspace region. The Tehran FIR is the relevant Iranian flight information region, and the western sector of that FIR is a major airspace region. A restriction closing airports across that region, suspending civil flight permits, and requiring new CAA approval for IFR passenger operations is a suspension of commercial arrivals and departures in that region. The rule does not require magic words or a nationwide closure.

Nor must plaintiff win under the safe harbor. The safe harbor for complete suspension at two listed airports is sufficient, not exclusive. The main rule covers a broad closure or suspension in a major region. So the defense's point that some listed airports had daylight/fresh-approval operations does not defeat plaintiff's route to Yes. PX-1 also says limited exceptions do not disqualify a broad closure. Here the exceptions prove the rule: ordinary permissions were canceled, and operations could occur only by new Iranian approval under time-limited conditions.

The alleged conflicts in airport lists do not undermine the shared core. Different articles summarized different permitted airports or examples, but all plaintiff exhibits point to the same central fact: western Tehran FIR operations were broadly closed or suspended, with narrow exceptions. The decisive question is not whether every secondary article listed every airport identically; it is whether the record makes an Iran-initiated major regional non-weather suspension more likely than not.

Finally, timing is not meaningfully uncertain. PX-3 gives May 22 20:46Z to May 25 08:30Z. The market deadline was May 25 03:59Z. That covers the cutoff. PX-2, PX-4, and PX-5 independently place the restriction through May 25.

After rebuttal, the case is still simple: defendant has shown a custody gap, not a factual negation. The admitted reports, read together under the market rule, prove by a preponderance that Iran initiated a major non-weather closure before the deadline.
