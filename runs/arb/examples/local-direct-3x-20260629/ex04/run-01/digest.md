## Filings

### Openings

[plaintiff] Council members, this case is not about whether Israel annexed Syria, occupied all of Syria, or began a conventional war with Damascus. The market rule asks a narrower question: within the stated 2024 window, did Israel commence a military offensive intended to establish control over any portion of Syria? On a preponderance standard, the answer should be Yes if the evidence shows Israeli forces moved into Syrian territory, took or held positions there, and did so with an announced purpose to control those positions until Israel's security conditions were met.

The present record is limited but important. The case-packet rules, admitted as ev_54607018b65c_market-rules, define the operative test and preserve Polymarket's disputed Yes rationale. That packet says the Yes rationale rested on two linked points: first, credible reporting had already placed Israeli forces on the Syrian peak of Mount Hermon on December 8, 2024; second, on December 17, Prime Minister Netanyahu visited the Syrian side of Mount Hermon and announced that Israeli forces would remain on the peak until another arrangement guaranteed Israel's security. The packet further notes that the Syrian peak of Mount Hermon is not part of the Golan Heights. If supported by the underlying sources, those facts fit the rule: an armed entry into Syrian territory, followed by continued military presence and a stated intent to remain until a new security arrangement, is control over a portion of Syria.

The real dispute will likely be over characterization, not geography alone. The respondent may argue that Israel described the move as temporary and defensive, and therefore not an invasion or not an offensive. But the rule does not require permanence, annexation, civilian administration, or conquest of a large region. A temporary military seizure can still be an operation intended to establish control for as long as the controlling power's security conditions require. Defensive motive may explain why Israel acted; it does not negate that the action sought to take and hold positions beyond Israel's side of the line.

The decisive proof should therefore be official Israeli statements, UN/UNDOF materials on the Syria-Israel separation framework, and credible reporting that fixes the dates, location, and scope of Israeli deployment. The official-source path matters because the market rules expressly privilege official confirmation by Israel, Syria, the United Nations, or a permanent Security Council member, while also allowing a consensus of credible reporting. In the next evidentiary phase, the plaintiff expects to submit the underlying official statements and corroborating reporting rather than ask the council to rely on the packet's summary alone.

For now, the admitted packet proves the governing standard and identifies the precise theory of Yes: Israeli forces reached and held Syrian Mount Hermon during the market window, and Israel's own announced posture was to remain there until a security arrangement was found. It does not, by itself, fully prove every underlying event independent of the rationale; that is why the source chain must be completed. But it shows the dispute's center of gravity and why plaintiff's burden is realistic: the question is whether Israel established military control over any Syrian portion, and the record already points to a specific place, date range, official actor, and control-intent statement that would satisfy that test.

[defendant] Council members, the defendant's position begins with the market's exact words. This is not a general referendum on whether Israel acted in Syria, violated a disengagement arrangement, conducted airstrikes, or moved troops after the Assad government's collapse. The proposition resolves Yes only if Israel commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over a portion of Syria during the stated window. The Golan Heights are expressly counted as Israeli territory for this market, so location, purpose, and control intent are not side issues; they are the elements.

The current admitted record is thin. The only evidence is ev_54607018b65c_market-rules, the case-packet markdown. It proves the market question, the date range, the Golan carveout, the sources the council may use, and Polymarket's disputed Yes rationale. It does not itself authenticate the underlying Israeli statement, UN record, reporting, map, military order, geolocation, or chain of custody for the events described in the rationale. Plaintiff fairly acknowledges that the source chain must be completed. Until it is, the packet is a roadmap to a dispute, not proof that each factual element has been met.

The core dispute should be framed in three parts. First, what exact territory is at issue? The packet's rationale points to Mount Hermon and says the Syrian peak is not part of the Golan Heights. Plaintiff must prove the location actually occupied or held was outside the market's Golan carveout, and must distinguish the Golan, the 1974 area of separation or buffer zone, and any Syrian peak with reliable geography. Ambiguous references to the border, the Golan, or Mount Hermon will not be enough.

Second, what action occurred? A military presence, patrol, seizure of abandoned positions, construction of obstacles, or even a ceasefire violation is not automatically the same as commencing a military offensive. The market chose that phrase, and it requires more than showing that soldiers were somewhere controversial. The council should look for official or well-corroborated evidence of the nature, timing, scope, and purpose of any ground operation, not merely labels in commentary.

Third, what intent was shown? Plaintiff's theory depends on the phrase that forces would remain until another security arrangement was found. But a temporary defensive posture pending a security arrangement can support a different inference: an emergency border-security deployment after the collapse of the neighboring regime, not an offensive intended to establish control over Syrian territory in the market sense. The defendant does not ask the council to accept that inference now as proven; we ask only that the plaintiff be held to the burden of proving the opposite with admitted evidence.

The evidence that would matter most in the next phase is predictable: official Israeli statements from the relevant dates, UN or UNDOF materials describing the area of separation and any IDF presence, reliable maps fixing the legal and geographic status of the specific peak or positions, and credible reporting only where it supplies source-level detail rather than conclusory wording. Adverse material should be considered too: if UN documents show a violation, that may prove presence or treaty breach, but it still may not prove an offensive or control intent; if Israeli statements emphasize temporary defense, that cuts against the market's required intent even if it does not erase the deployment.

So the defendant's opening is narrow but important. The current record proves the rules and a contested rationale. It does not yet prove invasion under those rules. Plaintiff must bridge the missing links with admissible source material on geography, action, and intent. If the later record shows only a temporary defensive deployment in or near a buffer zone, or leaves the non-Golan location and control intent uncertain, the proposition should resolve No.

### Arguments

[plaintiff] Council members, the proposition is satisfied by a preponderance of the admitted record.

Start with the rule. The case packet, ev_54607018b65c_market-rules, asks whether Israel commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over any portion of Syria in the window ending December 31, 2024. It does not require annexation, a declaration of war, permanent occupation, casualties, or control of a large area. It also says official confirmation by Israel or the UN, or a consensus of credible reporting, may resolve the question.

The location element is met. UNDOF evidence, ev_26cf1a5ab33e, shows that the relevant 1974 framework is a ceasefire and disengagement arrangement between Israel and Syria, with UNDOF supervising the areas of separation and limitation and observing the ceasefire line between the two countries. That matters because the later reports are not describing ordinary movement within Israel. They are describing the buffer/area-of-separation framework between Israel and Syria.

The action element is met. The Times of Israel evidence, ev_96725428f566, reports that on December 8-9, 2024 the IDF captured the Syrian side of strategic Mount Hermon and deployed to the buffer zone inside Syrian territory. It reports that the IDF's 210th Bashan Division began deploying forces to the buffer zone inside Syrian territory, including atop the Syrian side of Mount Hermon, and that this marked the first time since the 1974 agreement Israeli forces had taken up positions in the buffer zone inside Syrian territory. It also reports Defense Minister Katz ordered the IDF to complete the takeover of the buffer zone in Syria and that troops seized additional positions. That is not mere rhetoric or a stray patrol; it is an armed ground deployment to take and hold strategic positions.

The control-intent element is met. The same evidence preserves Netanyahu's statement that Israel gave the army the order to take over these positions so no hostile force would embed next to Israel's border, and that it was a temporary defensive position until a suitable arrangement was found. The official-source evidence, ev_87a2be06b06f, then shows that on December 17 Netanyahu held an assessment on the summit of Mount Hermon with defense and security chiefs, received an operational briefing, issued directives on continued IDF deployment, and said the assessment was to decide on IDF deployment in that important place until another arrangement ensured Israel's security. That is a direct statement of control: Israeli forces would remain and their deployment would be decided by Israel until Israel's security arrangement existed.

Independent corroboration confirms both geography and duration. AP evidence, ev_2aa6c6bb7f84, reports Netanyahu entered Syrian territory during a tour of the buffer zone seized by Israel after Assad's fall, said Israel would stay until a security arrangement was found, and made the comments from Mount Hermon inside Syria about 10 kilometers from the Israel-held Golan Heights. That answers defendant's geography point: the reported site was inside Syria, not simply the market-excluded Golan Heights. It also corroborates that this was a seized buffer zone and an intended continuing military presence.

The defense's best point is that Israel called the move temporary and defensive. The record supports that characterization, and plaintiff does not hide it. But it does not defeat this market. A temporary defensive takeover is still a takeover. A military force can establish control for weeks, months, or until a security arrangement is found without annexing the land. The rule asks whether Israel intended to establish control over any portion of Syria, not whether Israel intended sovereign title forever. Here, the admitted evidence uses the language of capture, takeover, deployment inside Syrian territory, seized positions, continued deployment, and staying until a security arrangement. Those are the hallmarks of military control.

Nor does the word "offensive" require a pitched battle. The admitted record describes Israeli ground forces moving into Syrian territory after Syrian soldiers abandoned positions, taking strategic ground, and fortifying or holding it to exclude hostile forces. The operation was proactive and territorial: it changed who controlled the positions. That is enough under this market's functional standard.

On provenance and weight: the official gov.il PMO page was located but direct command-line retrieval was blocked; plaintiff therefore submitted an accessible mirror that identifies the source as Israel's Prime Minister's Office and preserves the official text, and submitted AP and Times of Israel corroboration. The UNDOF direct page also had access limits, but the admitted excerpt is tied to UNDOF/UN sources and corroborates the uncontested 1974 framework. Those limitations are worth noting, but they do not create a material conflict. The evidence lines up across the case packet, an official Israeli statement as mirrored, UN framework material, and credible reporting.

The proposition should resolve Yes: between September 12 and December 31, 2024, Israel commenced a military operation that took control of at least part of Syrian territory, including the Syrian side of Mount Hermon / the Syria buffer zone, and Israeli officials said forces would remain there until Israel's security arrangement was satisfied.

[defendant] Council members, after plaintiff's evidence, the defendant does not ask you to pretend nothing happened in the UNDOF zone or on the Syrian side of Mount Hermon. The record now supports a ground deployment beyond ordinary Israeli-held Golan territory. But the market did not ask whether Israel entered a buffer zone, violated the 1974 arrangement, or held a temporary security position. It asked whether Israel commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over any portion of Syria.

That extra wording matters. Plaintiff's proof is strongest on entry and presence; it is weaker on offensive character and intent. The Times of Israel exhibit, ev_96725428f566, repeatedly preserves the limiting account: the IDF said the deployment was a defensive and temporary measure; troops were carrying out defensive operations to prevent any threat; Netanyahu said the order was to take over positions so no hostile force embedded next to Israel's border; and he called it a temporary defensive position until a suitable arrangement was found. The PMO Dec. 17 mirror, ev_87a2be06b06f, likewise says the assessment was to decide on IDF deployment until another arrangement ensured Israel's security. That is security-contingency language, not the ordinary language of an offensive to establish territorial control.

The defense evidence reinforces that distinction. CRS reports that, in a letter to the United Nations, Israel said it had taken limited and temporary measures to counter threats to its citizens, and that the IDF had deployed temporarily in a few points and in a limited capacity east of Line A. That is ev_9ac4fbc4020a. The same CRS excerpt is candidly mixed: it says Israeli forces entered the UNDOF buffer zone and notes later reporting about longer-term infrastructure and a 2025 Katz statement. But the relevant market window and the official contemporaneous characterization remain limited, temporary, and defensive.

Plaintiff's argument turns the word "control" into a synonym for any military presence or takeover of positions. That is too broad. A force can temporarily occupy a checkpoint, patrol a demilitarized zone, or hold abandoned positions to prevent infiltration without thereby commencing a military offensive intended to establish control over territory in the market's sense. If the rule had meant any incursion, violation, or armed presence, it could have said so. Instead it used the combined phrase "military offensive intended to establish control," which requires the council to weigh purpose, scope, and character, not just physical location.

The UNDOF evidence, ev_26cf1a5ab33e, proves the 1974 Israel-Syria disengagement framework and the existence of areas of separation and limitation. It does not prove the market's ultimate intent element. A violation of a disengagement agreement may be serious, but breach is not the same proposition as invasion under this market. AP's exhibit, ev_2aa6c6bb7f84, adds credible corroboration that Netanyahu was inside Syria, that Israel seized a buffer zone, and that there was international condemnation. It also uses the same security-arrangement language. The condemnation and treaty-breach context do not erase the official temporary defensive framing.

Provenance also affects weight. Plaintiff's most important official-source exhibit is not a direct gov.il capture; it is a GlobalSecurity mirror of a PMO page after direct retrieval was blocked. The UNDOF exhibit similarly relies on indexed text and corroborating snippets after access limits. I do not claim those exhibits should be ignored, and I rely on their defensive language where it is against plaintiff's interest. But when the decisive question is not whether a deployment occurred but what legal-market characterization follows, the council should be careful about stretching excerpts and secondary reporting into an intent finding broader than the sources themselves.

The strongest adverse facts are the words "captured," "takeover," "seized," and "will stay." They support plaintiff's case. But on a preponderance standard, they are not enough when the same record repeatedly says temporary, defensive, limited, security-driven, and contingent on replacement arrangements after Syrian forces abandoned positions. A temporary emergency deployment may exercise local military control as a practical matter, but the market required an offensive intended to establish control. The admitted record is at least as consistent with a defensive buffer-zone hold pending security arrangements as with an invasion meeting the rule.

Accordingly, the defendant submits that plaintiff has proven a controversial Israeli deployment, but not the proposition as written. The record should resolve No unless the council is prepared to treat every limited defensive seizure of positions in a demilitarized border area as a military offensive intended to establish control. The rule's narrower language should be enforced.

### Rebuttals

[plaintiff] Council members, defendant's argument depends on adding requirements the market did not include: permanence, non-defensive motive, and something more than a territorial military takeover. The actual rule asks whether Israel commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over any portion of Syria. It says "any portion" and it does not say "permanent," "annexation," or "non-defensive."

Defendant's own CRS exhibit, ev_9ac4fbc4020a, narrows the dispute in plaintiff's favor. It states that as the Assad regime collapsed on December 8, 2024, Israeli military forces entered the UNDOF buffer zone established by the 1974 Israel-Syria Disengagement Agreement. It then says Israel described its measures as limited and temporary, and that Netanyahu told the press Israel's "seizure of control" over the UNDOF zone was a temporary defensive position until a suitable arrangement was found. That phrase is decisive: even the defense exhibit identifies the act as a seizure of control. The adjective "temporary" limits duration; it does not erase control.

The defense says the record shows a security-contingency deployment, not an intent to establish control. But the contingency is exactly the control condition: Israel would take and hold the positions until a suitable arrangement, or until an arrangement ensuring Israel's security, was found. A force that enters Syrian territory, takes over positions, excludes others from those positions, and remains until its own security condition is satisfied is exercising and intending control over that portion for that period.

Nor does "defensive" answer "offensive." Markets about military events normally look to what happened on the ground, not whether the actor offered a defensive legal justification. The admitted record describes a proactive Israeli ground movement into the UNDOF zone/Syrian Mount Hermon after Syrian forces abandoned posts; capture, takeover, seized positions, continued deployment, and fortification/longer-term infrastructure are all in the record. That is a military operation to change control of territory. It need not have involved heavy fighting to be an offensive for this market's purpose.

Defendant is right that treaty breach alone would not prove the proposition. Plaintiff's case is not treaty breach alone. The evidence proves location inside the Israel-Syria separation framework, the Syrian side of Mount Hermon or buffer zone, entry during the market window, official Israeli statements about taking over and remaining, and credible reporting corroborating seizure and occupation. The CRS exhibit adds, rather than subtracts, that Israel's action was a seizure of control over the UNDOF zone.

Finally, provenance concerns do not create a competing factual story. Defendant relies on the same PMO/CRS/press language plaintiff preserved. The accessible PMO mirror, AP, Times of Israel, UNDOF materials, market packet, and CRS excerpt all converge on the critical facts. The only disagreement is legal characterization. Under the actual market wording and preponderance standard, a limited, temporary, defensive seizure is still a military offensive intended to establish control over a portion of Syria. The answer remains Yes.

### Surrebuttals

The issue is whether Israel commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over Syrian territory.

The CRS exhibit does not carry plaintiff as far as they say. The sentence plaintiff highlights says Netanyahu described Israel's seizure of control over the UNDOF zone as a "temporary defensive position until a suitable arrangement is found." ### Closings

[plaintiff] Council members, the question is not whether Israel annexed Syria, held all of Syria, or acted without a claimed security rationale. The market asks whether, in the 2024 window, Israel commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over any portion of Syria. On this record, the answer is Yes.

PX-4 places the relevant zone in the 1974 Israel-Syria disengagement framework, supervised by UNDOF between Israeli and Syrian forces. The defense's best point is the repeated language of temporary, limited, and defensive. But those words do not negate the market rule. Temporary control is still control. Defensive motive is still compatible with a military offensive when the act is a proactive armed movement into another side's territory to take and hold strategic positions. Limited scope is irrelevant where the rule says any portion.

The defense asks you to require something beyond entry, capture, seizure, takeover, continued deployment, and announced retention until a security arrangement. But the market did not require permanence, sovereignty, or large-scale war. It required a military offensive intended to establish control over a portion of Syria. This case turns on applying the words actually written.
