## Filings

### Openings

[plaintiff] Council members, the plaintiff's case begins with the market definition itself. The question is not whether Israel formally declared war on Syria, nor whether Israel annexed territory by December 31. It is narrower: between September 12 and December 31, 2024, did Israel commence a military offensive intended to establish control over any portion of Syria? Under the case-packet rules, the Golan Heights count as Israeli territory, so the decisive geographic line is whether the relevant Israeli operation reached Syrian territory outside that carveout.

The current record contains one case-packet exhibit, ev_54607018b65c_market-rules. Its provenance is clean for opening purposes: it is an initial case-packet file, juror-visible, text-readable, and hash-identified. It proves the governing terms and also records the market's resolution rationale. That rationale identifies the factual theory we expect the evidence to confirm: Israeli forces reached the Syrian peak of Mount Hermon on December 8, 2024; on December 17, Prime Minister Netanyahu visited the Syrian side of Mount Hermon and stated that Israeli forces would remain on the peak until another arrangement was found that guaranteed Israel's security; and the Syrian peak of Mount Hermon is not part of the Golan Heights. If proved by official confirmation or a consensus of credible reporting, those facts satisfy the proposition.

The real dispute will likely be over characterization, not timing. The defense may say the deployment was temporary, defensive, and triggered by the collapse of the Assad regime, so it was not an "invasion" or an "offensive." But the market's operative language does not require permanent annexation or aggressive motive against the Syrian state. It asks whether Israel commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over any portion of Syria. A military entry into Syrian terrain, followed by holding the peak until Israel's security conditions are met, is control in the ordinary sense. Temporary control is still control; defensive purpose does not erase an intent to hold territory.

The proof that should decide this case is therefore concrete. First, official Israeli material and statements should show where Israeli forces deployed and what Israeli leaders said about remaining there. Second, UN material, especially UNDOF records concerning the area of separation, should show whether Israeli forces were inside Syrian-side areas and whether that presence was treated as a violation of the 1974 disengagement framework. Third, credible reporting should corroborate the date, location, and status of the Mount Hermon deployment. Fourth, geographic evidence should confirm that the Syrian peak or other held positions were outside the Golan Heights carveout.

At this stage I do not ask the council to treat outside sources as admitted proof. The opening record is limited. But the case packet already identifies a specific, testable path to Yes: December 2024 Israeli military movement into the Syrian side of Mount Hermon, followed by an official statement of continued presence until a security arrangement. If the forthcoming source record bears that out, the preponderance standard is met.

[defendant] Council members, the defense starts with the one thing the current record actually proves: the governing market terms. The proposition is not simply whether Israeli forces crossed a line, and it is not whether later observers used the word “invasion.” It asks whether, by December 31, 2024, Israel commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over a portion of Syria, with the Golan Heights counted as Israeli territory.

The only admitted evidence now is ev_54607018b65c_market-rules. Its provenance as a case-packet file is clean, and it sets the rules. It also records Polymarket’s resolution rationale. But the rationale is not the same thing as the source chain that must carry the plaintiff’s burden. It does not attach the official Israeli statement, the UN record, the underlying reporting, or the geographic proof that fixes the exact status of the relevant peak or buffer-zone positions. It tells us what the administrator concluded; it does not by itself authenticate every factual link needed for this council’s independent preponderance decision.

That distinction matters because the case will likely turn on three precise questions.

First, geography. The market expressly carves the Golan Heights into Israeli territory for purposes of this proposition. The plaintiff therefore must prove that the alleged controlled area was outside that carveout, not merely near the Golan, on Mount Hermon generally, or in a border/security zone described imprecisely by reporters.

Second, the nature of the operation. The word “offensive” does work in this market. A temporary defensive deployment into positions after the collapse or abandonment of Syrian military positions is not automatically a military offensive in the market sense. If the plaintiff’s evidence shows only a reactive buffer-zone deployment to prevent hostile forces from occupying adjacent positions, the council should hesitate before converting that into the market’s trigger.

Third, intent. The plaintiff must show intent to establish control over Syrian territory, not just operational presence conditioned on security arrangements. Temporary control, prolonged presence, and offensive control can overlap, but they are not identical. The decisive evidence will be the full official wording, the military directives, UN descriptions of the area of separation, reliable reporting on what was actually seized or held, and maps that place those positions relative to the Golan carveout.

The defense is not asking the council to ignore the case-packet rationale. It identifies serious questions and obvious source targets for the later evidentiary phases. But it is still derivative. In those later phases, the plaintiff should have to admit the primary or best available materials: official Israeli statements in full context, UN or UNDOF material concerning the 1974 disengagement framework, credible contemporaneous reporting, and competent geographic evidence. The defense will test those materials for provenance, date, exact location, translation and context, and for contrary indications that Israel characterized the move as temporary, defensive, or limited rather than an offensive intended to establish sovereign-like control.

So the defense theory is straightforward: this case should not be resolved by the administrator’s summary alone. If the admitted source record proves an Israeli military offensive, outside the Golan carveout, with an intent to establish control by the deadline, the proposition may be satisfied. If the record instead proves a limited defensive deployment amid Syria’s governmental collapse, with ambiguous geography or no admitted primary proof tying the location and intent together, the plaintiff has not met even a preponderance standard.

### Arguments

[plaintiff] Council members, the admitted record now answers the defendant's three objections: geography, nature of operation, and intent.

PX-1 gives the rule. The market resolves Yes if Israel commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over any portion of Syria by December 31, 2024. The rule does not require annexation, permanent occupation, a declaration of war, or offensive motive in the sense of seeking regime change. It asks whether Israeli military action was intended to establish control over any Syrian territory. PX-1 also records the market rationale that the Syrian peak of Mount Hermon is not part of the Golan Heights, that Israeli forces reached it on December 8, and that Netanyahu later confirmed a continued presence until a security arrangement.

The newly admitted sources corroborate that rationale. PX-2, an Israel PMO statement from December 8, says Israel gave the army the order to "take over these positions" beyond the border in Syria. It adds the adverse words too: temporary and defensive. Those words matter, but they help define why Israel took control; they do not erase that Israel ordered the army to take over positions.

PX-3 is even more direct. On December 12, the Prime Minister's Office said Netanyahu ordered the IDF to "temporarily take control of the buffer zone in Syria" until an effective force enforces the 1974 Separation of Forces Agreement. That is official Israeli confirmation of the controlling act, within the deadline, in Syria. A temporary control order is still an intent to control territory; the market did not say control had to be permanent.

PX-4 supplies official UN confirmation from UNDOF. Since December 7, UNDOF observed significant IDF movements within the area of separation and along the ceasefire line. As of December 13, UNDOF confirmed IDF forces remained in the area of separation in multiple locations, and UNDOF told Israel those actions violated the 1974 Disengagement Agreement. That proves the action was not merely a vague border posture within Israeli territory. The UN body charged with observing that area treated IDF presence as military activity in the area of separation.

PX-5 proves the intent element. On December 17, Netanyahu was on the summit of Mount Hermon, received an operational briefing, issued directives on continued IDF deployment, and said the assessment was to decide on IDF deployment in that important place until another arrangement was found ensuring Israel's security. That is not a fleeting patrol. It is continued deployment to hold terrain until Israel's condition is met.

PX-6 and PX-7 resolve the geographic dispute by contemporaneous credible reporting. PX-6 reports that on December 8 the IDF took over the Syrian side of the Mount Hermon range to expand the buffer zone. PX-7 reports Netanyahu visited the Syrian side of Mount Hermon on December 17 and that Israel and Israeli troops would remain there until another arrangement guaranteed security. Together with PX-1's statement that the Syrian peak is outside the Golan Heights carveout, the preponderance is straightforward: the relevant territory was Syrian, not Israeli under the market definition.

The defense's best point is that Israel called the move defensive and temporary. Plaintiff does not hide that; PX-2 and PX-3 preserve it. But the proposition does not turn on whether Israel had a defensive reason. It turns on whether Israel commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over any portion of Syria. The words "take over," "take control," "remain," and "continued deployment," combined with UNDOF's confirmation of IDF presence in the area of separation and credible reporting on the Syrian side of Mount Hermon, carry the plaintiff's burden.

This record also satisfies the market's authority clause. There is official confirmation by Israel in PX-2, PX-3, and PX-5; official UN confirmation in PX-4; and corroborating credible reporting in PX-6 and PX-7. Provenance is adequate: the UNDOF source was directly fetched from UN Peacekeeping; the reporting sources were fetched and extracted; the gov.il captures were limited by HTTP 403 to local fetch, but they are identified to official source URLs and submitted with that limitation disclosed.

On a preponderance standard, Israel invaded Syria under this market's definition in December 2024. The council should find the proposition true.

[defendant] Council members, the plaintiff’s new record proves a narrower point than the proposition requires. It proves that Israel placed forces in the Syria buffer-zone context after the Assad collapse. It does not prove, by a preponderance, that Israel commenced a “military offensive intended to establish control” within the market’s meaning.

Start with the text of PX-1. The trigger is not any Israeli military presence in Syria. It is a military offensive with the intent to establish control. That language matters because the same record repeatedly describes a temporary, defensive, security-buffer action.

PX-2 is plaintiff’s own official Israeli source from December 8. It says Israel ordered the army to take over positions, but it immediately explains why and how: to ensure no hostile force embeds itself next to Israel’s border, as “a temporary defensive position until a suitable arrangement is found.” That is not surplusage. It is the official characterization of the act the plaintiff asks you to treat as an invasion.

PX-3 is the plaintiff’s strongest control exhibit, and the defense does not pretend otherwise. It says Netanyahu ordered the IDF to “temporarily take control of the buffer zone in Syria.” But the same sentence says the purpose and endpoint: until an effective force enforces the 1974 Separation of Forces Agreement. In ordinary language, that is temporary security control pending replacement enforcement. The plaintiff’s argument makes “temporary take control” automatically equal “offensive intended to establish control.” If that were the rule, the word “offensive” would add almost nothing.

PX-4, the UNDOF statement, confirms significant IDF movements and that IDF forces remained in the area of separation in multiple locations as of December 13. It also says UNDOF told Israel those actions violated the 1974 agreement. That is important, and it is adverse to the defense on legality and presence. But a disengagement-agreement violation is not the same as the market trigger. PX-4 does not call the action an invasion, does not analyze intent, and does not describe a campaign to establish control over Syria. It is consistent with an unlawful or objectionable buffer-zone deployment, which is not necessarily a “military offensive” under this market.

PX-5, the December 17 Mount Hermon statement, also remains security-framed. Netanyahu received an operational briefing and discussed continued deployment “until another arrangement” ensures Israel’s security. Again, that supports a temporary security posture. It does not show annexation, border change, regime-directed attack, or an intended permanent control arrangement.

The defense’s AP exhibit, D-1, is useful because it captures the contemporaneous dispute without turning either side into a slogan. AP reported that Israel had begun seizing control of a demilitarized buffer zone in Syria, and it also reported the UNDOF violation position. But under the article’s own “Is Israel invading Syria?” section, AP recorded that Israeli political and military leaders stressed the seizure was temporary and not a prelude to entering other parts of Syrian territory. It quoted a military official describing “a temporary step” to keep border stability and allow UN forces to stay, and an analyst saying Israel was not currently trying to change the border or prepare for an invasion into Syria, but conducting a tactical operation in response to the dynamic situation.

That does not erase the plaintiff’s evidence. It shows why the evidence is not one-sided enough to satisfy the exact market proposition. The record now contains official Israeli confirmation of temporary control, official UN confirmation of movements and a treaty violation, and credible reporting that the same action was contemporaneously understood as temporary, tactical, defensive, and not preparation for a broader invasion.

There is also a weight problem with the official Israeli webpages as preserved. PX-2, PX-3, and PX-5 are admitted, but their source descriptions disclose that direct local fetches returned HTTP 403 and that the submitted texts are concise captures of indexed or rendered page text. That does not make them worthless; the URLs and metadata are meaningful. But it does mean the council should be careful about treating snippets as complete context while using them to decide a close interpretive question.

On geography, the plaintiff likely shows some relevant activity in Syrian territory, especially through PX-3, PX-4, PX-6, and PX-7. The defense’s point is not that nothing happened. It is that the proposition asks more than “IDF temporarily entered and held buffer-zone positions in Syria.” It asks for a military offensive intended to establish control. The best admitted evidence of intent says temporary, defensive, security, pending another arrangement, and avoiding hostile forces next to the border.

Under a preponderance standard, words like “take control” matter, but so do the limiting words attached to them. This record supports a finding that Israel violated the disengagement arrangement and temporarily controlled positions in Syria after a security vacuum. It does not more likely than not prove the market’s stronger formulation: a military offensive intended to establish control over Syrian territory. The council should find the proposition not proven.

### Rebuttals

[plaintiff] Council members, the defense's argument turns on a false choice: either Israel's move was temporary and defensive, or it was a military offensive intended to establish control. The record shows both. The market does not say the control must be permanent, annexationist, or unrelated to defense. It says "intended to establish control over any portion of Syria."

The defense's own AP exhibit, PX-R1, confirms the controlling facts. AP says the Israeli military "began seizing control" of a demilitarized buffer zone in Syria. It reports Netanyahu saying Israeli forces were "moving to control" a roughly 400-square-kilometer demilitarized buffer zone in Syrian territory. It also reports Foreign Minister Saar saying the Israeli military took "targeted and temporary control" of certain areas near the border. Those are not plaintiff's embellishments; they are the defendant's admitted credible report and official-characterization evidence.

The word "temporary" does not save the defense. A temporary control order is still an intent to control for the time it lasts. The rule covers "any portion" of Syria; it does not require a long-term strategy, a border change, or an advance into every other part of Syria. Indeed, PX-R1's analyst statement that Israel was not trying to change the border answers a requirement the market never imposed.

Nor does the word "defensive" negate "offensive" under this market. The record describes military forces moving into, taking over, seizing, and controlling positions in Syrian territory and in an area where UNDOF says there should be no military forces or activities. PX-4 says UNDOF confirmed IDF remained in multiple locations in the area of separation and told Israel that these actions violated the 1974 agreement. That is an active military advance into controlled terrain, not merely watchful border posture.

The defense also asks the council to discount the gov.il captures because local fetch returned 403. That limitation was disclosed by plaintiff. But the defense itself relies on those same exhibits as D-3, D-4, and D-6 for the temporary/defensive wording. The fair approach is to weigh them for the words both sides use: they say temporary and defensive, but they also say take over, take control, continued deployment, and remain until an arrangement is found.

So the defendant's best evidence narrows, but does not defeat, the proposition. It shows the operation was limited, security-driven, and temporary. The proposition does not exclude limited, security-driven, temporary control. On the admitted record, Israel intentionally took and held Syrian territory in December 2024. That is enough under the market's definition.

### Surrebuttals

[defendant] Council members, the plaintiff is right about one limited point: temporary control can still be control. The defense is not asking for a rule that “temporary” or “defensive” automatically defeats the proposition.

But the plaintiff’s rebuttal still collapses the market language. The trigger is not merely “Israel intentionally controlled any portion of Syria.” It is that Israel commenced a military offensive intended to establish control. Those words require more than proof of a security deployment that took temporary control in a vacuum created by Syria’s collapse.

That is why the AP exhibit matters even though it contains control language. AP did report “seizing control,” “moving to control,” and “targeted and temporary control.” But AP also framed the live question as “Is Israel invading Syria?” and reported the contemporaneous answer from Israeli political and military leaders: the seizure was temporary, not a prelude to entering other parts of Syrian territory, not an effort to change the border, and, in the quoted analyst’s words, a tactical operation rather than a long-term strategy. That evidence goes directly to the characterization that the market chose: invasion by military offensive, not merely any unlawful or temporary buffer-zone control.

The UNDOF exhibit likewise proves an important but narrower fact. It supports that IDF forces were in the area of separation and that UNDOF viewed the actions as violating the 1974 agreement. A ceasefire-agreement violation is serious. It is not automatically the same as a market-defined military offensive intended to establish control over Syria. The plaintiff’s rebuttal treats “military activity where it should not be” as equivalent to “offensive”; the rule uses a stronger and more specific phrase.

Nor should the gov.il captures be split in half. If the council credits their “take over,” “take control,” and “continued deployment” words, it should also credit the limiting words in the same captured statements: “temporary,” “defensive,” “until a suitable arrangement is found,” “until there is an effective force,” and “ensures Israel’s security.” Those are not after-the-fact lawyer glosses. They are the official context of the conduct.

So the remaining dispute is not whether Israel crossed into Syrian territory or exercised some temporary control. The record largely supports that. The question is whether the plaintiff has shown the additional market trigger: a military offensive intended to establish control. On this mixed record, with the best sources repeatedly describing a temporary tactical security measure rather than an invasion or border-changing operation, the answer should remain No.

### Closings

[plaintiff] Council members, the question is not whether Israel annexed Syrian territory, permanently changed a border, or marched toward Damascus. The market asks whether Israel commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over any portion of Syria by December 31, 2024. On this record, the answer is Yes.

Start with the admitted rule and rationale in PX-1. The Golan Heights count as Israeli territory, but the Syrian peak/side of Mount Hermon does not. The case therefore turns on whether Israel moved into and intended to control Syrian territory. The remaining exhibits answer that directly.

Israel's own statements supply the core admissions. PX-2 says Israel gave the army the order to take over positions beyond the border in Syria. PX-3 says Netanyahu ordered the IDF to temporarily take control of the buffer zone in Syria until an effective force enforces the 1974 agreement. PX-5 says Netanyahu, on Mount Hermon, received an operational briefing, issued directives on continued IDF deployment, and addressed deployment in that important place until another security arrangement is found. The defense emphasizes the words temporary and defensive. Those words are in the record, but so are take over, take control, continued deployment, and remain until an arrangement. The latter words establish control; the former words explain duration and motive.

The UN evidence removes any suggestion that this was merely an internal Israeli border posture. PX-4 says UNDOF observed significant IDF movements within the area of separation and along the ceasefire line, confirmed IDF remained in multiple locations as of December 13, and told Israel those actions violated the 1974 Disengagement Agreement. A violation of that agreement is not automatically the market trigger, but here it confirms that Israeli military forces moved into and remained in a place where they were not supposed to be.

The location evidence is sufficient on a preponderance standard. PX-6 reports that the IDF took over the Syrian side of the Mount Hermon range on December 8. PX-7 reports Netanyahu visited the Syrian side of Mount Hermon on December 17 and that Israeli troops would remain there until a security arrangement. PX-1 independently records that the Syrian peak is outside the Golan Heights carveout. The defense does not seriously prove an alternate location; it argues characterization.

Even the defense's AP exhibit supports plaintiff on the decisive words. PX-R1 says the Israeli military began seizing control of a demilitarized buffer zone in Syria. It says Netanyahu described forces moving to control a roughly 400-square-kilometer buffer zone in Syrian territory. It says Foreign Minister Saar described targeted and temporary control of areas near the border. That exhibit also contains the defense's best point: officials and analysts called the move temporary, tactical, and security-driven, not a prelude to entering other parts of Syria. But the market does not require a prelude to a broader invasion. It requires control over any portion of Syria.

The source limits have been disclosed. The gov.il captures were not full local HTML captures because local fetch returned 403. But the defense relies on those same captures for the temporary/defensive wording, and the central control facts are independently corroborated by UNDOF, AP, Jerusalem Post, Ynet, and the case packet. The record is not perfect, but preponderance does not require perfection.

The best contrary inference is that Israel acted defensively during a security vacuum. Accept that. It still intentionally moved military forces into Syrian territory, took or seized control, held positions, and conditioned withdrawal or future posture on a satisfactory security arrangement. Temporary defensive control is still intended control. The proposition is therefore more likely true than false under the market's own definition. The council should find that Israel invaded Syria in 2024.

[defendant] Council members, the defense closing is narrow because the record is narrow.

The plaintiff has likely proved that Israeli forces temporarily controlled positions in Syrian territory, including the buffer-zone context, after the Assad government’s collapse. The plaintiff has also proved that UNDOF viewed IDF activity in the area of separation as a violation of the 1974 agreement. Those are significant facts. They are not, by themselves, the proposition.

The proposition asks whether Israel commenced a military offensive intended to establish control over any portion of Syria. If the market had meant any cross-border military presence, any treaty violation, or any temporary security control, it could have said so. It chose the stronger phrase “military offensive intended to establish control.” The council should give that phrase real work.

The best evidence of intent is the official Israeli wording the plaintiff relies on. PX-2 says “take over,” but also says “temporary defensive position until a suitable arrangement is found.” PX-3 says “temporarily take control of the buffer zone in Syria,” but gives the endpoint: until an effective force enforces the 1974 Separation of Forces Agreement. PX-5 says continued deployment at Mount Hermon until another arrangement ensures Israel’s security. Read as a whole, those statements describe a temporary security deployment keyed to restoring or replacing a security arrangement, not an offensive to establish control in the ordinary invasion sense.

The UNDOF evidence does not close that gap. PX-4 confirms IDF movements, presence in the area of separation, and a violation of the disengagement agreement. But UNDOF did not determine the market question. A ceasefire or disengagement violation can be unlawful and still not be a “military offensive intended to establish control” as opposed to a temporary buffer-zone deployment.

The AP evidence is the best contemporaneous framing because it contains both sides. It reports seizure and control language, so the defense is not hiding the hard facts. But it also reports that Israeli leaders said the move was temporary and not a prelude to entering other Syrian territory; it records an official describing a temporary step to keep stability and allow UN forces to stay; and it quotes an analyst saying Israel was not trying to change the border or prepare for an invasion, but was conducting a tactical operation in response to Syria’s dynamic situation. That is exactly the distinction the defense has pressed from the start.

The plaintiff’s final position effectively reads “offensive” out of the rule. Under that reading, any temporary military control of any Syrian position, even one expressly framed as defensive and pending enforcement of the existing separation agreement, resolves Yes. But the market did not ask only whether Israel temporarily controlled territory. It asked whether Israel invaded Syria by commencing a military offensive intended to establish control.

On this record, the most accurate finding is mixed: Israel temporarily entered and controlled Syrian-side buffer-zone positions; UNDOF objected; Israeli officials conditioned the deployment on security arrangements and repeatedly framed it as temporary and defensive; credible contemporaneous reporting recorded that it was not a broader invasion, not a border-change operation, and not a long-term strategy.

A mixed record can satisfy a preponderance standard only if the decisive inference is more likely than not. Here the decisive inference is not mere control; it is offensive intent to establish control. The plaintiff has not carried that extra burden. The council should find the proposition not proven.
